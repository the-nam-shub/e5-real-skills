# Episode #345: Multiple Guests on AI Creative Tools in B2B Marketing

This episode gathered practitioners from ElevenLabs, Brain Labs, Bitly, and UiPath to share working AI creative workflows. It broke meaningful new ground on implementation specifics, particularly around Claude-based design systems and AI video production pipelines, though the underlying argument (AI enables rather than replaces creative teams) is not new.

## New practices extracted

- **Claude brand skill with Notion-backed governance (Liz, Brain Labs).** Build a Claude Enterprise skill that encodes your full brand guide as structured instructions, then store those instructions as markdown files in Notion so updates flow automatically into the skill via integration. Pair this with a formal change request process where a design team reviews and approves proposed skill modifications rather than letting users edit directly. This separates one-off requests from systemic improvements and prevents skill drift at scale.

- **Claude plus Figma MCP for infographic generation.** Install the Figma MCP (Model Context Protocol) as an app within Claude, then use your brand skill to generate infographics from raw data input. The output pushes directly to Figma for download. The integration is currently one-directional, so all design decisions should be finalized in Claude before pushing.

- **Claude plus Notion integration for task-triggered design execution.** Drop a Notion task link into Claude chat, instruct it to complete the task, and the skill runs the full design workflow and opens the output in Google Slides with brand guidelines applied. This removes manual handoffs between task management and design execution.

- **AI video storyboard workflow before generation (Vicente, Bitly).** Start with a Claude-drafted script, then generate keyframe images using an image model (Nano Banana was cited) as a storyboard. Feed those images as references into a video generation tool. Video tools produce more consistent results when given image references rather than text prompts alone. This workflow reduced a customer education video to a few hundred dollars and two weeks of production time.

- **Voice cloning versus dubbing by language pair (Carter, UiPath).** When localizing video content, test both approaches rather than defaulting to one. Voice cloning performs better for romance-to-romance language pairs; dubbing with native voices performs better when the source and target languages are linguistically distant (the example given was English to Asian languages). Document which approach works per language combination.

- **AI-generated B-roll for branded environments.** Use image and video generation tools to create specific B-roll shots that do not exist in stock libraries, including environments with your own branding applied. Carter described generating a branded theater for a live event LED volume backdrop as a concrete use case.

- **AI Creative Producer as a defined hiring role (Luke, ElevenLabs).** Create a role explicitly scoped to producing full videos using AI tools, from scripting through final output. Evaluate candidates with a take-home challenge requiring them to produce a complete video in 30 to 60 minutes. This role requires creative direction instincts and cinematography understanding, not just technical tool proficiency.

- **Descript plus Claude plus VidIQ for YouTube SEO (Carter, UiPath).** Upload a finished video to Descript, run custom Claude prompts to generate SEO tags and descriptions, then validate those tags using the VidIQ plugin on YouTube. This replaces a multi-team SEO workflow with a single-person process.

- **Avoid AI for talking-head footage.** Both Luke (ElevenLabs) and Vicente (Bitly) independently confirmed they do not use AI video generation for A-roll footage of people speaking to camera. The quality is not yet sufficient for close-up facial expressions and lip sync. Use voiceover narration with AI-generated visuals instead.

- **Claude as research assistant for content synthesis (Dave Gerhardt).** Rather than prompting AI to generate content from scratch, gather source materials (transcripts, articles, notes), spend 30 minutes building a detailed brief, and use Claude to synthesize and organize. The subject matter expert controls narrative and voice; Claude handles research aggregation.

## Where this creates tension

This episode reinforces the existing disagreement on whether AI primarily replaces or enables marketing work (disagreement ID: ai-replaces-vs-enables-marketers, currently 1 vs 1).

Liz joins the enabling side, arguing that the Claude brand skill system was built to free the design team for higher-value work, not to reduce headcount. Dave Gerhardt's framing of AI as a "producer" or research assistant rather than a content generator sits in the same camp. Neither guest addressed the displacement scenario directly; both assumed creative professionals remain central and AI expands their output capacity.

Drew Pinta (episode #346) holds the opposing position, arguing that approximately 80% of current marketing work will be automated away and that marketers must proactively reinvent their roles. The support count remains 1 vs 1, but the enabling-side argument in this episode is grounded in specific implemented systems rather than general framing, which gives it more evidentiary weight than the current count reflects.
