# Homepage & Website Strategy

## Overview
This skill covers B2B homepage and product page strategy: how to structure messaging, choose visuals, design CTAs, handle multi-persona audiences, build conversion architecture, and optimize for both humans and search engines. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 19 episodes. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved.

---

## Foundational Principles

**Treat your website as your primary digital storefront.** Website experience is a company-level strategic decision, not just a marketing tactic. Decisions about pricing visibility, demo accessibility, and ease of booking should be made with the same rigor as physical retail store design — they directly impact whether people buy from you. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #178)

**Assign a single owner with cross-functional buy-in.** Designate one person (typically the head of marketing) as the directly responsible individual (DRI) who owns the final decision on website copy and design. Gather feedback from cross-functional stakeholders in a structured session, present 2–3 options back to leadership, allow voting if desired, but make the final call with the CEO and the DRI only. This prevents design-by-committee from diluting messaging. Document the decision and move forward without reopening feedback loops. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #141)

**Fix broken or outdated pages immediately.** When you identify obvious problems — broken pages, outdated copy, missing CTAs — fix them without waiting for formal prioritization. Share these fixes in team updates to show momentum. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #133)

**Understand who is actually visiting your homepage.** Homepage traffic typically comes from organic search, direct traffic, or PR/partnerships — meaning visitors have unclear or low intent and broad knowledge levels. The homepage must explain basics clearly rather than assume prior knowledge. Save specific, persona-targeted messaging for dedicated landing pages. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## Homepage Messaging & Hero Section

**Note: There is a genuine disagreement about whether the hero should lead with specific capability/audience or with customer problems and emotional drivers. See "Where Experts Disagree" before writing hero copy.**

**Ensure the first fold contains a clear, user-centric value proposition in one bold sentence.** The first fold is your only guaranteed impression before users bounce. Make it count with a single, bold statement that clearly communicates what you do for the user — not what you are. Avoid feature placement or company-centric language. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Structure homepage messaging with category definition, value proposition, and differentiation.** Lead with category definition (what is it?) so visitors immediately understand what you do. Follow with value proposition that communicates your "onliness" — why you, not competitors. Then provide proof and differentiation. This framework answers the questions buyers ask: What is it? Is it for me? Why you? (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Evaluate your hero using the "rule of four."** The headline and subheading combination should clearly communicate: (1) what the product/service does, (2) who it's for, (3) why the user should care (the benefit or outcome), and (4) trust indicators. If any of these are missing or unclear, the messaging is incomplete. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Establish a clear, core company message before attempting persona-specific tailoring.** If you cannot articulate a single, clear value proposition without diving into persona-specific details, you have a positioning problem that needs to be solved first. Develop a core message that explains what the company does at a fundamental level, then layer persona-specific messaging on top through navigation, landing pages, or secondary sections. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Avoid vague, generic messaging that tries to satisfy all stakeholders.** When internal stakeholders have conflicting priorities, the temptation is to write copy that makes everyone happy. This results in messaging that says nothing specific. Be specific about what you do, who you're for, and what problem you solve. Specificity wins over generic language that attempts to appeal to everyone. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Edit homepage copy ruthlessly and use whitespace to support messaging hierarchy.** More copy is not inherently bad, but it must be edited for clarity and necessity. Prioritize which key messages are essential and remove redundancy. Use whitespace and design to break up copy and guide the reader's eye to the most important elements. Supporting visuals should enhance, not duplicate, the copy. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Ensure homepage messaging aligns with the campaigns and channels that drive traffic to it.** If a marketing campaign has a specific look, feel, or message that brings users to the homepage, the homepage experience should match or build on that tone and promise. Misalignment between campaign messaging and homepage messaging causes bounce. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## Homepage Structure & Navigation

**Note: There is a genuine disagreement about whether the homepage should be optimized primarily for conversion or function as a navigation roadmap. See "Where Experts Disagree" before deciding on your organizing principle.**

**Recognize that your homepage cannot serve all audiences and products equally.** When you have multiple products or serve multiple buyer personas, the homepage's job is not to explain everything — it's to funnel people to the right destination quickly. Find either a core problem you solve across all products or a through-line value proposition, then use the homepage to route people efficiently to product-specific or persona-specific pages. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Identify the shared pain point that overlaps across multiple personas to anchor homepage messaging.** Rather than trying to speak to each persona separately on the homepage, find the common problem or friction point that all personas experience. Build the homepage value proposition around this overlap, then use navigation and landing pages to direct different personas to more specific information. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Use dedicated landing pages for specific personas rather than trying to serve all audiences on the homepage.** The homepage should serve as a general entry point with a clear, unified value proposition. For specific personas or use cases, create separate landing pages with tailored messaging. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Structure website navigation with a dedicated Solutions section segmented by persona or use case.** Each solution page should contain messaging, copy, and value propositions tailored to that specific persona (e.g., Freelancer, Startup, Enterprise, Agency), allowing visitors to self-select into relevant messaging without requiring complex personalization logic. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #173)

**When the homepage cannot serve multiple audiences well, create a separate product offering or homepage for the underserved segment.** If your company serves audiences with significantly different needs, budgets, or use cases, consider creating a separate offering with its own homepage and messaging (e.g., Fiverr Pro). This allows you to maintain clarity on the main homepage while serving the specific needs of the secondary audience. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Maintain a unified brand message on your main website, then create persona-specific pages that branch off from the homepage.** This structure preserves brand coherence while enabling persona-specific messaging (e.g., "Webflow for Freelancers," "Webflow for Agencies"). (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #155)

**Include trust indicators prominently on the homepage to reduce purchase friction.** Trust indicators like customer testimonials, number of users/clients served, money-back guarantees, or certifications should be visible and relevant to the primary value proposition. Place them where they support the main messaging rather than as an afterthought. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Use specific customer outcomes as social proof instead of generic logo walls.** Customer logos alone don't build trust — their absence creates doubt, but their presence is table stakes. Instead, feature specific outcomes: name the customer, quantify the result (e.g., "30% faster adoption"), and make it prominent on the homepage. Specific, quantified results resonate far more than logo walls. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Move quantitative metrics and proof points higher on the page.** If you have quantitative proof points (e.g., "40% faster delivery," "3x ROI"), place them above the fold or in the first scroll section. This helps early-stage prospects quickly validate that your solution delivers measurable results. (Source: Scott Cappuzzo, Episode #251)

**Track bounce rate, scroll depth, time on page, and navigation patterns alongside conversion metrics.** Monitor bounce rate as an indicator of message-channel mismatch, scroll depth and time spent to understand user behavior flow, and navigation patterns to identify what content is actually resonating. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**A/B test the homepage as a complete composition rather than individual components in isolation.** Test fold-by-fold or in small grouped sections rather than optimizing each component separately. Testing every component independently can result in a page where each element converts well individually but the overall experience becomes incoherent. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Combine user interviews with surveys to gather direct feedback on homepage messaging and design.** Talk to both random first-time visitors and heavy/returning users separately, as they have different needs. Use surveys to get direct input from your audience, and don't be shy about giving up real estate temporarily to collect insights. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## CTA Strategy & Conversion Architecture

**Note: There is a genuine disagreement about whether your primary website CTA should push visitors toward a demo or toward middle-of-funnel tools first. See "Where Experts Disagree" before designing your conversion architecture.**

**Build always-on conversion points that capture demand without gating everything.** Create multiple, always-on conversion opportunities that don't require a demo request: personalized event banners, product tours (ungated to reduce friction), blog subscription options, calls to follow subject matter experts on LinkedIn, and on-demand content. These conversion points should be relevant to the content or use case the visitor is viewing. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #206)

**Ensure CTA wording matches the prospect's actual next step, not just your ideal next step.** Choose CTA text that reflects what the prospect actually expects to happen. For example, "Get started today" should lead to an immediate product experience, not a sales call booking form. Mismatched CTAs create friction and reduce conversions. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Avoid placing redundant CTAs in the main navigation that duplicate menu items.** If "Pricing" is already in your main navigation menu, don't also place a "View Pricing" CTA in the header. Instead, use header CTAs for your primary conversion goal (e.g., "Book Demo" or "Start Free Trial"). This reduces cognitive load and focuses attention on your main conversion objective. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #251)

**Make CTA outcomes explicit in product section links; avoid vague labels.** When linking to product pages or features from the homepage, use action-oriented language that clarifies what the visitor will see or do (e.g., "Explore Conversion Intelligence" or "See How Conversion Intelligence Works") rather than just the product name. (Source: Scott Cappuzzo, Episode #251)

**Place CTAs both at the top and bottom of the page.** Ensure visitors have a clear conversion path regardless of where they are in their scroll. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## Product Pages

**Clarify whether your product page is a homepage for the product or a deep-dive feature page, then structure accordingly.** Homepage-style pages should emphasize positioning and key value props; deep-dive pages should focus on feature discovery, integrations, and use-case details. Testimonials are more important on homepage-style pages; less critical on deep-dive pages. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Lead with a clear, specific capability in the hero section, not a vague outcome.** The hero section should clearly state what the product does (a specific capability) rather than a generic outcome. Include context about who it's for to help visitors self-identify. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

**Prominently feature your key differentiator throughout the product page, not just once.** If your product has a clear differentiator, make it a central theme throughout the page rather than mentioning it only once or burying it. Repeat and reinforce the differentiator in multiple sections so prospects understand why your product is different. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Use clear, specific headers that help skimmers quickly find the information they need.** Most visitors will skim product pages rather than read every word. Use headers that are specific and informative (e.g., "Detect outages quickly" instead of "Advanced monitoring"). Headers should help prospects quickly locate the argument or feature they're looking for. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Include both a "Take a tour" (demo) and "Book a demo" CTA on product pages.** Provide two distinct CTAs: one for prospects who want to self-explore the product and one for those ready to talk to sales. Do not force all visitors down a single path. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

**Include customer testimonials with clear attribution and keep them brief.** Always include the customer's name, title, and company so prospects can assess credibility. Keep testimonials concise. Video testimonials should be under 30 seconds. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Show your product in multiple formats across the website.** Don't hide your product demo behind a single CTA. Showcase it in multiple ways: interactive demos on product pages, video walkthroughs, GIFs showing key features, and customer testimonials using the product. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

**Use progressive disclosure to balance comprehensive information with scannability.** Structure website content to show high-level information first, then allow visitors to drill deeper if interested. For example, start with a 5–8 step overview in an interactive demo, then offer the option to explore specific features in more detail. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

**Focus product pages on showing your product, not on worrying about competitors seeing it.** Competitors can easily access your product through other means. Instead, focus on clearly showing your product and highlighting your differentiation. Transparency about your product builds trust with prospects and helps them self-educate. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Add TL;DR, FAQs, and comparison content to product pages for AEO optimization.** Structure product and service pages with: (1) a TL;DR or key takeaways section at the top, (2) 3–10 FAQs that mirror real buyer questions sourced from sales calls or feedback loops, and (3) comparison pages (e.g., "us vs. competitor") that highlight differentiation. Use accordion-style formatting for FAQs to improve readability. These elements help both humans and LLMs quickly understand your offering. (Source: Clare Schmitt, Episode #324)

## Product Page Visuals & Design

**Note: There is a genuine disagreement about whether to use real product screenshots or people/contextual visuals as hero images. See "Where Experts Disagree" before making this decision.**

**Note: There is a genuine disagreement about how long product pages should be. See "Where Experts Disagree" before setting length targets.**

**Establish a visual hierarchy for product page assets: GIFs first, then screenshots, then abstract visuals, never stock photography.** Product GIFs (animated 2–3 second clips showing specific features) are highest priority, followed by product screenshots, then abstract visuals. Stock images waste valuable product page real estate that should educate prospects about the actual product. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

**Use actual product UI screenshots that show differentiation, not generic screenshots that could apply to any competitor.** When including product screenshots, ensure they show something unique or differentiated about your product. Zoom in on the specific features or UI elements that set you apart. (Source: Dan, Episode #222)

**Mix up visual design and color contrast at every page fold to maintain engagement.** Introduce different color contrasts, layouts, and visual treatments at each section to keep visitors engaged and prevent the page from feeling monotonous. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

**Keep product page videos under 1 minute, preferably under 30 seconds.** Longer videos (2+ minutes) significantly reduce engagement and watch-through rates. This applies to both product videos and customer testimonial videos. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

**Ensure visual elements support and clarify copy rather than duplicate or distract from it.** Avoid repeating the same visual twice. GIFs and animations should add value; if they don't clarify or enhance understanding, remove them. Design should bring copy to life, not overwhelm it. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## Personalization

**Personalize website experience by industry, use case, or company size rather than individual-level data.** Use safe, non-creepy personalization dimensions like industry, use case, or company size (SMB vs. enterprise) to tailor messaging and navigation. Avoid reverse IP lookups or other individually identifying tactics that feel invasive. (Source: Guy Yalif, Episode #251)

**Use a quiz or upfront questions to gather personalization data transparently.** Ask visitors upfront questions about their role, company size, or use case via a quiz or form. This approach is transparent, avoids the "creepy" feeling of invisible tracking, and actually increases visitor willingness to provide information because they see the value in getting a personalized experience in return. (Source: Scott Cappuzzo, Episode #251)

**Implement website personalization to shift messaging for returning customers.** Use website personalization technology to detect returning customers and shift homepage or key page messaging based on their prior behavior. For example, if a returning visitor previously viewed the freelancer solution page, personalize the homepage to emphasize freelancer-relevant messaging. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

## Interactive Tools & Mid-Funnel Engagement

**Build interactive ROI calculators using no-code AI tools.** Use tools like Lovable to create interactive ROI calculators that sales teams can run live on discovery calls. Write a detailed prompt describing the calculator logic, inputs, and outputs (e.g., cost of current state, efficiency gains, annual savings). Include industry benchmarks and helpful text to guide users who may not know baseline metrics. Structure as a multi-step form that generates a downloadable PDF with stakeholder-ready results. Deploy on websites, at events, and share directly with sales teams. (Source: Jessica Lytle, Episode #278)

**Embed an ROI calculator on the homepage to bridge the mid-funnel gap.** Create an interactive ROI calculator that appears early on your website (ideally above the fold or easily discoverable) to help prospects understand the financial impact of your product. Use sliders to show ranges so prospects can see outcomes for different company sizes. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

**Build free tools and templates to meet buyers earlier in their journey.** Create free, downloadable tools (calculators, templates, job description builders) that solve a specific problem your target audience faces, without requiring a product demo or sales conversation. Use these tools to capture intent and educate buyers about their own needs before introducing your product. (Source: John Short, Episode #148)

## Chatbots & Live Chat

**Evaluate live chat and chatbot tools based on strategic fit and resource availability.** Before implementing live chat or chatbot tools, assess whether they align with your business strategy and whether you have the resources to manage them. If your site has low conversion rates and low inbound volume, adding chat will likely just create another task for your team without meaningful impact. Consider AI-powered solutions that can handle FAQs and basic navigation without requiring human staffing, but only if the content fed into the AI is high-quality. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #143)

**Configure chatbots to appear only when idle and without sound.** If using a chatbot, set it to appear only after the user's mouse has been idle for a period of time, and disable auto-play sound. Avoid chatbots that pop up immediately, make noise, or persist across pages after being dismissed. (Source: Guy Yalif, Episode #251)

## SEO & Technical Optimization

**Treat the footer as a strategic SEO asset.** Since the footer appears on every page, link to high-value landing pages and solution pages using language that matches search intent and customer queries. Use specific phrases like "automated payment processing," "CRM for property managers," or "operations management software" rather than generic links. This increases the surface area for ranking on valuable keywords. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #224)

**Use review aggregation widgets to consolidate multi-platform reviews on your website.** Deploy a review aggregation widget that pulls reviews from multiple platforms (G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, Google Reviews, etc.) into a single display. This improves SEO performance — search engines favor sites with aggregated review signals — while giving visitors a consolidated view of social proof. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #143)

**Use autoplay for homepage videos rather than requiring a click to start playback.** If including video on the homepage, set it to autoplay so visitors see it without taking an extra action. Only use video if it's compelling enough to hold attention once it starts playing. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should your primary website CTA push visitors toward a demo, or toward middle-of-funnel tools and content first?

**Support summary: 5 vs 1**

**Position A — Build mid-funnel tools as primary CTAs before pushing to demo:**
Rather than driving all traffic to a demo request, build valuable middle-of-funnel assets (interactive tools, calculators, assessments, on-demand content) as primary CTAs. These build trust and create multiple conversion opportunities before asking for a demo, which has low conversion rates from cold traffic.

Supporters:
- **Dave Gerhardt** (Episodes #206 and #271): Recommended building middle-of-funnel assets as primary CTAs instead of pushing directly to demo, arguing this approach is more effective than pushing prospects from awareness to demo with low conversion rates. Also recommended replacing traditional gated content with interactive tools (website graders, A/B test previews, product trial tools) that provide immediate value without requiring a demo request upfront.
- **Kyle Coleman, CMO at Copy.ai** (Episode #206): Recommended building always-on conversion points (product tours, blog subscriptions, LinkedIn follows, on-demand content) that capture demand without forcing every visitor into a demo funnel.
- **Natalie Marcotullio, Head of Growth at Navattic** (Episode #178): Recommended embedding an ROI calculator above the fold to bridge the mid-funnel gap, giving prospects a reason to engage without immediately booking a demo.
- **John Short, CEO of Compound Growth Marketing** (Episode #148): Recommended building free tools and templates that solve specific problems for target audiences without requiring a demo, using them to capture intent and educate buyers before introducing the product.

**Position B — Keep demo booking as the primary conversion goal, but segment CTAs by intent level:**
Keep demo booking as the primary conversion goal but segment CTAs by buyer intent level (e.g., "Quick Learning Call," "Full Product Demo," "Start Buying") to reduce friction and match the CTA to actual intent rather than eliminating the demo CTA.

Supporters:
- **Natalie Marcotullio, Head of Growth at Navattic** (Episode #178): Recommended creating multiple demo booking pathways segmented by intent level rather than replacing the demo CTA with mid-funnel tools. This reduces friction while keeping demo booking as the conversion goal. *(Note: Marcotullio appears on both sides — she supports mid-funnel tools AND segmented demo CTAs, suggesting these may be complementary rather than mutually exclusive.)*

**Context dependency:** The mid-funnel tools approach may be more appropriate for high-ACV enterprise products where buyers need more education before a demo, while segmented demo CTAs may work better for PLG or mid-market products. However, the core tension — whether to de-emphasize the demo CTA in favor of earlier-stage conversion points — is a genuine strategic disagreement applicable across most B2B contexts.

**Trend note:** More recent episodes (2024–2025) cluster strongly around the mid-funnel tools position, suggesting a shift away from demo-first website strategy.

### 2. Should your homepage hero lead with a specific capability/audience, or with the customer's problem and emotional outcome?

**Support summary: 4 vs 2**

**Position A — Lead with specific capability and audience:**
The hero section should clearly state what the product does (a specific capability) and who it's for, rather than a vague outcome. Audience-specific headlines that name the target customer allow visitors to instantly self-qualify.

Supporters:
- **Robert Kaminski, product marketing consultant** (Episode #222): Recommended leading with a clear, specific capability in the hero (e.g., "Advanced monitoring and reporting for Microsoft 365") rather than a generic outcome like "Boost engagement." Argued vague value propositions could apply to any product.
- **Dave Gerhardt** (Episode #178): Recommended writing homepage headlines that explicitly name the target audience (e.g., "For HubSpot Super Admins") to immediately qualify visitors. Argued this "plants the flag" and is more effective than generic or clever headlines.
- **Peep Laja, CEO of Wynter** (Episode #119): Recommended structuring the homepage with category definition first (what is it?), then value proposition, then differentiation. Argued specificity wins over generic language.
- **Guy Yalif, CEO of Intellimize** (Episode #251): Argued that generic headlines like "Unlock Insights, Drive Revenue" fail to communicate what the product does or who it's for, and that strong hero headlines should clearly state what the product does.

**Position B — Lead with customer problem and emotional drivers:**
The hero section should communicate the specific pain the customer is experiencing and the result they'll get — not features, technology, or product details. Leading with customer problems and emotional drivers differentiates you from competitors who all lead with features.

Supporters:
- **Talia Wolf, Founder of GetUplift** (Episodes #132 and #251): Argued the hero has three seconds to communicate the specific pain solved and the result the visitor will get — not features or technology. The hero should create an "aha moment" by focusing on the customer's problem. Also argued that B2B homepages that focus on features cause all competitors to look the same; instead, lead with what the customer is struggling with, what they've tried that hasn't worked, and why your solution matters emotionally.

**Context dependency:** This is a genuine disagreement regardless of context, though it may be partially reconcilable — Peep Laja's framework includes both category definition AND value proposition, which could incorporate emotional drivers. However, the core tension between leading with "what it does and who it's for" vs. "what problem it solves emotionally" is a real strategic choice that affects headline writing and hero structure.

### 3. Should your homepage be optimized primarily for conversion, or function as a navigation roadmap for multiple audiences?

**Support summary: 2 vs 2**

**Position A — Homepage as conversion asset:**
Treat the homepage as a critical marketing asset that must be optimized for conversion. It should have a clear, user-centric value proposition in the first fold and be designed to drive visitors toward a primary conversion goal.

Supporters:
- **Maura Rivera** (Episode #301): Described the website as a foundational marketing pillar that must be beautiful, simple, clearly communicate its message, and be optimized for conversion.
- **Lee Reshef, VP Marketing at Fiverr** (Episode #220): Recommended placing CTAs top and bottom, introducing the primary value proposition in the first fold, and focusing on what's in it for the user. Explicitly said to treat the homepage as a balanced experience with conversion-focused sections.

**Position B — Homepage as navigation roadmap:**
For companies with multiple personas or use cases, the homepage should function as a roadmap or jumping board rather than a pure conversion page. After the hero, use the homepage to direct different visitor segments to the pages they need, rather than forcing everyone through the same conversion path.

Supporters:
- **Talia Wolf, Founder of GetUplift** (Episode #132): Explicitly recommended treating the homepage as a navigation roadmap for multi-persona audiences, using content sections to segment audiences by intent and stage of awareness rather than pushing everyone toward a single conversion.
- **Diane Wiredu, Founder of Lion Words** (Episode #300): Argued that when you have multiple products or personas, the homepage's job is not to explain everything but to funnel people to the right destination quickly, routing them efficiently to product-specific or persona-specific pages.

**Context dependency:** The navigation roadmap approach is explicitly recommended for multi-product or multi-persona companies, while the conversion-focused approach may be more appropriate for single-product companies with a clear ICP. However, both Talia Wolf and Lee Reshef are speaking about general homepage best practices, and their advice genuinely conflicts on whether conversion or navigation should be the primary organizing principle.

### 5. How long should a product page be?

**Support summary: 2 vs 2**

**Position A — Keep pages short: 3–4 scrolls maximum:**
Limit product pages to approximately 3–4 scrolls maximum. Longer pages overwhelm visitors and reduce engagement. Apply the 80/20 principle and surface only the top features and key information.

Supporters:
- **Madhav Bhandari** (Episode #222): Explicitly recommended keeping product pages to 3–4 scrolls maximum, arguing that longer pages (7–8+ scrolls) overwhelm visitors and reduce engagement.
- **Natalie Marcotullio** (Episode #178): Recommended using progressive disclosure to structure website content — starting with a 5–8 step overview then offering the option to explore specific features in more detail — rather than imposing a strict scroll limit. *(Note: Marcotullio's progressive disclosure approach is compatible with shorter pages but does not prescribe the same strict limit.)*

**Position B — Avoid excessive copy, but no strict length limit:**
Avoid excessive copy and prioritize clarity and scanability, but the goal is conciseness and digestibility rather than a strict scroll limit. Use headers, bullet points, and visual breaks to make content scannable.

Supporters:
- **Robert Kaminski** (Episode #222): Recommended avoiding excessive text and prioritizing concise, scannable copy, but framed this as a quality/clarity issue rather than prescribing a specific scroll limit.
- **Diane Wiredu, Founder of Lion Words** (Episode #220): Argued that more copy is not inherently bad but must be edited for clarity and necessity, using whitespace and design to guide the reader. Did not prescribe a length limit.

**Context dependency:** Madhav Bhandari's 3–4 scroll recommendation may apply more to feature-specific product pages, while Robert Kaminski distinguishes between homepage-style product pages and deep-dive feature pages (which might warrant more length). However, the specific scroll-count prescription is a genuine point of disagreement.

## What NOT To Do

**Don't use vague, generic hero headlines** that could apply to any product (e.g., "Unlock Insights, Drive Revenue," "Streamline Your Deliveries"). These fail to communicate what the product actually does or who it's for. (Source: Guy Yalif, Episode #251)

**Don't use generic product screenshots as hero images** unless they directly amplify your core message. Visitors already assume software works on multiple devices. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #251) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

**Don't place redundant CTAs in the main navigation** that duplicate menu items already present (e.g., "View Pricing" when "Pricing" is already in the nav). (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #251)

**Don't lead with features, pricing, and technology** instead of the customer's problems and desired outcomes. This causes all competitors to look the same. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #251)

**Don't use abstract icons or generic visual elements without clear context or explanation.** If you use icons, pair them with clear text that explains what they represent. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

**Don't use animated explainer videos on product pages.** Use real product screenshots, GIFs, or actual product video (e.g., Loom recordings) that show the real interface instead. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

**Don't include blog articles or generic resources on product pages.** Visitors on a product page have already moved past top-of-funnel awareness. Blog articles distract from this goal and create loops that send prospects away from the page. Case studies showing real product usage are acceptable; generic articles are not. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Don't repeat the same feature list multiple times on a product page.** Avoid listing the same features or capabilities in multiple sections. This creates redundancy and wastes space that could be used for deeper explanation or differentiation. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Don't embed YouTube videos on product pages.** YouTube embeds can display unrelated video recommendations and distract prospects. Host videos natively or use alternative platforms that don't introduce external distractions. (Source: Dan, Episode #222)

**Don't overwhelm the first fold with too much information.** Avoid overloading the hero with features, news, or multiple competing messages. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Don't test each homepage section separately in isolation.** Testing every component independently can result in a page where each element converts well individually but the overall experience becomes annoying or incoherent. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Don't use chatbots that pop up immediately, make noise, or persist across pages after being dismissed.** These drive visitors away. (Source: Guy Yalif, Episode #251)

**Don't use reverse IP lookups or other individually identifying personalization tactics** that feel invasive to visitors. (Source: Guy Yalif, Episode #251)

**Don't use design graphics, doodles, or stock imagery** as substitutes for real product screenshots. Design graphics have become the new stock photo — everyone copies the trend. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119) *(Note: this is contested for hero sections specifically — see Where Experts Disagree)*

**Don't try to explain everything on the homepage** when you have multiple products or personas. A diluted homepage serves no one well. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)
