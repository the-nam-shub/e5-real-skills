# Customer Win-Back

## Overview
This skill covers how to re-engage dormant leads and win back churned customers in B2B marketing contexts. It addresses sequencing, tone, sender selection, and messaging strategy for both cold/dormant prospects and fully churned accounts. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests; only what those guests explicitly recommended is included here.

---

## Re-engaging Dormant or Cold Leads

When reaching out to leads who were previously in your sales process but have gone cold, follow this sequence:

1. **Do not lead with product features or benefits.** Open with acknowledgment of the prior relationship, not a pitch. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
2. **Give them a reason why now is a good time to reconnect.** This could be a relevant new case study, a meaningful product update, or an exclusive incentive — something that makes the outreach feel timely and earned rather than arbitrary. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
3. **Ask for their input before you tell them anything.** Invite them to share what has changed on their end or what their current challenges are. This signals genuine interest and reestablishes dialogue. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
4. **Send as a plain-text email from a specific person** — ideally someone the lead has interacted with before — not a branded marketing template. The goal is to feel like a human reaching out, not a campaign firing. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
5. **Include an easy opt-out option.** Respect their choice to disengage. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
6. **Only after re-establishing connection should you introduce new features or product updates.** The re-engagement conversation must come first. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)

## Winning Back Churned (Boomerang) Customers

Churned customers represent a broken trust relationship, not just a lapsed contract. The approach must reflect that.

1. **Give it time first.** Segment your win-back ABM efforts by how long ago the customer churned. Let emotions settle before making contact. Reaching out too soon risks reinforcing the negative experience that caused them to leave. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
2. **Lead with honesty and humor, not product updates.** Open with a message that directly acknowledges the company fell short — something like *"I'm sorry, we sucked"* — and admits that vendors and humans miss sometimes. This humanizes the brand and signals you are not simply trying to close a deal. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
3. **Choose the least-salesy sender available.** Consider having the CEO or head of product reach out rather than a sales rep. The goal is to signal that this is a genuine conversation, not a pipeline play. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
4. **Optionally offer a small incentive to gather feedback.** A gift card or similar gesture in exchange for candid input on why they churned can open the door and generate useful intelligence. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
5. **Only after establishing this honest, human tone should you share product improvements** that address the original reason they left. The trust rebuild must precede the pitch. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not open a win-back or re-engagement message with product features or benefits.** Leading with what's new or improved signals you are prioritizing the sale over the relationship. Both guests independently identified this as the wrong starting point. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247; Chris Rack, Episode #150)
- **Do not send branded marketing templates to dormant leads.** Plain-text, person-to-person emails outperform campaign-style outreach in re-engagement contexts. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
- **Do not reach out to churned customers immediately after they leave.** Emotions are still raw. Segment by churn recency and wait for an appropriate window before making contact. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
- **Do not have a salesperson lead the win-back outreach to churned accounts.** The association with a sales role undermines the trust-rebuilding goal. Use a founder, executive, or product leader instead. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)
- **Do not skip the opt-out option** when re-engaging dormant leads. (Source: Sheri Otto, Episode #247)
