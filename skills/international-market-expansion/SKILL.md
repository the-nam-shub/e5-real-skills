# International Market Expansion

## Overview
This skill covers B2B marketing practices for expanding into new geographic markets, from initial market entry strategy and hiring to content localization and AI-assisted translation at scale. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 3 episodes. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved.

---

## Market Entry Strategy

### Hire Local Marketer-Salesperson Pairs
When entering a new country or region, hire at least two people: a marketer and a salesperson, both from that country and living there. Do not attempt to serve a new market from headquarters with translated content or remote staff unfamiliar with the region.

- The marketer's job is to create **native content** tailored to local market nuances, legislation, and cultural differences — not translations of existing content.
- The salesperson must already have an **existing network and presence** in that market to generate early customer relationships.
- Treat this two-person unit as a self-contained go-to-market team, analogous to how the company originally launched in its home market.

(Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)

### Budget One Full Year With No Revenue Expectations
Treat the first year in a new market as a sunk-cost investment. Do not include projected revenue from the new market in annual forecasts during this period.

- Allocate budget for the two-person hire (marketer + salesperson) and allow them to operate independently.
- Use the first year to validate whether the product and messaging resonate locally before scaling.
- Give the team space to find both product-market fit and go-to-market fit specific to that region.

(Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)

## Pre-Expansion Research

### Identify Dominant Local Channels Before You Enter
Before committing to a new market, research which platforms and channels your target audience actually uses in that region. Do not assume your home-market channel mix will transfer.

- Example: LinkedIn is the dominant professional network in the US, but **Xing** is the equivalent in Germany.
- Identify local automation tools and outreach strategies specific to those platforms.
- Allocate marketing effort to where your audience congregates locally, not where they congregate in your current market.

(Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)

## AI-Assisted Localization at Scale

### Use Phrase + Claude to Scale Content Localization
Implement a localization platform (such as Phrase) combined with Claude to rapidly localize content into multiple languages without proportionally scaling headcount.

- Shift your localization team's role from manual translation and QA to **strategic market selection and quality oversight**.
- Use AI to handle the bulk of localization work; apply human QA only to critical markets where brand consistency is essential.
- For lower-priority markets, accept approximately **94% accuracy** in exchange for speed.
- This approach has delivered a reported **16x increase in sign-ups** across the first four languages launched.

(Source: Tara Robertson, Episode #288)

## AI Video Localization

### Test Voice Cloning vs. Dubbing by Language Pair
When localizing video content, do not default to a single approach across all target languages. Test both voice cloning and dubbing to determine which sounds more natural for each specific language combination, and document results in a localization playbook.

- Voice cloning works better for **closely related language families** (e.g., Spanish to French).
- Voice cloning sounds unnatural when applied across **distant language families** (e.g., American English to Asian languages); use pre-recorded native voices for dubbing in those cases.
- Build a playbook that records which approach works best for each language pair so future campaigns can move faster.

(Source: Carter, Episode #345)

> ⚠️ **Note: The question of whether to apply AI voice cloning universally or conditionally is contested — see "Where Experts Disagree" below.**

### Clone Executive Voice for Brand Consistency Across Markets
Use voice cloning technology to record a CEO or executive speaking in their native language, then clone that voice to produce ads in other languages. This maintains executive presence and brand consistency without requiring the executive to record in multiple languages or hiring separate voice talent for each market.

(Source: Luke, Episode #345)

> ⚠️ **Note: This practice is contested — see "Where Experts Disagree" below.**

## Where Experts Disagree

### Should AI voice cloning be applied universally across all language pairs, or only conditionally based on language distance?

**Support summary: 1 vs. 1** (both guests from Episode #345, same episode)

---

**Position A — Apply AI voice cloning universally across target languages**

*Supported by: Luke (Episode #345)*

Luke recommends cloning a CEO or executive's native-language voice to produce ads in other languages, citing brand consistency and executive presence as the primary benefits. His recommendation does not qualify which language pairs this approach works for or flag cases where it may sound unnatural.

---

**Position B — Apply AI voice cloning only for closely related language families; use native dubbing for distant pairs**

*Supported by: Carter (Episode #345)*

Carter explicitly tested both voice cloning and dubbing across language combinations and found that voice cloning works well for romance-to-romance language pairs (e.g., Spanish to French) but sounds unnatural when applied across distant language families (e.g., American English to Asian languages). Carter recommends using pre-recorded native voices for dubbing in those cases, and building a localization playbook that documents which approach works for each combination.

---

**Context dependency:** Luke's advice may implicitly assume language pairs where cloning works well, while Carter is explicitly accounting for cross-family language distance. Both guests are speaking in the same episode about the same tactic, making this a genuine tactical disagreement about universal versus conditional application — not a difference in context or use case.

**Trend note:** None available.

**Why it matters for your decision:** Applying AI voice cloning indiscriminately across all language pairs risks producing unnatural-sounding ads in key markets (e.g., Japan, Korea), which can damage brand perception. Carter's conditional approach requires more upfront testing and a more complex workflow, but protects quality in linguistically distant markets. Luke's universal approach is simpler to execute and preserves executive presence, but may not account for the quality degradation Carter observed. If you are expanding into Asian markets, Carter's evidence-based caution warrants serious consideration before committing to a voice-cloning-only workflow.

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not translate existing home-market content and call it localization.** Hire local marketers who create native content from scratch, tailored to local legislation, culture, and nuances. (Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)
- **Do not include new-market revenue in your annual forecast during year one.** Doing so creates pressure that undermines the team's ability to find genuine product-market fit. (Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)
- **Do not assume your home-market channels dominate in new regions.** Research local platforms before entering; defaulting to LinkedIn in a market where Xing or another platform dominates wastes budget and reach. (Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)
- **Do not apply AI voice cloning indiscriminately across all language pairs without testing.** Voice cloning across distant language families (e.g., English to Japanese) can sound unnatural and damage brand perception in those markets. (Source: Carter, Episode #345)
- **Do not require human QA on every localized asset in every market.** Reserve intensive human review for critical markets; accepting ~94% AI accuracy in lower-priority markets enables significantly faster scaling. (Source: Tara Robertson, Episode #288)
