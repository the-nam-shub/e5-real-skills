# Social Media Strategy for B2B Marketers

## Overview
This skill covers social media strategy for B2B marketers, including how to choose channels, structure content, grow audiences, measure progress, and scale output. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 2 episodes. No general marketing knowledge has been added — gaps in coverage are intentional.

---

## Channel Selection and Focus

**Start with one channel before expanding.** Pick the single social channel where your target audience is most present and commit to it exclusively until you build real momentum. Each platform has distinct formats, algorithms, and audience expectations. Splitting effort across multiple channels simultaneously dilutes results. Once you achieve traction on one channel, you can add a second and selectively repurpose content — but recognize that not all content translates across platforms. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

**For B2B SaaS startups with limited resources, LinkedIn is a strong primary channel.** Set a specific, measurable goal (e.g., a follower count target) and direct all awareness-building effort there rather than attempting PR, events, SEO, and paid ads simultaneously. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #119)

**Use Twitter/X to rapidly test messaging, then scale winners to LinkedIn.** Post frequently on Twitter (10+ times per day) to validate which ideas resonate — each tweet reaches its own audience independently. When a tweet shows strong engagement, expand it into a longer-form LinkedIn post. Be aware that LinkedIn penalizes posting more than once per day, so reserve it for polished, validated ideas. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Metrics and Measurement

**In early stages, track output metrics — not engagement metrics.** When your audience is small and you lack benchmark data, focus on metrics you control: number of posts published, blog posts written, influencer videos created, etc. Obsessing over engagement or virality at this stage creates pressure without useful signal. Consistent output builds reps and reveals what resonates. Engagement metrics become meaningful only once you have scale. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Treat follower count as a distribution asset, not a vanity metric.** Social followers represent an opted-in audience that multiplies your promotional reach without paid spend. A large follower base means that when you have something to promote — a webinar, lead magnet, or product launch — a single post can drive significant results. Example: 160,000 LinkedIn followers enabled one post to drive 200 newsletter subscribers; one email and two LinkedIn posts drove 900 webinar registrations. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

## Content Research and Benchmarking

**Before creating content on a new platform, study what's already working there.** Search for keywords relevant to your industry, sort by top posts or highest engagement, and analyze the format, length, structure, and messaging of high-performing content. Use this as a template — do not simply apply formats from other platforms. Example: on TikTok, search relevant hashtags, find videos with the most likes and comments, and recreate similar formats with your own angle. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Analyze competitor LinkedIn pages to identify high-performing content formats.** Use LinkedIn's page analytics feature to view competitor pages and their engagement metrics. Identify which content types generate the highest engagement and replicate the winning format in your own strategy. This approach revealed that memes generated 10–100x higher engagement than other content types for one brand, leading to a daily meme posting strategy. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use X's "See Similar Posts" feature to find inspiration and benchmark your own performance.** When a post resonates, click "See Similar Posts" to view the algorithm's related content on that topic. This surfaces how competitors and other creators are covering the same subject. Identify posts with higher engagement, analyze what made them more successful (depth, formatting, use of bullet points, etc.), and use those insights to rewrite and reschedule your own content with improvements. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## Content Production and Workflows

**Structure X threads with a proven four-part formula:**
1. An eye-catching opening image (screenshot of a company or person, a growth chart, or a relevant visual)
2. A hook built around a compelling stat or data point
3. Core content that is "ridiculously valuable" — make the reader think "why is this free?"
4. A soft call-to-action at the end (reference another thread or invite newsletter subscription — not a hard sell)

This format drives both engagement and downstream conversions such as newsletter signups and customer inquiries. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Use a batched, delegated workflow for X thread production:**
- Source thread ideas from community discussions (e.g., Slack communities), comments on related posts, or questions about specific companies or topics
- Write the idea as a blog post first, then convert it into thread format
- Use a team of writers to research and draft threads based on a first-principles methodology
- Schedule one-off tweets using Buffer; schedule threads using Typefully

This workflow enables batching and delegation while maintaining quality and consistency. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Repurpose top-performing posts monthly.** Use analytics tools (e.g., Shield app or Taplio for LinkedIn) to identify your highest-performing posts each month. Repost the exact same content or a slightly refreshed version back into your content queue. This leverages proven engagement without requiring new creative work. Example: a LinkedIn post about a lead magnet drove 700 subscribers on first posting; reposting it six months later drove 200 more subscribers in a single day. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

## Organizational and Team Strategy

**Train your sales team to build personal brands and share content on social media.** Empower sales reps to develop their own social presence and post about their work, industry insights, and the customer problems they solve. Sales reps with personal brands generate more direct pipeline, higher quota attainment, and stronger customer relationships than those relying solely on email outreach. Provide training on how to show up on LinkedIn and X in a way that aligns with company messaging. This multiplies your brand's reach across many voices. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not obsess over engagement or virality metrics when your audience is small.** You lack the benchmark data to make those metrics meaningful. Focus on output consistency first. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Do not try to execute across multiple social channels simultaneously when starting out.** Diluted effort produces diluted results. Master one channel before adding another. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121; Dave Gerhardt, Episode #119)
- **Do not apply content formats from one platform directly to another.** Each platform has unique audience expectations and algorithmic behavior. Research what works natively on each platform before creating content for it. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Do not post to LinkedIn more than once per day.** LinkedIn penalizes reach when you post too frequently. Reserve it for polished, validated content. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
- **Do not use hard-sell CTAs at the end of X threads.** Soft CTAs (referencing related content, inviting newsletter subscriptions) outperform direct sales pitches in social contexts. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Do not treat follower count as a vanity metric to be dismissed.** A large, opted-in follower base is a real distribution asset with measurable promotional value. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)
