# Positioning and Messaging

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketers should develop positioning strategy, craft external messaging, test copy with real buyers, and avoid common messaging failures. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 3 episodes. No general marketing knowledge has been added — gaps in coverage are intentional.

---

## Positioning Strategy: The Foundation

**Distinguish positioning from messaging before doing anything else.**
Positioning is an internal strategic document that defines who you serve and what use case you solve (e.g., "CRM for accountants"). Messaging is the external communication that translates that positioning into language that resonates with customers. Positioning is not a line of copy — it is a strategic anchor. Conflating the two leads to confused internal debates where strategy and execution get mixed together. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Specialize in an underserved category rather than remaining a generalist.**
Identify an emerging or underserved category with mediocre existing content and limited competitors. Research which topics lack quality coverage, then systematically dominate that space by creating superior content, hosting events, writing books, and developing proprietary frameworks. This approach can move a brand from unknown to category leader within five years. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Consider separate brands when products serve fundamentally different markets.**
If a company has two products with different target markets, use cases, and value propositions, evaluate whether to create separate brands rather than forcing them under one umbrella. Trying to retrofit conflicting positioning under one brand creates confusion. (Example: CXL spun agency services into a separate brand — Bureau — rather than keeping them alongside e-learning under one identity.) (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Buyer Understanding: Research Before Messaging

**Conduct buyer intelligence surveys before writing any messaging.**
Survey your target buyer segment to understand their actual priorities, challenges, and what they care about in buying decisions. Use this data to ensure your messaging addresses real problems — not assumed ones. Messaging that targets the wrong pain points will fail regardless of how well it is written. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**In enterprise sales, appeal to junior and manager-level buyers first.**
In enterprise deals, senior executives (VPs, C-level) typically delegate initial research to junior staff (managers, directors) who create a shortlist of 5–10 companies to evaluate. The senior executive then chooses from that filtered list. Messaging must appeal to the manager/director level first, because they control which options ever reach the executive. Do not assume you are only selling to the C-suite. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use different messaging for technical buyers versus economic buyers.**
Identify whether you are selling to the technical buyer (who needs specific technical details) or the economic buyer (who cares about business outcomes). Use technical language for technical buyers — do not dumb it down, as they want specifics. Use outcome-focused language for economic buyers. Ideally, create two distinct versions of messaging tailored to each buyer type. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Messaging Frameworks and Structure

**Evaluate messaging quality across five components: clarity, relevance, value, differentiation, and brand perception.**
Break down messaging into five measurable dimensions:
1. **Clarity** — Do people understand what the product is and how it works?
2. **Relevance** — Do the promised benefits align with the customer's actual priorities?
3. **Value** — Is the perceived value communicated?
4. **Differentiation** — Why choose this company over competitors?
5. **Brand perception** — Does the tone match the intended brand personality?

This framework enables objective discussion about messaging instead of subjective preference debates. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Structure the homepage in a specific order: category → value proposition → differentiation.**
Lead with the category (what is it?) so visitors immediately understand what you do — this can appear in the headline or a preheader. Follow with your value proposition that communicates your "onliness" (why you, not competitors). Then provide proof, examples, and differentiation. This sequence answers the questions visitors ask in order: *What is it? Why should I care? Why you?* The information hierarchy should reflect customer priorities, not internal stakeholder preferences. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Prioritize messaging and creative development over channel optimization.**
Invest heavily in developing strong, memorable messaging and creative concepts before optimizing distribution channels. A strong message can drive 3–5x performance improvements and has inherent self-distribution because people share and discuss it. Weak messaging cannot be fixed by better ad targeting or channel selection. Once messaging is locked, demand gen teams can optimize distribution. (Example: "Friends don't let friends buy a CDP" outperformed generic messaging by 500%.) (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Prioritize increasing user motivation over reducing friction.**
There are two levers for improving conversion: reducing friction and increasing motivation. Increasing motivation is approximately 10x more powerful than reducing friction. Motivation is driven by messaging and copy, not design or UX. Focus resources on compelling messaging that makes users want to take action rather than optimizing form fields or removing steps. Users with high motivation will tolerate significant friction. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Message Testing and Iteration

**Test messaging with target customers before launching paid advertising campaigns.**
Before spending significant budget on paid ads (e.g., LinkedIn PPC), validate that your core messaging resonates with target customers. Test on your most important pages (homepage, key product pages) and before launching new products or campaigns. This prevents wasting ad spend on messaging that does not land. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Conduct message testing by putting copy in front of your actual target customer segment.**
Use a two-sided marketplace approach to connect your messaging (website copy, email, mockups) with actual members of your target customer segment. Ask specific research questions such as "How likely are you to request a demo?" and "What's still unclear?" Gather rich qualitative feedback on what resonates, what confuses, and what questions remain. This can be done in 24 hours and provides actionable insights for copy iteration. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use OODA loops to rapidly iterate on messaging through fast feedback cycles.**
Apply the military OODA loop framework (Observe, Orient, Decide, Act) to messaging optimization: create an initial version, put it in front of your target audience, observe their feedback on what is clear/unclear/interesting/boring, orient based on that feedback, decide on changes, and act by implementing them. The speed of your feedback loops determines how quickly you reach resonant copy. A 24-hour feedback loop is achievable and far superior to monthly or quarterly cycles. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use message testing to resolve stakeholder disagreements objectively.**
When a CEO or stakeholder is attached to a particular headline or messaging approach, use message testing to get objective feedback from target customers. Present the data to the stakeholder rather than arguing subjectively. Rational decision-makers will change their position when presented with evidence that their preferred messaging does not resonate with the target audience. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Copy Execution

**Use real product screenshots instead of doodles or abstract graphics.**
Replace decorative doodles, abstract graphics, and stock imagery with actual screenshots of your product in action. Real screenshots are the clearest way to show how the product works. While brand personality and visual design matter, clarity about product functionality should take priority. Doodles have become the new stock photo — overused and less effective than showing the actual product. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Have product marketing own positioning and strategy; use a dedicated copywriter for execution.**
Product marketing should own the overall positioning, website strategy, and messaging framework. However, the actual execution of copy (headlines, subheads, body copy) should be handled by a dedicated copywriter who specializes in the craft. Most product marketers are not strong copywriters. The copywriter works within the positioning and strategy set by product marketing but handles the craft of making copy compelling and concise. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**When reposting evergreen content, audit tone and language for shifts in audience sentiment.**
When republishing high-performing older content, review the language for cultural or sentiment shifts. Update terminology that may now be perceived negatively while preserving the core idea. (Example: replace "hustle" with "work hard" if audience sentiment has shifted against hustle culture, keeping the message intact while avoiding potential backlash.) (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## What NOT To Do

**Do not write vague, generic messaging to satisfy multiple internal stakeholders.**
When internal stakeholders (CEO, sales, marketing) have conflicting priorities, the temptation is to write vague copy that attempts to satisfy everyone. This results in messaging that sounds professional but says nothing specific. Be specific about what you do, who you serve, and what problem you solve. Specificity is more persuasive than attempting to appeal to everyone. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Do not assume you are selling only to the C-suite in enterprise deals.**
Senior executives rarely do initial vendor research themselves. Messaging aimed exclusively at the C-level may never reach them if it fails to resonate with the managers and directors who build the shortlist. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Do not blame distribution channels for poor performance when messaging is the real problem.**
Demand gen teams are frequently blamed for underperformance when the actual issue is weak creative or messaging. Optimizing targeting, bidding, or channel mix cannot compensate for a message that does not resonate. Fix the message first. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Do not prioritize friction reduction over motivation in conversion optimization.**
Removing form fields and streamlining UX is far less impactful than improving the messaging that motivates users to act. Do not let design and UX work substitute for the harder work of developing compelling copy. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Do not use decorative visuals as a substitute for showing the actual product.**
Abstract graphics and doodles obscure how a product works. Clarity about product functionality should take priority over visual decoration. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Do not run paid campaigns before validating that your messaging resonates.**
Spending significant budget on ads before message testing is a predictable way to waste money. Validate first, then scale. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
