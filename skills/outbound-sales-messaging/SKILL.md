# Outbound Sales Messaging

## Overview
This skill covers how to write and structure B2B outbound messaging that compels prospects to consider change — not by pitching your product, but by surfacing the hidden costs of their current behavior. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests; nothing has been added from outside that source material.

---

## The Core Reframe: Status Quo Is the Real Competitor

Before writing a single word of outbound copy, establish this foundational mindset with the user: in most B2B sales cycles, the primary competitor is not another vendor — it is the prospect's current way of doing things.

- Recognize that prospects can acknowledge your solution is objectively better and still choose to do nothing, because "good enough" beats "uncertain change." (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- Do not default to competitive messaging framed as "we're better than X." That framing only works if the prospect has already decided to change. Most haven't. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- Reframe the goal of outbound messaging: the job is not to win a feature comparison — it is to help prospects decide whether change is necessary at all. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

---

## Eliminating Me-Centric Language

Audit any outbound email or message the user brings for the following before offering improvements:

- Scan for overuse of "we," "our," and the sender's company name in the opening lines. This pattern — sometimes called the "we problem" — is endemic in cold outbound and signals to prospects that the email is about the sender, not them. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- Flag any message that leads with the sender's company, product features, or competitive differentiation. These are me-centric structures.
- Restructure outbound so it opens with the prospect's context, behavior, or situation — not the sender's identity or offering.
- Do not mention the product, solution, or company name in initial outreach. The goal is to make prospects think about themselves first, before asking them to think about you. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

## Leading with Cost of Staying the Same (Not ROI)

When helping users choose what angle to lead with in outbound messaging, apply this principle:

- Prioritize the cost of staying the same over ROI claims. ROI is a "maybe" — it describes a potential future return contingent on action. That is high-risk messaging for a prospect who hasn't yet decided they have a problem. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- The cost of staying the same is concrete and present. It describes negative consequences the prospect is already experiencing (or will experience) from their current approach. This is lower-risk and more persuasive at the top of the funnel. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- Sequence the messaging logic: first, help the prospect perceive a negative consequence of their current state → then, and only then, does ROI messaging become effective. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- Distinguish between "cost of doing nothing" (passive framing) and "cost of staying the same" (active framing that implicates the prospect's own behavior). Use the latter. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

## The Four-Question Framework for Building Outbound Messaging

When a user needs to build outbound messaging from scratch, or diagnose why existing messaging isn't working, guide them through this four-question framework. Recommend they work through it collaboratively with sales, product, and leadership:

1. **Who is most likely to have the problem we solve?** Identify the specific persona and context — not a broad ICP, but the person whose current behavior creates the problem. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
2. **How do they solve it today, and why?** Understand the current approach and the reasoning behind it. This prevents messaging that dismisses the status quo rather than acknowledging it. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
3. **What unintended negative consequence results from their current approach?** This is the core of the message. The consequence should be something the prospect may not have fully recognized or named. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
4. **Who else took a different approach?** Identify a reference point — a peer, a customer, a recognizable archetype — who changed their behavior and experienced a different outcome. This creates social proof without requiring a product pitch. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

Use the answers to these four questions to craft messaging that creates **perception curiosity** — an itch to scratch that makes prospects want to learn more about their own situation before they're asked to evaluate your solution. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

## Perception Curiosity Cold Email Structure

When helping a user write or rewrite a cold email, follow this five-part structure designed to trigger perception curiosity (the cognitive itch that occurs when someone encounters information that contradicts what they believe to be true):

1. **Subject line:** Write a subject line that prompts opening by referencing the prospect's context or a surprising outcome — not your product. Example: "Uber bill" rather than "[Company] + [Prospect Company]." (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
2. **Establish context with a relevant statistic:** Open the body by grounding the prospect in their own world. Use a specific, credible data point that reflects their environment or behavior. Example: "Chicago gets 119 rainy days a year." (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
3. **Highlight the behavior gap or unintended consequence:** Name what most people in their situation do — and what it costs them. Example: "Only 5% of people remember to bring an umbrella, so 95% end up ordering Ubers at surge pricing." (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
4. **Reference someone who solved it differently:** Introduce a peer or archetype who changed their behavior and got a different result. Example: "Betty found a way to cut her Uber bill by $200/month." Do not name your product here. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
5. **Ask if they're open to learning how:** Close with a low-friction question that invites curiosity, not commitment. Do not pitch your product or solution in this ask. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)

This structure makes the prospect think about their own behavior before they are ever asked to think about you or your company.

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not lead with your company name, product, or features in cold outreach.** This is the "we problem" — it signals the email is about you, not the prospect, and reduces the chance of engagement. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- **Do not lead with ROI claims in initial outbound.** ROI is a conditional promise ("if you do this, you might get that") and is ineffective before the prospect has decided they have a problem worth solving. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- **Do not frame the primary competitive threat as another vendor.** Messaging built around "we're better than [Competitor]" fails against status quo inertia, which is the more common reason deals stall or die. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- **Do not use "cost of doing nothing" framing.** It is passive and lets the prospect off the hook. Use "cost of staying the same" instead — it implicates the prospect's active behavior and is more likely to create urgency. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- **Do not mention your product in the cold email structure above.** The entire point of the perception curiosity approach is to make the prospect curious about their own situation first. Introducing your product too early collapses that curiosity into a sales pitch. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
- **Do not build outbound messaging in isolation.** The four-question framework is designed to be worked through with sales, product, and leadership — not by marketing alone. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #343)
