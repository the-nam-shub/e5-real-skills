# B2B Podcast Strategy

## Overview
This skill covers B2B podcast strategy from pre-launch validation through ongoing production, guest sourcing, content repurposing, and ROI measurement. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 20 episodes; no external best practices have been added. Where guests disagree, both positions are presented with full attribution rather than a single synthesized recommendation.

---

## Pre-Launch: Validation and Purpose

Before helping a user launch a podcast, work through the following questions with them. Do not skip this phase.

**Validate audience fit first.** Ask whether the target audience actually listens to podcasts, whether existing podcasts already serve this niche, how frequently those competitors publish, and whether there is a genuine gap (infrequent publishing, low quality, underserved angle). Only proceed if audience consumption is confirmed and a gap exists. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)

**Define a clear purpose before starting.** The podcast's purpose must connect to overall business strategy and brand goals. If the user cannot articulate why they are doing this beyond "we should have a podcast," flag that the project risks becoming a vanity exercise that consumes time without delivering value. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Allocate podcast investment within a balanced marketing budget.** A podcast should not consume the entire marketing strategy. Recommend roughly 70% of marketing time and resources toward activities that drive immediate revenue (outreach, ads, sales enablement) and 30% toward long-term brand building including podcasts and thought leadership. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

> **Note:** How much pre-work to do before launching is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.

## Launch Approach and Production Setup

**Start with minimal equipment; do not delay for perfect production.** Advise users to begin with a basic USB microphone and simple editing tools such as GarageBand. Focus on consistency and content quality first. Invest in better equipment, professional editing, and production support only after proving the show can sustain itself and has an audience. Every successful creator started with minimal gear. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #320)

**Use free or low-cost tools to lower the barrier to entry.** Free tools such as GarageBand for editing and Anchor for hosting are sufficient to produce and distribute episodes at launch. This allows rapid testing of the concept without budget approval. Focus on getting guests and publishing consistently, then iterate based on feedback and download metrics. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)

> **Note:** The balance between launching fast and validating first is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.

**Consider batch-recording full seasons in-person.** Rather than recording individual episodes over time, one approach is to fly in-person and record a full season (typically 8–10 episodes) over two days. Prepare detailed outlines for each episode covering specific points and stories, but do not script the conversation. Bring multiple outfit changes and plan episode order strategically. This creates consistent energy and chemistry throughout the season and allows episode order to be adjusted based on how the recording day goes. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #321)

**Choose format based on your strengths and network, not industry trends.** Do not default to interview-format podcasts simply because they are popular. If the host has a strong network and is comfortable on mic, interview shows work well. If they are better at scripted content or storytelling, use that format instead. The format should match the host's strengths and the value they can deliver to the audience. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

## Content Strategy and Positioning

**Build a podcast that serves the audience's needs, not the product's narrative.** The podcast should be valuable to the target audience independent of the product. Focus on problems the audience faces and decisions they make, not on the company's solution. If episodes keep steering back to the product, the concept needs to be reconsidered — even if that means pivoting days before launch. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

**Use a podcast as an awareness magnet, not a direct lead generation tool.** Position the podcast as a way to continuously attract people to the brand by providing value independent of the product. Measure success not only by direct pipeline attribution (which will be difficult to track) but by secondary signals: growth in personal social following, registrations for related events, and general brand buzz. This is particularly effective when hosted by company leaders who can have authentic conversations. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #321)

**Frame the podcast as an integrated content strategy when pitching internally.** When presenting a podcast to sales or leadership, do not pitch it as a standalone channel. Show how each episode generates multiple content assets — LinkedIn clips, ghost-written posts, sales talking points, newsletter content. This makes the ROI clearer and justifies the long-term investment. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Use podcasts to build brand and trust through non-product-focused conversations.** A B2B podcast does not need to discuss the product or even the industry directly. Topics like books, lessons, entrepreneurship, and industry trends build familiarity, trust, and brand affinity. When prospects encounter the company later, they already have positive associations from listening, which increases conversion likelihood. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Use podcasting as a thinking and positioning tool, not just a distribution channel.** Record long-form content (20–30 minutes uninterrupted) and re-listen to your own recordings. This surfaces ideas that landed, messaging that could be refined, product positioning insights, and strategic clarity. The act of speaking unscripted forces organic thinking; re-listening reveals patterns and insights that were not consciously remembered. Allocate approximately 3 hours per week for this practice. (Source: Chris Walker, Episodes #281 and #211)

**Use podcasting as a practice medium for developing clear points of view.** Even if a podcast never grows a large audience, the act of articulating ideas in conversation trains clearer thinking and stronger opinions. This translates to better marketing strategy and communication. The learning happens through the doing, not through audience size. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episode #257)

## Guest Sourcing and Outreach

**Use podcast download analytics to identify and recruit top-performing guests.** Sort podcast analytics by most downloads over a given period (e.g., the past year), identify the top 10 episodes, and reach out to those speakers for future episodes and events. This approach validates content resonance before booking, ensures the speaker already has a relationship with the audience, and avoids paying for unknown speakers. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #281, #225, and #211)

> **Note:** Whether to prioritize analytics-identified guests or guests you already have relationships with is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.

**Build guest relationships through demonstrated engagement before pitching.** When reaching out to potential guests, establish credibility by demonstrating genuine familiarity with their work. Reference specific details from their previous interviews or posts and show evidence of regular interaction (e.g., LinkedIn comments) before requesting an appearance. This increases response rates significantly compared to generic outreach. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Use podcast guest invitations as an ABM tactic.** Invite target accounts and decision-makers to appear as guests on the podcast, then follow up with sales outreach at a later date. This gives high-value prospects a reason to engage with the brand and builds relationship equity before a direct sales conversation. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #137)

**Appear on other people's podcasts to build skills and awareness.** Appearing as a guest on 8–10+ B2B podcasts per month — even those with small audiences of 50–200 listeners — builds credibility with devoted audiences and creates content repurposing opportunities. Each appearance is a touchpoint that elevates brand recognition. The cumulative effect of multiple small audiences is more valuable than chasing one large platform. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Use podcast appearances to build video and speaking skills.** Appearing on other people's podcasts (rather than starting your own) builds comfort speaking on camera, improves speaking skills, and creates networking opportunities. The value is in skill-building and relationship-building, not reach. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

## Interview Preparation

**Over-prepare for interviews, then let the conversation flow naturally.** Before recording, do extensive research on the guest: listen to their past interviews, read their work, understand their background. Create a detailed list of prepared questions. Once the conversation starts, be willing to abandon the notes and follow the natural flow. The preparation provides confidence and context; the flexibility creates authenticity. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #320)

**Prepare more heavily for guests you do not already know.** Interviewing someone without a prior relationship requires significantly more mental energy and preparation than interviewing someone you know. When the relationship already exists, the conversation flows naturally. For cold interviews, invest heavily in preparation to compensate for the lack of existing rapport. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207)

> **Note:** How to balance relationship-based vs. analytics-based guest selection is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.

## Content Repurposing: The Content Waterfall

**Use each podcast episode as the nucleus of a multi-format content flywheel.** From a single episode, extract and produce: short video clips (highly edited), written articles, social media threads, newsletter content, and blog posts. This single source of content fuels multiple distribution channels and reduces production burden. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #225 and #189; Chelsea Castle, Episodes #262 and #172; Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Use AI tools to extract insights from transcripts at scale.** Use AI to extract key insights, frameworks, and stories from podcast transcripts and convert them into multiple content formats. This creates a content strategy where one core piece generates multiple distribution touchpoints, maximizing the value of the original recording. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episode #172)

**Repurpose podcast content across platforms systematically.** Embed audio in blog posts on related topics, create short video clips for social media, turn key quotes into carousel posts, publish full transcripts on Medium with canonical links back to your site, and share individual insights as social media posts. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Use podcast analytics to inform event speaker lineups.** The guests who generated the most downloads are validated content performers. Reach out to them directly, note their popularity on the show, and invite them to speak at events. This is cost-effective, ensures content quality, and speakers are more likely to accept because they already have a relationship with the audience. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #281, #225, and #211)

**Host live podcast takeovers at events featuring partner podcasts.** Create dedicated time slots at events for live podcast recordings featuring partner podcasts and hosts. This gives partners a platform, multiplies event content output, and creates shareable content for both shows. Partners benefit from the live audience and cross-promotion; you benefit from additional content and prestige. (Source: Jared Fuller, Episode #174)

## ROI Measurement and Attribution

**Measure podcast ROI by relationship depth and brand awareness, not direct attribution.** Podcasts have fuzzy attribution and will not show clear direct ROI in traditional marketing dashboards. Measure success by: (1) download growth and audience size, (2) qualitative feedback from listeners (DMs, event conversations), (3) brand awareness lift (people recognizing the brand at events), and (4) depth of connection with the audience — time spent listening builds stronger relationships than email. Accept that podcast ROI is long-term and relationship-based, not transactional. This works best when leadership is comfortable with non-traditional attribution. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)

**View podcasting as a multi-purpose business engine, not just a direct sales channel.** A podcast can serve multiple functions simultaneously: building brand trust, generating content insights that feed marketing and product strategy, interviewing potential customers, and creating touchpoints that contribute to long-term relationship building. The direct signal that a podcast is working is when members and customers say they joined or stayed because of it, but the indirect benefits — strategy insights, brand building, network effects — are equally important. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #320)

**Correlate podcast listens to demo bookings to measure impact.** Plot weekly podcast listens against weekly demo bookings on a scatter chart. If listens and demos move together consistently (dots clustered in upper-right and lower-left quadrants), there is evidence of correlation. If dots are scattered across all quadrants, the metrics are unrelated. This five-minute analysis provides directional evidence of podcast impact without complex data science. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #191)

**Position podcasts as brand-building and lead nurturing, not direct response.** Podcasts contribute to sales indirectly — someone may listen for six months before becoming a customer when a catalyst (budget approval, job change) triggers a purchase decision. Measure success through brand metrics and long-term engagement rather than immediate conversion. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #137)

**Use podcasting as a long-term networking and learning tool.** Start a podcast focused on a topic you are genuinely passionate about, with the goal of meeting interesting people and distributing knowledge — not to generate immediate sales. Measure success by the quality of relationships and learning, not download numbers. Results compound over years. (Source: Sean Lane, Episode #187)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should B2B podcast episodes be kept short (18–22 minutes) or can they be longer?

**Support summary: 3 vs 1 (long-form advocates vs. short-episode advocate)**

**Position A — Keep episodes short (18–22 minutes):**
Shorter podcast episodes get more downloads than longer ones, regardless of content quality. Listeners decide based on title and length before listening. Higher download counts signal to Apple Podcasts and Spotify algorithms to circulate the show more. Long interviews should be broken into multiple focused segments — for example, four 20-minute episodes from one 80-minute interview — to maximize total downloads and algorithmic distribution. *(Source: Jay Schwedelson, Episode #329)*

**Position B — Long-form episodes are valuable:**
Long-form content (20–30 minutes of uninterrupted recording, or full-length interviews) is valuable as a thinking tool, brand-building mechanism, and deep engagement driver. The value is in depth of connection, not algorithmic optimization. Batch-recording full seasons of 8–10 episodes over two days in-person produces consistent energy across substantive, longer episodes. *(Sources: Chris Walker, Episodes #281 and #211; Jess Cook, Episode #321)*

**Context dependency:** These positions are partially addressing different goals. Jay Schwedelson's advice is specifically about maximizing algorithmic distribution and download counts. The long-form advocates are focused on depth of engagement, strategic thinking development, and brand building. If the user's primary goal is algorithmic reach and download growth, short episodes may win. If the goal is deep audience connection, internal strategic value, or brand building, long-form may be better. However, both camps are addressing the same B2B podcast context, making this a genuine tactical disagreement about what to optimize for — not just a difference in use case.

**Why it matters:** Episode length directly affects both production strategy (whether to split interviews) and distribution outcomes (algorithmic visibility vs. depth of engagement). Getting this wrong means either leaving downloads on the table or sacrificing the relationship-building value that makes B2B podcasts worthwhile.

---

### 2. Should you launch a podcast quickly with minimal setup, or validate and plan carefully first?

**Support summary: 2 vs 2**

**Position A — Launch fast with minimal setup:**
Do not wait for perfect production or budget approval. Use free tools like GarageBand and Anchor, focus on consistency and content quality, and iterate based on feedback. Every successful creator started with minimal gear. Do not ask for permission; just launch and learn. *(Sources: Erin May, Episode #337; Dave Gerhardt, Episode #320)*

**Position B — Validate and plan before launching:**
Before investing in a podcast, validate that the target audience actually listens to podcasts and that there is a gap in existing coverage. Conduct desk research and talk to users. Only launch if audience consumption is confirmed and a clear gap exists. Also define a clear purpose connected to business strategy before starting — without this clarity, the podcast becomes a vanity project. *(Sources: Erin May, Episode #337; Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)*

**Important nuance:** This disagreement partially dissolves on closer inspection. Erin May holds both positions simultaneously — validate first, then launch lean. Dave Gerhardt holds both positions across different episodes — launch lean in Episode #320, plan clearly in Episode #189. The genuine tension is between a bias-to-action framing (just start) and a strategic validation framing (confirm fit first). This is a real tactical tension for marketers deciding how much pre-work to do before committing.

**Why it matters:** Launching without audience validation risks wasting months of production effort on a channel the audience does not use. Over-planning risks never launching at all. Which failure mode to guard against depends on the user's specific situation.

---

### 3. Should you prioritize podcast guests you already have relationships with, or use analytics to find the best performers?

**Support summary: 3 vs 1 (analytics-first vs. relationship-first)**

**Position A — Prioritize existing relationships:**
Interviewing someone you already have a relationship with requires significantly less mental energy and preparation, and produces more natural, conversational content. Prioritize guests you know, or invest heavily in preparation for cold interviews to compensate for the lack of existing rapport. *(Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207)*

**Position B — Use analytics to identify top performers:**
Use podcast download analytics to identify which guests generated the most downloads and engagement, then invite those guests to future episodes and events. This data-driven approach validates content quality before booking and creates a flywheel where best-performing content informs the guest pipeline. Sort analytics by most downloads over the past year, identify the top 10 episodes, and reach out to those speakers. *(Sources: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #281, #225, and #211)*

**Context dependency:** These positions are not fully in conflict — they address different aspects of guest sourcing. Jason Lemkin is speaking about interview quality and host preparation burden; Dave Gerhardt is speaking about audience resonance and content performance. A podcast could use analytics to identify top performers AND prioritize those the host already has relationships with. However, they do conflict when a high-performing analytics pick is someone the host does not know, forcing a choice between relationship ease and data-validated performance.

**Why it matters:** Guest sourcing strategy directly affects both content quality and production sustainability. Booking analytically strong guests who produce awkward interviews, or defaulting to comfortable relationships that do not resonate with the audience, are both real failure modes.

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not launch a podcast without knowing why you are doing it.** Without a clear purpose connected to business strategy, the podcast becomes a vanity project that consumes time without delivering value. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

- **Do not make the podcast about your product.** If episodes keep steering back to the product or solution, the concept needs to be reconsidered. The podcast should be valuable to the audience independent of what you sell. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266; Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

- **Do not delay launching while waiting for perfect equipment or production setup.** Start with a basic USB microphone and simple editing tools. Prove consistency first, then invest in production quality. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #320)

- **Do not treat a podcast as a direct-response or lead generation channel.** Podcasts are not direct-response channels and will not generate immediate sales. Measuring them against short-term conversion metrics will make them look like failures even when they are working. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #137; Erin May, Episode #337)

- **Do not make the podcast your entire marketing strategy.** Allocate roughly 70% of marketing resources to activities that drive immediate revenue and 30% to long-term brand building. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

- **Do not use generic outreach when recruiting podcast guests.** Reference specific details from the guest's previous work and show evidence of genuine engagement before requesting an appearance. Generic pitches significantly reduce response rates. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

- **Do not produce a podcast episode and stop there.** Each episode should feed a content waterfall of articles, social clips, newsletter content, and other formats. Failing to repurpose leaves most of the value of the original recording unrealized. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episodes #262 and #172; Dave Gerhardt, Episode #225; Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

- **Do not use webinar replays as a substitute for genuine podcast content.** The format and quality of podcast content directly impacts how effective it is as a nurturing tool. A podcast built on entertainment and genuine conversation feels different from one that is simply repurposed webinar replays. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #137)

- **Do not skip audience validation before launching.** Confirm that your target audience actually listens to podcasts and that there is a gap in existing coverage before investing in production. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)
