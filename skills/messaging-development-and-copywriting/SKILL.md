# B2B Messaging Development and Copywriting

## Overview
This skill covers the full arc of B2B messaging work: diagnosing messaging problems, building foundational frameworks, writing and testing copy, and enabling consistent execution across channels and teams. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast. No general marketing knowledge has been added. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved.

---

## 1. Diagnose Messaging Problems Before Writing Copy

**Distinguish messaging from copy before doing any work.** Messaging is the strategic substance—what you're communicating about your value and solution. Copy is the execution—the words, tone, and format used to express that message. Most copy problems are actually messaging problems in disguise. Fix the messaging first; then translate it into copy. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episodes #300 and #226; Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Use the CUB test to identify specific positioning weaknesses.** Ask: Is the positioning Confusing (unclear category, trying to list everything)? Unbelievable (claims without substantiation)? Boring (not targeted at the right problem for your market)? Pinpoint which failure mode applies before attempting to rewrite. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

**Listen for predictable signals of weak positioning in sales conversations.** Weak positioning shows up as: (1) "What are you again?"—prospects don't understand what you are; (2) "You're just like Salesforce"—prospects misclassify you; (3) "I get what you do, I just don't get why we'd pay for that"—prospects don't see the value. When you hear these repeatedly, you have a positioning problem. Use this evidence to convince sales leadership and the CEO that positioning work is necessary—frame it as a sales productivity issue, not a marketing initiative. (Source: April Dunford, Episode #309)

**Validate positioning through sales feedback and panel testing before major rebrands.** Listen to actual sales calls to hear how reps explain the company to prospects, then validate with panel testing. If the positioning requires explanation or generates confusion ("what is it?"), it's wrong. Test alternatives until the positioning generates the right competitive frame. In Paddle's case, shifting from "revenue delivery platform" to "complete payments infrastructure for software" immediately changed prospect questions from "what is it?" to "how do you compare to Stripe?"—indicating correct positioning. (Source: Andrew Davies, Episode #195)

**Lead positioning change initiatives with sales performance data, not opinion.** When proposing a positioning change, start by establishing that the current positioning is underperforming using concrete metrics (declining win rates, lower conversion rates). Use this data to build consensus that change is necessary before presenting new positioning. This shifts the conversation from opinion-based debate to fact-based problem-solving. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

## 2. Build the Messaging Foundation

### Define Your Core Message

**Establish a single flagship message (One Key Message) as your north star.** Ask: if prospects had to remember one thing about your product, what would it be? If team members give different answers, this signals misalignment that must be resolved before proceeding. This OKM prevents the "messaging buffet" problem where companies try to communicate too many value propositions at once. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episodes #300 and #226)

**Evaluate your flagship message against five criteria.** Test it against: (1) Is it memorable? (2) Would everyone across the team say it the same way? (3) Could people explain it? (4) Is it actually coming from the value of your product? (5) Does it differentiate you and push your brand forward? If it fails any of these, refine before proceeding. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Run a five-second test to validate memorability.** Show your website or marketing message to someone for five seconds, then ask what they remember. If they can't recall your key message or what you do, your messaging isn't working. Use this to pressure-test headlines, homepages, and key messaging before launch. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Test brand messages against the competitor claim test.** Ask: could a competitor claim the exact same thing and have it be believable? If yes, the message is not differentiated enough and should be rejected. This forces you to move beyond generic claims and find positioning that only your company can authentically own. (Source: Ari Yablock, Episode #284)

**Use a value proposition framework instead of a unique selling point.** Structure it around: the old way (how they do it now), the problem with that way, and what they can do now thanks to your product, plus the impact or gain. This is more customer-centric and works better for B2B products that solve problems across multiple user profiles, since differentiation usually comes from an intersection of elements rather than a single selling point. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**When you can't name a villain, lead with a bold promise.** When naming a villain or calling out an outdated approach isn't viable, lead with a bold promise that encapsulates your value—for example, Copy Hackers' "become the most profitable person in the room." This promise becomes the through-line message that translates across all marketing channels. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

### Structure Your Messaging Architecture

**Build a messaging pyramid from one sentence to multi-level proof points.** Develop: (1) one-sentence company definition; (2) three-sentence version; (3) messaging pillars (e.g., simplicity, future of work); (4) two-sentence elaboration per pillar; (5) five-sentence deep dive per pillar; (6) proof points and public data supporting each pillar. Build this by interviewing product, engineering, PMM, and revenue teams. This framework feeds into talk tracks and executive communications. (Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episodes #302 and #193)

**Define 2-3 messaging pillars and structure your annual content program around them.** Assign each pillar to specific events or time periods throughout the year. Ensure every event, campaign, and piece of content ladders back to whichever pillar is in focus. This creates coherence across the year while allowing you to go deep on one idea at a time. (Source: Kira Federer, Episodes #254 and #184)

**Define and publish "red threads"—3-5 core truths to hammer across all marketing.** Give your team creative freedom to execute however they want, as long as they're hitting on these red threads. This maintains brand consistency and strategic focus while empowering teams to be creative without needing approval on every execution detail. (Source: Kady Srinivasan, Episode #276)

**Deliberately exclude certain words and language from your brand vocabulary.** Brand strategy includes deciding what NOT to say. Identify words or phrases that contradict your brand positioning and remove them from all communications. This forces you to find new language that aligns with your positioning and prevents defaulting to industry clichés. (Source: Ari Yablock, Episode #284)

**Prioritize one message per messaging element or sentence.** Avoid messaging bloat by ensuring each headline, value proposition, or sentence contains a single, clear message. When you list multiple attributes, prospects don't know which one to remember. Pick one and own it completely. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Apply the power of three to big-picture storytelling, not sentence-level messaging.** The power of three works for overarching narrative structure (keynote with three acts, brand story with three chapters), but should not be applied to headlines or individual sentences. When companies force three benefits into a single headline, they often end up with fluffy, generic messaging where the third item is included just to complete the pattern. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

## 3. Conduct Research to Inform Messaging

### Customer Research

**Follow a five-step messaging development process.** (1) Document and evaluate existing messaging to establish a benchmark and identify gaps; (2) Conduct voice of customer research; (3) Perform competitor messaging audit to identify white space; (4) Synthesize findings by manually reviewing transcripts, highlighting themes, and mapping priorities; (5) Document messaging blocks into a source of truth (one-pager, messaging hub) that teams can reference. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Base messaging on market signals and customer research, not internal brainstorming.** Start with customer research—speaking to customers, analyzing calls, looking for themes and patterns—rather than workshopping ideas internally. Even in the age of AI, manually review customer quotes and call transcripts to identify patterns. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Conduct customer interviews with a messaging lens, not a case study lens.** Dig into the challenges customers faced, triggers that made them look for a solution, benefits they experienced, and the value in their own words. This serves two purposes: it shapes your message hierarchy and their language directly informs how you write messaging, keeping you honest to buyer language rather than internal jargon. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Focus customer interviews on the problem state before they knew your solution existed.** Ask about what they were doing before, what pain points they faced, what goals they were trying to reach, and what solutions they considered. Avoid jumping immediately to how they use your product—that's the middle of the story, not the beginning. (Source: Shoshana Kordova, Episode #250)

**Use three specific techniques to extract deeper insights in customer interviews.** (1) Pause for 3-4 seconds after a customer's short answer—they will often elaborate unprompted. (2) Ask clarifying "why" questions that seem obvious to uncover the real underlying motivation. (3) Use the "so you can" exercise: for any benefit or pain point, ask "so you can [do what]?" to get to the deeper job the customer is trying to accomplish. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

**Ask specific survey questions to uncover emotional drivers.** Instead of "why did you choose us?", ask: (1) "What was going on in your life that made you search for a solution like this?" to uncover the emotional moment before purchase; (2) "If we took this product away tomorrow, what would you miss the most?" to identify your actual value proposition. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #231)

**Conduct structured customer interviews using three core questions.** Interview 50+ customers, board members, and employees using: (1) What problem do we solve? (2) Who do we solve it for? (3) What is the impact of solving that problem? Record and analyze responses in a spreadsheet to identify patterns, gaps, and opportunities for differentiation. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #206)

**Use customer conversations as a cheat code when you're not a subject matter expert.** When joining a company in an unfamiliar domain, conduct qualitative customer interviews focused on product-market fit questions (what was happening before, what did it unlock, what concerns did they have). Extract language, positioning, and messaging directly from customer responses to use in headlines, website copy, and marketing materials. (Source: Kevin White, Episodes #286 and #179)

**Apply customer insights across multiple deliverables, not just one.** After collecting insights, apply them to: case studies, positioning and messaging, website copy, sales battle cards, ICP refinement, and product roadmap influence. Insights compound in value when applied across the organization. (Source: Shoshana Kordova, Episode #250)

**Mine Reddit, LinkedIn, and review sites to uncover customer pains and desired outcomes.** Scroll organic conversations on Reddit and LinkedIn, and review G2 and Capterra to identify the top 3 pains and top 3 desired outcomes customers mention. Use these insights to identify messaging gaps on your website. This requires no approval and can be done immediately. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #231)

**Extract sticky copy and unusual angles from customer research.** During voice of customer research, actively listen for memorable, unusual, or distinctive ways customers describe your product. Create a swipe file of interesting customer quotes and phrases. Go beyond simply using customer words verbatim—look for the unusual angles and unexpected framings that differentiate how they think about your solution. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Build a messaging swipe file from real conversations.** Maintain a document where you screenshot and collect messaging that resonates in real conversations: LinkedIn comments, email replies, things people say in meetings, webinars, or events. When you notice people's heads nod or they light up at a particular phrase, capture it. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #139)

**Send cross-functional teams to industry conferences to absorb customer language.** Bring demand gen, operations, product, and other non-traditional event attendees to conferences so they can directly observe how customers speak about their problems. This firsthand exposure to customer language translates into better messaging and positioning across the organization. (Source: Sandra Rand, Episode #265)

### Sales Team Research

**Use sales team insights as a secondary source for messaging research.** When customer interviews aren't feasible, talk to your sales team. They run daily messaging tests through conversations and emails with prospects and have realistic, down-to-earth insights into what potential customers actually care about. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Leverage sales team insights and pipeline patterns to identify positioning problems.** Before positioning work, interview sales leadership about: (1) What objections come up repeatedly? (2) Who are we actually competing against? (3) What's the typical sales cycle length and why? (4) What causes deals to stall or be lost to no decision? (5) What's the pattern of product adoption? Use this data to validate positioning hypotheses. (Source: April Dunford, Episode #309)

**Refine positioning by observing how salespeople naturally articulate value.** Rather than expecting salespeople to use exact marketing-created messaging, listen to how they naturally communicate with prospects. If salespeople are not using your positioning as written, ask them why they're saying it differently instead of assuming they're wrong. Their real-world customer conversations often reveal gaps in your positioning. (Source: Jessica Andrews, Episode #217)

**Interview customer-facing teams for competitive positioning insights.** Conduct interviews with sales, customer success, support, and implementation teams to uncover patterns about why customers choose your product. Ask sales: "How do you know an inbound lead is a good fit?" Ask success/implementation: "How do you know an implementation will be successful?" (Source: Talya Heller, Episode #246)

**Validate your differentiator by asking sellers and buyers directly, not through surveys.** Have empathy sessions with your sales team and get on the phone with actual buyers. Ask sellers: why do people buy you over competitors? Ask buyers: what changed for you when you chose this solution? Phone conversations yield more fruitful insights than mass surveys. (Source: Kira Federer, Episodes #254 and #184)

### Competitive Research

**Perform a competitor messaging audit to identify white space.** Audit competitor messaging to understand what they're saying and identify what's left unsaid. This prevents me-too messaging and helps you find angles that are genuinely differentiated. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Validate market leadership claims with third-party research before making them public.** When claiming market leadership, hire a research agency to conduct independent research and validate the claim. This provides credibility and defensibility for your positioning, especially in competitive categories where multiple vendors claim leadership. (Source: Melton Littlepage, Episode #223)

## 4. Develop Specific Messaging Approaches

### Narrative and Story Structure

**Every story needs an enemy. Use three categories to identify yours.** There are only three types of enemy: (A) direct competitors—other companies solving the same problem; (B) competing approaches—alternative ways to solve the same problem; (C) beliefs you stand against—convictions rooted in your product that point back to your positioning. Ask: "What are we actually competing against?" This determines what you talk about in your story. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Name an enemy in your messaging; don't blame the customer.** Identify an external enemy or problem (e.g., forms, attribution tools, boring webinars) rather than blaming the customer for their current approach. This makes the customer feel understood rather than wrong. Drift's "no forms" positioning made the enemy the form itself, not the marketer using forms. The customer needs to understand why their current approach isn't working, but they also need to feel it's not their fault—it's the tools or systems they were given. *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree for the related debate on whether to lead with status quo cost vs. emotional trust vs. ROI.)* (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #344)

**Name the villain—the root cause of your buyer's problem—to clarify your flagship message.** Identify the villain: not your competitor, but the underlying cause of your buyers' problems. Examples: Loom's villain is time-sucking meetings; Cloud Zero's villain is unpredictable cloud billing. Naming the villain makes it easier for buyers to understand why your solution matters and creates a clear narrative anchor. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Write essays to clarify and develop your company's story.** Writing essays forces you to articulate your ideas clearly and discover what you actually believe about your category. You only know you have a good idea when you write it down. Essays serve as the foundation for all other marketing—they give you material to repurpose across homepages, ads, social posts, and sales decks. If you can't write an essay about your product or category, you don't have a story yet. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Start with the hook or headline, not the body copy.** When writing any marketing asset, begin by identifying and writing the hook or headline first. This becomes your north star. Work backwards and structure the rest of the content around it. The hook should answer: what's the one thing we want people to say or remember about this? (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Develop persona-specific strategic stories before attempting a company-wide narrative.** Start by developing clear, differentiated stories for specific buyer personas (e.g., HR, Finance, IT) before crafting an overarching company narrative. For each persona, define the strategic message. The company-wide story can evolve later as a synthesis of persona stories, rather than starting with a generic corporate message. (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

**Pair new positioning with sustained thought leadership across multiple channels over 6-12 months.** Positioning is not a one-time launch. Pair it with thought leadership (CEO presentations, keynotes, sales decks, product launches, content) that all reinforce the same narrative. Repeat the core message across multiple channels and events over 6-12 months. This repetition is what builds market awareness and internal belief in the positioning. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #254; Kira Federer, Episodes #254 and #184)

### Translating Features to Value

**Find the sweet spot between feature and pure value when explaining unique capabilities.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* When your best differentiator is a feature that buyers don't understand, don't just state the feature or jump to generic value. Find the middle ground: explain the feature in terms of what it enables the customer to do, then connect that to a concrete business outcome. Example: "Fuzzy logic algorithm" → "Fast queries on large data" → "Answer customer questions while they're on the phone instead of calling back in two days." (Source: April Dunford, Episode #309)

**Map product messaging along a spectrum from features to outcomes to identify the right zoom level.** Create a spectrum for your product that maps from specific features through intermediate benefit layers to high-level outcomes. Identify which layer resonates most with your target buyer and use that as your primary message, supporting it with proof and actions at other layers. Example for Toggl: feature (automated time categorization) → first benefit layer (know exactly where your team's time is going) → high-level outcome (optimize your team workflows). (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Use narrative distance to balance feature-level detail with high-level outcomes.** Apply the fiction-writing concept of narrative distance: zoom in on specific product details and capabilities, zoom out to high-level outcomes and benefits, and zoom all the way out to the broader context. Don't spend too long at any one layer. This prevents messaging from being either too technical (all zoomed in) or too generic (all zoomed out). (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Bundle features into value stories rather than feature-dumping.** Instead of listing all features individually, group related features into thematic blocks that tell a story about what the buyer can accomplish. Prioritize features by impact: major differentiators get their own block, related features bundle together, and minor features go in FAQs. (Source: Tas Bober, Episode #185)

**Document jobs-to-be-done as customer outcomes, not product features.** Create a list of 5-10 concrete outcomes or jobs your product helps customers accomplish, framed from the customer's perspective and business context—not as product capabilities. Examples: "prepare a quarterly business review deck for my CFO," "submit an annual financial plan." This document becomes an input for campaign ideas and ensures messaging focuses on customer value rather than feature lists. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #285)

### Emotional and Psychological Drivers

**Anchor messaging to five timeless customer motivation principles.** *(Note: the ROI element of this framework is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* The five principles are: (1) Make me look good—provide templates, frameworks, or ideas they can use immediately; (2) Save me time—prove time savings with data, stats, and testimonials, not just claims; (3) Make me money—show how your product drives revenue, reduces churn, or enables personal advancement; (4) Help me avoid pain—address fears customers secretly worry about and show a way out; (5) Help me reach the next level—speak to ambition and growth. Each principle requires proof and credibility; marketing claims alone are insufficient. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)

**Recognize that B2B buying decisions are highly emotional because the buyer's job is on the line.** B2B is more emotional than B2C because the stakes are higher for the individual decision-maker. A B2B buyer who makes a bad decision risks their job. Your marketing must make the buyer feel like a hero for choosing your solution. This emotional dimension should inform your positioning, messaging, and how you tell your brand story. (Source: Kimberly Storin, Episode #229)

**Leverage self-image and social-image as core emotional drivers in B2B messaging.** Identify and emphasize two primary emotional motivators: self-image (how the buyer wants to feel about themselves after purchase—confident, proud, capable) and social-image (how the buyer wants others to perceive them—knowledgeable, promoted, a go-to expert). Reflect these emotions in messaging, social proof, visuals, and value propositions rather than leading with features, pricing, or technology. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episodes #251, #231, and #132)

**Uncover emotional drivers through targeted customer interviews and surveys.** Ask specific questions about how customers felt before and after purchasing, what problems your solution eliminates, how they would describe you to a friend, and what they would miss if they could no longer use your product. This reveals the self-image and social-image emotions driving B2B purchase decisions. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

**Ground messaging in deep customer understanding of what motivates them and what pain they experience.** Invest in understanding your customers deeply—what motivates them, what pain they experience, what language they use to describe their problems. Use this understanding to craft messaging that speaks directly to their pain and shows how your solution addresses it. (Source: Megan Lueders, Episode #229)

### Outbound and Pipeline Messaging

**Remove "we" language from outbound messaging; focus on the prospect's problem and behavior.** Audit outbound emails and messaging for "we," "our," and company-name language. Instead, structure outbound to focus on the prospect's problem, how they currently solve it, and the unintended consequences of their current approach. Do not mention your product, company, or solution in the initial outbound message. The goal is to get the prospect thinking about their own behavior and situation before introducing your offering. *(Note: the question of what to lead with in outbound is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #308)

**Use a four-question framework to build status quo-busting outbound messaging.** Answer four questions collaboratively with sales, product, and leadership: (1) Who is most likely to have the problem our solution solves? (2) How do those people solve the problem today and why? (3) What is the unintended negative consequence of how they're solving it now? (4) Who else took a different approach? Use these answers to craft outbound that highlights the cost of staying the same and creates perception curiosity—an itch to scratch—rather than pitching features. *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #308)

**Target individual persona pain points rather than company-level challenges.** Focus on the specific pain points and concerns of the individual decision-maker, not the company as a whole. Understand what keeps that specific person up at night—their personal risk, reputation, or job security—and address that directly in your messaging. (Source: Alex Fine, Episode #245)

**Extract customer objections from sales calls and convert them into marketing messages.** Use ChatGPT to analyze sales call transcripts and identify the top 3-5 unresolved objections or hesitations prospects express. Then generate marketing hooks and messaging angles that directly address these objections, turning them into campaign themes. Examples: "We're new, so we work harder" or "Yes, we're a newer player—here's why that matters." *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree on AI's role in messaging.)* (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #285)

**Before scaling outbound, do foundational homework on persona, messaging, and pain points.** Deeply understand your target audience and personas, develop messaging that speaks to their specific pain points, and ensure your story and offer relate to people who care. Without this foundation, no amount of outbound activity will convert. This messaging and persona work must precede hiring or scaling outbound teams. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)

## 5. Write and Refine Copy

**Prioritize clarity over cleverness in B2B messaging.** Avoid trying to be exciting or convey innovation through big words and fancy phrases. This confuses people and causes them to move on. The temptation to use abstract, sophisticated language to seem innovative actually undermines messaging effectiveness. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Convert abstract language to concrete specifics.** Replace abstract words (especially those ending in "-tion" like "operationalization" or phrases like "strategic solution") with concrete, specific language that buyers can visualize. Test by asking: can you picture this in your mind? If multiple people picture different things, it's too abstract. For example, instead of "real time visibility," say "now you can see all your financials from different accounts in one place." (Source: Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Rephrase generic claims with specific, concrete language.** Replace overused B2B phrases with specific, concrete alternatives. Instead of "easy to set up," say "you'll be up and running in nine minutes." Instead of "seamless integration," say "you don't have to alter your workflows—we work with the tools you're already using." Specificity makes claims feel more credible and memorable. (Source: Tas Bober, Episode #185)

**Apply the "so what" test to every line of copy.** For every line of copy, ask "so what?" to identify whether you're describing a feature or translating it into a benefit for the user. Replace company-centric language ("we built," "we've packaged") with user-focused language ("you can," "you'll get"). (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Use simple, conversational language instead of marketing jargon.** Write website copy in plain language that describes what you actually do, not what you claim to do. Instead of "Accelerate pipeline 10x," say "Get warm intros" or "Connect prospects with customers." Avoid industry jargon and overstatement. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

**Accept that clarity requires the risk of offending some people.** When writing marketing copy, you will inevitably offend or disappoint some stakeholders by being clear and direct. This is the cost of clarity. Don't try to please everyone or include every feature/benefit on a page just to avoid hurting feelings. Clarity beats consensus. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Avoid making unbelievable or disconnected value claims.** Do not make value arguments that don't logically connect to the product's core function. For example, claiming that monitoring software "increases workplace productivity" is unbelievable and weakens credibility. Stick to value claims that directly flow from what the product does. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Use technical language for technical buyers; use business language for economic buyers.** Match your messaging language to your buyer type. Technical buyers (engineers, product managers) need specific technical details and terminology—don't dumb it down. Economic buyers (executives, CMOs) need business-focused language. Ideally, create two versions of messaging: one for technical audiences and one for business decision-makers. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Leverage founder or leader personal brand and perspective as a copy advantage.** A founder or leader with a strong public voice and distinct perspective on the industry creates an unfair advantage for web copy and marketing. When prospects encounter your website, they may already be familiar with your founder's content from other channels. The website can play off the founder's established voice rather than starting from scratch. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

**Earn attention first, then transition to product messaging.** The marketing sequence should be: (1) run a play designed purely to get attention and stand out, without leading with product features or benefits; (2) once you have attention, use that attention to tell your product story. Most people are not actively shopping, so they will not find generic product messaging interesting. Attention-first plays create the initial hook; product messaging comes after you've earned mindshare. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #316)

## 6. Homepage and Website Messaging

**Recognize that your homepage cannot serve all audiences and products equally.** When you have multiple products or serve multiple buyer personas, your homepage's job is not to explain everything—it's to funnel people to the right destination quickly. Find either a core problem you solve across all products or a through-line value proposition, then use the homepage to route people efficiently to product-specific or persona-specific pages. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Establish a clear, core company message before attempting to tailor messaging to specific personas.** If a company cannot articulate a single, clear value proposition without diving into persona-specific details, it has a positioning problem that needs to be solved first. Develop a core message that explains what the company does at a fundamental level, then layer persona-specific messaging on top through navigation, landing pages, or secondary sections. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Identify the shared pain point that overlaps across multiple personas to anchor homepage messaging.** Rather than trying to speak to each persona separately on the homepage, find the common problem or friction point that all personas experience. Build the homepage value proposition around this overlap, then use navigation and landing pages to direct different personas to more specific information. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Use dedicated landing pages for specific personas rather than trying to serve all audiences on the homepage.** The homepage should serve as a general entry point with a clear, unified value proposition. For specific personas or use cases, create separate landing pages with tailored messaging. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**When the homepage cannot serve multiple audiences well, create a separate offering or homepage for the underserved segment.** If your company serves audiences with significantly different needs, budgets, or use cases (e.g., individual buyers vs. enterprise), consider creating a separate offering (like Fiverr Pro) with its own homepage and messaging. (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Structure website navigation with a dedicated Solutions section that segments by persona or use case.** Each solution page should contain messaging, copy, and value propositions tailored to that specific persona, allowing visitors to self-select into relevant messaging without requiring complex personalization logic. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #173 and #155)

**Add a problem block immediately below the hero section.** Place a "problem block" directly under your hero section that empathizes with the prospect's pain points. List 3-5 specific problems your audience faces. This mirrors the prospect's situation back to them, builds rapport, and signals that the page is about solving their problems, not promoting your company. (Source: Tas Bober, Episode #185)

**Ensure the first fold contains a clear, user-centric value proposition in one bold sentence.** The first fold is your only guaranteed impression before users bounce. Make it count with a single, bold statement that clearly communicates what you do for the user (not what you are). Avoid feature placement or company-centric language. *(Note: what exactly belongs in the hero section is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* (Source: Lee Reshef, Episode #220)

**Restate product positioning on the pricing page with per-plan positioning.** Many visitors arrive at the pricing page directly via search or navigation without seeing your homepage. Restate your core positioning at the top of the pricing page. Additionally, include a positioning statement for each plan—a sentence or two under the plan name explaining what's included, who it's for, and why they might need it. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

**Lead the pricing page heading with your value proposition, not a generic label.** Replace generic pricing page headings like "Pricing" or "Pick the Plan for You" with your actual value proposition. The heading is one of the hardest-working pages on your website; use it to communicate what the product does and who it's for. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

**Use descriptive package names that hint at use case or audience.** Move beyond generic tier names like "Basic," "Pro," and "Enterprise." Create package names that hint at who the package is for or what job it does. Combine this with a subheading underneath each package name to reinforce the target audience and what they'll be able to do. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

**Address buyer's existing expectations, assumptions, and competitive alternatives in your messaging.** Recognize that buyers come to your site with "baggage"—expectations based on past experiences, assumptions about your category, and knowledge of competitive alternatives. Identify what buyers are actually switching from (not just competing products, but spreadsheets, manual processes, or nothing) and frame your message around that specific alternative. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Tailor messaging to the buyer's stage of awareness.** Adjust your messaging based on where your buyer sits in the awareness spectrum. Problem-aware buyers need you to highlight the problem and show how your solution addresses it. Solution-aware buyers need positioning within the category and proof of value. Product-aware buyers need differentiation and competitive comparison. The stage of awareness shapes what messages you prioritize, how detailed they are, and the order they appear on the page. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

## 7. Product Page Messaging

**Clarify whether your product page is a homepage for the product or a deep-dive feature page, then structure accordingly.** Homepage-style pages should emphasize positioning and key value props; deep-dive pages should focus on feature discovery, integrations, and use-case details. Testimonials are more important on homepage-style pages; less critical on deep-dive pages. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Prominently feature your key differentiator throughout the product page, not just once.** If your product has a clear differentiator (e.g., "built on ServiceNow"), make it a central theme throughout the page rather than mentioning it only once or burying it. Repeat and reinforce the differentiator in multiple sections. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Focus product pages on showing your product, not on comparing against competitors.** Do not worry about competitors seeing your product UI or features on your product page. Competitors can easily access your product through other means. Instead, focus on clearly showing your product and highlighting your differentiation. Transparency about your product builds trust with prospects. (Source: Robert Kaminski, Episode #222)

**Build product launches around buyer problems, not product features.** Start product launch planning by identifying the specific buyer problem you're solving, not the product itself. Use a one-sentence framework: "How can [target audience] [desired outcome] by [your solution]?" This makes it easier to get sales alignment because you're leading with buyer relevance, not product specs. (Source: Zach Roberts, Episode #163)

**Bundle related features together to create a cohesive launch story.** Before launching individual features, evaluate whether multiple features can be bundled together to tell a stronger, more cohesive story. The bundled story should address a specific pain point or challenge rather than just being a collection of features. (Source: Natalie Taylor, Episode #163)

**Package daily product releases into larger narrative stories for enterprise audiences.** Product teams ship frequently, but enterprise buyers need packaged stories to understand value. Rather than launching every feature as it ships, bundle releases into larger narratives with clear messaging about why each feature matters. Self-serve/PLG audiences want features immediately, but enterprise audiences need context, education, and a coherent story. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #270)

**Use distinct messaging strategies for PLG and SLG audiences.** PLG audiences (self-serve users, creators, enthusiasts) respond to feature-based selling and want new capabilities immediately. SLG audiences (enterprise buyers) need education, value stories, and use-case-based messaging that explains the "why" behind features. Tailor messaging, content, and launch cadence to each audience's needs. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #270)

## 8. Organizational Alignment and Execution

**Require founder and C-suite involvement in messaging development.** Messaging is a leadership problem, not just a marketing problem. Do not develop messaging in isolation with the marketing team and then try to roll it out. Require the founder, CEO, head of sales, customer success, and other key stakeholders to be actively involved. Without internal alignment and buy-in from leadership, messaging will lack consistency across the organization. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Partner with sales leadership first when repositioning, before CEO or product.** When repositioning a company, prioritize alignment with the VP of Sales or sales leader before seeking CEO or product approval. Sales is closest to customer objections and win/loss data, and their buy-in is critical for execution. Position the repositioning effort as a service to sales, not as a marketing initiative. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Show positioning work early and iteratively to avoid rejection after weeks of hidden development.** When developing new positioning, share early drafts and work-in-progress versions with stakeholders rather than disappearing for weeks and returning with a finished product. Early visibility prevents the perception that positioning was developed in isolation and gives stakeholders a sense of involvement. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**Facilitate positioning workshops with product marketing and sales together.** Use an external facilitator to bring product marketing and sales together to define true differentiators. Start by mapping what customers actually care about, then work backward to identify the beliefs and assumptions you can challenge. This prevents the loudest voice from winning. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episodes #232 and #170)

**Prioritize consistent execution of a good message over perfect messaging that no one uses.** A pretty good message that the entire company uses consistently is more valuable than incredible messaging that sits unused. Focus on getting organizational alignment around a core narrative so that every interaction reinforces the same message. (Source: Kira Federer, Episodes #254 and #184)

**Repeat core messaging across channels using a political campaign model.** Treat marketing messaging like a political campaign: the stickier and more repeated your message, the more the market will be aware of it and the better your sales team will tell that story. Audit all customer touchpoints (ads, SDR outreach, AE calls, CSM interactions, executive communications) to ensure messaging consistency. By the time internal teams are tired of repeating a message, external audiences are just beginning to absorb it. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #123)

**Prioritize internal marketing and sales enablement over external campaigns.** Invest as much or more effort in internal marketing (communicating strategy, messaging, and enablement materials to sales) as you do in external marketing campaigns. Before launching external campaigns, ensure sales understands the messaging, has the right materials, and is aligned on the strategy. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #123)

**Build champion-focused internal selling assets, not generic sales decks.** Create assets specifically designed to help your champion sell internally to their buying committee (CFO, CTO, COO, etc.), rather than generic sales decks. Tailor assets by use case and buyer persona, using their language and KPIs. These assets should address the champion's specific job of justifying the purchase internally. (Source: Talya Heller, Episode #246)

**Build a centralized messaging and content repository for campaign consistency.** Create a single source of truth for all campaign messaging, content, and assets. This allows the team to move faster during pivots, ensures consistency across channels, and prevents duplicate work. (Source: Jessica Skovira, Episode #210)

**Commit to "boring" marketing—consistent, proven messaging and tactics—rather than constantly changing campaigns.** Research across 100 companies shows that successful GTM strategies rely on consistency and repetition of proven messaging, not constant creative reinvention. Examples: Chick-fil-A kept the same cow campaign for decades; Salesforce kept "Dreamforce"; HubSpot kept "Inbound." The market likes what works. Resist internal pressure to change messaging for the sake of novelty. (Source: Sangram Vajre, Episode #299)

## 9. Testing and Validating Messaging

**Test messaging with small initial audiences before scaling.** Start with small live events, limited social media audiences, or small-scale campaigns to test messaging, positioning, and creative approaches before investing heavily. Even with 10-17 people at an event, you can gather feedback on what resonates, refine your pitch, and identify winning analogies or frameworks. (Source: Chris Walker, Episodes #281 and #211)

**Use paid ads to test messaging hypotheses across new geographic markets before full investment.** Paid ads are a fast way to learn about new markets and validate messaging. When expanding to a new geography, run small ad tests with your standard messaging first. If performance is poor, use that signal to test alternative messaging hypotheses. (Source: Domi de Saint-Exupéry, Episode #332)

**Test messaging with target customers before launching paid campaigns.** Before spending money on paid ads or campaigns, validate your messaging with your target audience using message testing. This prevents wasting ad budget on messaging that doesn't resonate. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Test messaging using wireframes or Google Docs, not final designs, to avoid brand bias.** When testing messaging, remove logos and design elements to prevent respondents from being biased by brand recognition. Use basic wireframes, Google Docs, or Balsamiq mockups instead of polished designs. This ensures feedback is about the copy and messaging, not the brand or visual design. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use message testing data to resolve internal disagreements about copy and messaging.** When internal stakeholders disagree on headlines, value propositions, or copy, run a message test with your target audience instead of debating subjectively. Let data from actual target customers settle the disagreement. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use Wynter for B2B-specific messaging validation.** Wynter (wynter.com) is a B2B-specific messaging testing tool that provides immediate feedback on whether your messaging resonates. While on the higher price end, it validates messaging, value propositions, homepage copy, and navigation with your actual target buyer segment. (Source: Haley Carpenter, Episode #282; Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Evaluate website messaging across five components: clarity, relevance, value, differentiation, and brand perception.** (1) Clarity—do visitors understand what you do? (2) Relevance—do you address their priorities? (3) Value—is the value proposition clear? (4) Differentiation—why choose you over competitors? (5) Brand perception—does tone match your brand? This framework enables objective discussion about copy instead of subjective preference debates. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use social media posts as a message testing feedback loop.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* Treat LinkedIn posts as a low-cost way to test messaging and hooks before committing to major announcements or campaigns. Post ideas, angles, or talking points and observe which ones resonate. When a post performs well, use that validated messaging as the foundation for your next product announcement or campaign. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

## 10. Rebrand and Repositioning Messaging

**Frame a rebrand as infrastructure, not a marketing initiative.** When pitching a rebrand to the CEO, frame it as infrastructure that touches every department and every customer touchpoint—from recruiting materials to quarterly business reviews. For financially-minded CEOs, quantify the cost of inconsistent messaging across departments as business leakage. (Source: Clare Schmitt, Episode #333)

**Separate category training from brand training when rebranding and repositioning.** If you are simultaneously changing both category positioning and brand identity, conduct separate training sessions for each. Category training covers the new industry/competitive landscape and value proposition; brand training covers the new name, visual identity, and messaging. Separating these prevents confusion and allows you to measure the impact of each initiative independently. (Source: Clare Schmitt, Episode #333)

**Use a brand positioning spectrum exercise to define what you are NOT.** Create a spectrum chart plotting opposing brand attributes (e.g., "social vs. personal," "community-focused vs. individual-focused") and have your executive team place your brand on each spectrum. This reveals disconnects between how different leaders perceive the brand and forces clarity on positioning. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episodes #244 and #158)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Can AI be used effectively for core messaging strategy, or only for copywriting execution?

**Support summary: 3 vs 2**

**Position A: AI should not be used to generate core positioning or messaging strategy.**
Diane Wiredu (Episode #226) explicitly stated that prompting AI to figure out your messaging based on existing messaging datasets produces only generic output, since the AI is trained on what already exists. The strategic thinking—understanding what makes you different, what your customers need, what angle to take—must come from humans; AI executes faster once that thinking is done. Emma Stratton (Episodes #295 and #234) distinguished messaging (strategic first manifestation of positioning requiring decisions about what to stand for) from copywriting (wordsmithing), and stated AI is currently good at copywriting but not at messaging because messaging requires strategic decisions that can't be automated.

**Position B: AI can be actively used in the messaging development process when trained on the right inputs.**
Pranav Piyush (Episode #285) recommended using ChatGPT to analyze sales call transcripts, identify top unresolved objections, and generate marketing hooks and messaging angles that directly address those objections—framing this as a legitimate messaging development technique, not just copy execution. Anton Ruis (Episode #278) described building a custom GPT trained on company messaging frameworks that generates persona-specific buyer briefs including market context, internal pressures, emotional triggers, and proprietary messaging frameworks—going well beyond copy execution into strategic messaging territory. John Short (Episode #271) recommended building a custom GPT trained on customer call transcripts and feedback to test messaging, simulate objections, and get feedback on website copy.

**Context dependency:** Partially context-dependent. The "AI only for copy" camp may be referring to using generic AI prompts without proprietary data, while the "AI for messaging inputs" camp is describing AI trained on company-specific customer data and frameworks. If you account for this distinction, the disagreement partially dissolves—both sides might agree that generic AI prompts produce generic output. However, Pranav Piyush's use of ChatGPT on call transcripts to generate messaging angles is a genuine tactical disagreement with Diane Wiredu's position.

**Trend note:** The "AI useful for messaging inputs" position comes entirely from more recent episodes (2025), while the "AI only for copy" position also appears in 2025. No clear chronological divergence.

**Why it matters:** If AI can legitimately accelerate messaging development when fed the right inputs, teams that avoid it are leaving efficiency on the table; if it only produces generic output, teams using it for strategy are building on a weak foundation.

---

### 2. Should your hero section lead with a specific capability/feature or a high-level outcome?

**Support summary: 3 vs 2 vs 1**

**Position A: Lead with a specific capability.**
Robert Kaminski (Episode #222) explicitly recommended leading with a clear, specific capability in the hero section rather than a vague outcome, using "Advanced monitoring and reporting for Microsoft 365" as a positive example versus "Boost engagement" as a negative example. Peep Laja (Episode #119) recommended leading with category definition (what is it?) so visitors immediately understand what you do, prioritizing clarity about the product over emotional or outcome-based framing.

**Position B: Lead with the emotional pain point and outcome.**
Ryan Narod (Episode #330) argued that mass-market creative (e.g., Super Bowl ads) should abstract away from product-specific messaging to focus on emotional pain points and aspirations, making the message universally resonant. Talia Wolf (Episode #251) argued that B2B homepages focusing on features cause all competitors to look the same; instead, lead with what the customer is struggling with and what they've tried that hasn't worked, with features supporting the narrative rather than leading it. Lee Reshef (Episode #220) recommended that the first fold contain a single bold statement communicating what you do for the user (not what you are), focused on what's in it for the user and what problem you solve.

**Position C: Find the middle ground between feature and outcome.**
April Dunford (Episode #309) described a three-step translation: feature (fuzzy logic algorithm) → what it enables (fast queries on large data) → concrete business outcome (answer customer questions while they're on the phone instead of calling back in two days). She explicitly argued against both pure feature language and jumping to generic value.

**Context dependency:** Significantly context-dependent. Ryan Narod is explicitly discussing mass-market brand awareness creative (Super Bowl ads), while Robert Kaminski is discussing product pages for B2B software. These are different contexts. However, Talia Wolf's advice about B2B homepages directly conflicts with Robert Kaminski's product page advice in the same B2B context. April Dunford's middle-ground position applies broadly. The Kaminski vs. Wolf tension is a genuine disagreement for B2B product/homepage copy.

**Why it matters:** The choice between leading with capability versus outcome in your hero section directly affects whether visitors immediately understand what you do versus whether they emotionally connect with the problem—getting this wrong can increase bounce rates or reduce qualified engagement.

---

### 3. Should you use social media to test and discover core messaging, or validate messaging through customer conversations first?

**Support summary: 2 vs 1**

**Position A: Social media is an effective, low-cost messaging test channel.**
Dave Gerhardt (Episode #275) recommended treating LinkedIn posts as a low-cost way to test messaging and hooks before committing to major announcements, using engagement signals to identify winning messaging to scale. Peep Laja (Episode #119) recommended posting frequently on Twitter to test which messaging ideas resonate, then expanding successful ones to LinkedIn, framing Twitter as a rapid messaging test environment.

**Position B: Validate messaging through customer conversations, not social media.**
Chris Walker (Episode #139) explicitly distinguished messaging (core value proposition validated through customer conversations, sales testing, surveys) from content (how you deliver that message). He stated you should not use social media to discover your core messaging—only to test content formats after messaging is validated.

**Context dependency:** Partially context-dependent. Chris Walker may be drawing a distinction between discovering foundational positioning (which requires customer conversations) versus testing message variations and hooks (which social media can inform). Dave Gerhardt and Peep Laja may be referring to the latter. However, Dave Gerhardt's framing of using social posts to find "the foundation for your next product announcement" suggests he's using social for more than just format testing, creating a genuine disagreement with Walker's position.

**Why it matters:** If you use social media engagement as a proxy for messaging validity, you may optimize for what gets likes rather than what converts buyers—understanding the right validation channel for messaging could prevent building campaigns on a misleading signal.

---

### 4. Should outbound messaging lead with ROI or the cost of staying the same?

**Support summary: 3 vs 1**

**Position A: Lead with the cost of staying the same.**
Jen Allen-Knuth (Episodes #343 and #308) explicitly argued that ROI is inherently high-risk messaging because it's a "maybe"—prospects must first perceive a negative consequence to their current state before ROI becomes relevant. She developed a four-question framework specifically designed to highlight the cost of staying the same, not ROI, stating that prospects are already solving their problem with existing methods and need to understand why staying with that method is costly before ROI becomes relevant. Dave Gerhardt (Episode #311) listed "Make me money—show how your product drives revenue, reduces churn, or enables personal advancement" as one of five timeless customer motivation principles, positioning ROI as a primary messaging lever—though this is in the context of general marketing messaging rather than outbound specifically.

**Position B: Lead with emotional trust; use ROI to justify decisions already made.**
Melton Littlepage (Episode #223) argued that enterprise buyers make decisions with their heart (emotion, trust, confidence) and then justify with their head (ROI analysis). He recommended leading with emotional appeals—brand trust, confidence that the solution will work and won't jeopardize their career—rather than ROI or feature comparisons, especially in security buying where risk aversion is high.

**Context dependency:** Partially context-dependent. Jen Allen-Knuth is explicitly discussing outbound pipeline generation messaging, while Melton Littlepage is discussing enterprise brand marketing and sponsorships. The disagreement partially dissolves when you separate outbound cold messaging (where status quo cost applies) from brand/awareness marketing (where emotional trust applies). However, both are making claims about what should come before ROI messaging, which creates a genuine tension in how to sequence messaging priorities.

**Why it matters:** Choosing the wrong lead message in outbound can result in prospects dismissing your pitch before engaging; understanding whether to lead with status quo pain or emotional trust could meaningfully change pipeline conversion rates.

## What NOT To Do

**Don't lead with generic, high-level benefit language that applies to all competitors.** Don't lead with vague, universally applicable benefits like "save time," "increase revenue," "become more productive," "fast actionable insights," or "free your team." These phrases apply to nearly every product in a category and create the generic messaging problem. Instead, identify the specific, intermediate benefit layer that's unique to your solution and your buyer's actual workflow. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #300)

**Don't use AI to generate core positioning from scratch.** Prompting an AI to figure out your messaging based on existing messaging datasets will only produce generic output, since the AI is trained on what already exists. The strategic thinking must come from you and your team; AI is a tool to execute faster once that thinking is done. *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Don't force messaging into rigid category formulas.** Resist the temptation to fit your messaging into popular formulas like "the [category] for [audience]." This formula only works when you serve a truly specific, well-defined subset of the market with a clear category. Most B2B tech products solve problems across multiple user profiles and compete with various alternatives. Forcing a rigid formula obscures your real differentiation. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Don't develop messaging in isolation and then try to roll it out.** Messaging developed without founder, CEO, head of sales, and customer success involvement will lack consistency across the organization. Teams will continue to tell different stories. Messaging is the embodiment of product strategy—if the go-to-market or product team isn't aligned on what the product is and who it's for, messaging work will be difficult. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Don't try to please everyone with vague copy.** When internal stakeholders have conflicting priorities, the temptation is to write vague copy that tries to make everyone happy. This results in messaging that says nothing specific. Be specific about what you do, who you're for, and what problem you solve. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Don't use umbrella terms like "digital transformation" to hide behind vague enterprise messaging.** Enterprise messaging often uses umbrella terms that sound important but mean nothing specific. Instead, be concrete about what you solve. Enterprise buyers are still humans who need clarity and relevance. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Don't use "we-centric" language in outbound messaging.** Most cold outbound is dominated by self-referential messaging ("we help," "we revolutionized," "our solution"). This fails to break through status quo. Structure outbound to focus on the prospect's problem, how they currently solve it, and the unintended consequences of their current approach. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #308)

**Don't lead with product features in outbound; lead with the prospect's problem.** Prospects are already solving their problem with existing methods. They need to understand why staying with that method is costly before your product becomes relevant. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episodes #308 and #343)

**Don't include logos or testimonials just because they're from well-known companies.** Choose social proof that reflects your actual ICP. If your target is mid-market SaaS companies, a testimonial from a Fortune 500 company may not resonate or be credible to your prospects. Testimonials should specifically address the problems your ICP faces. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #231)

**Don't launch every product feature as it ships for enterprise audiences.** Enterprise buyers need packaged stories to understand value. Rather than launching features on a staggered timeline, bundle releases into larger narratives with clear messaging about why each feature matters. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #270)

**Don't use the word "webinar" in promotional materials.** A/B test shows that calling a webinar a "live panel," "live insider session," or similar alternative outperforms the word "webinar" by approximately 35% in registration rates. Apply this naming change across all promotional materials, subject lines, and marketing copy. (Source: Jay Schwedelson, Episode #329)

**Don't skip the messaging foundation and jump to advanced tactics like personalization.** Before implementing advanced tactics, ensure the foundation is solid: clear messaging, clear value proposition, clear journey, and sufficient social proof. If the foundation is cracked, advanced tactics will fail. (Source: Haley Carpenter, Episode #282)

**Don't use placeholder copy formulas as finished copy.** Formulas like "Product X for Persona Y" are starting points, not finished copy. They should only appear on the page if both the product category and persona are extremely clear and specific. Otherwise, push beyond the formula to create messaging that actually resonates and builds curiosity. Avoid vague qualifiers like "modern" or "people-centric" that don't differentiate. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #220)

**Don't change messaging for the sake of novelty.** Resist internal pressure to change messaging just to look good or try something new. Consistency and repetition of proven messaging is what drives business outcomes. (Source: Sangram Vajre, Episode #299)

**Don't expect copy to create differentiation that doesn't exist in the product.** Copy cannot create differentiation that doesn't exist in the product. If your product is a "me-too" offering with no meaningful difference from competitors, even excellent copy will struggle. Before investing heavily in copy, ensure the product has genuine differentiation. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)
