# Thought Leadership Content

## Overview
This skill covers B2B thought leadership content strategy: how to build authority, develop ownable ideas, choose the right formats and channels, and sustain a content engine that drives market perception and demand. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast. No general best practices have been added beyond what guests explicitly recommended.

---

## Developing Your Core Idea and Point of View

**Identify and name a specific problem, not a category.**
Rather than trying to create or own a new category name, identify and name a specific problem your target audience experiences. Examples: "revenue leak," "go-to-market bloat," "the great ignore," "evidence gap." This is called a "conceptual scoop"—you observe a trend or problem and give it a name that resonates with your ICP. This approach is more defensible and memorable than category creation, and it gives your audience language to describe their own pain. (Source: Brendan Hufford, Episode #242)

**Pick one word to own.**
Identify a single word that represents what you want to be known for in your market. This word serves as your North Star internally (keeping content focused) and externally (carving out mindshare). The word should trigger mentions in closed-door conversations where prospects discuss solutions to their problems. Ensure it meets three criteria: your audience cares about it, you have expertise in it, and you sell it. Once chosen, commit to executing your content strategy around that word consistently. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Develop 2–3 conviction-backed positions.**
Identify 2–3 specific hills you will die on within your niche and articulate them with conviction. These should be contrarian but grounded in your experience—not contrarian for shock value. Communicate these views publicly (podcasts, LinkedIn) to become a point of reference for prospects who share those beliefs, making you their champion's champion. (Source: Lashay Lewis, Episode #192)

**State the obvious publicly and repeatedly.**
Ideas that seem obvious to you often represent a competitive advantage because few people have the courage or clarity to state them publicly. If something feels obvious to you, it's likely because you've thought deeply about it—but most people haven't. Stating the obvious clearly and repeatedly can differentiate you in a crowded market. Don't dismiss an idea as too simple or well-known; if you're the one saying it consistently and with conviction, it becomes associated with you. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #344)

**Prioritize authenticity over sounding smart.**
When developing thought leadership content, draw from your real experience and what you've actually done, rather than trying to sound knowledgeable or include industry best practices you haven't personally tested. Audiences respond to authentic stories and genuine insights far more than to polished expertise. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

## Content Frameworks and Models

**Use the Authority Flywheel.**
Build a repeatable content loop that reinforces your ownable idea by consistently addressing four elements: (1) the problem you're solving, (2) your point of view on that problem, (3) the big promise or outcome people will get from working with you or using your solution, and (4) proof or evidence that your approach works. Repeat this cycle across all content and channels. This creates momentum and allows you to say the same thing in a thousand different ways without sounding repetitive, because each piece of content hits different angles of the same core idea. (Source: Katelyn Bourgoin, Episode #344)

**Apply the Chocolate-Covered Almond model.**
Create content that balances nutrient density (valuable, educational information) with taste (entertainment, engagement, and shareability). Ensure that educational or dense value content is wrapped in engaging, entertaining formats. This prevents content from being purely transactional or purely entertainment, making it more likely to be consumed, shared, and remembered. Particularly effective for thought leadership and brand-building content. (Source: Katelyn Bourgoin, Episode #344)

**Source topics from research, real conversations, and observed pain points.**
Build thought leadership content by combining three sources: (1) research on workplace trends and emerging topics (using outlets like Morning Brew, industry studies), (2) direct conversations with employees and people outside the company experiencing specific challenges, and (3) real-life observations and pain points. This ensures content is timely, relatable, and grounded in actual problems people face, rather than speculative or generic. (Source: Arielle Gordis, Episode #166)

## Sustaining a Content Engine

**Pair positioning with sustained thought leadership over 6–12 months.**
Positioning is not a one-time launch. Pair your new positioning with thought leadership (CEO presentations, keynotes, sales decks, product launches, content) that all reinforce the same narrative. Repeat the core message across multiple channels and events over 6–12 months. This repetition is what builds market awareness and internal belief in the positioning. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #254 and #184)

**Establish a weekly subject matter expert interview cadence.**
Schedule recurring interviews (weekly or twice weekly) with internal subject matter experts—executives, solutions engineers, product leaders, or power users—to capture their insights on your category, problems, and solutions. Record and transcribe these interviews, then use AI workflows to convert transcripts into blog posts, social content, and promotional materials. This creates a consistent, scalable source of authentic thought leadership while training internal experts on messaging and category language. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #206)

**Use podcast recording and re-listening as a thinking and refinement tool.**
Record long-form podcast content (20–30 minutes uninterrupted) as a way to externalize and test ideas in real time. Then re-listen to your own recordings to identify strong points, refine language, spot product positioning insights, and cement ideas into repeatable frameworks. Allocate approximately 3 hours per week for this practice. (Source: Chris Walker, Episodes #281 and #211)

**Publish original research on a fixed cadence.**
Establish a regular cadence (e.g., weekly on Tuesdays) for publishing original research reports on topics relevant to your market. Each report should include: sample size, customer profile, sales cycle length, methodology, and specific metrics. Expect controversy and disagreement—this drives engagement and conversation. Use the reports to support product decisions and establish your company as a data-driven authority. Retarget report viewers with ads to extend reach. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Build authority before launching an agency or consultancy.**
You don't need to quit your job and immediately start an agency. Instead, build your methodology and authority while employed elsewhere. Write about your approach, develop a point of view, and create content that demonstrates your expertise. This can start as a course or digital product before evolving into an agency or consultancy. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

## Audience Building and Channel Strategy

**Become the go-to resource for your niche.**
Rather than promoting your product directly, position your brand as the expert resource for a specific audience segment. Your entire content strategy—across search, social, email, and other channels—should answer the questions and solve the problems that audience faces. This builds trust and affinity before any sales conversation. When you eventually have something to sell, that audience already knows and trusts you. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #209 and #121)

**Pursue SERP domination and mindshare domination as dual goals.**
SERP domination means owning multiple positions on Google's search results page for keywords relevant to your industry (blog posts, YouTube videos, Twitter threads, social accounts all ranking for the same terms). Mindshare domination means becoming the go-to resource people turn to when they have a problem in your space—before they even search. Pursue both by creating content that answers common questions and distributing it across multiple channels. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #209)

**Invest heavily in enablement content for non-customers.**
Spend more time and resources creating educational content and enablement for people who are not yet customers than for existing customers. The goal is to educate the broader market on how to use tools and solve problems in your space, positioning your company as a trusted expert. This builds trust, generates organic word-of-mouth referrals, and creates a strong foundation of goodwill. When these educated prospects eventually need a solution, your company will be top-of-mind because you've already helped them succeed. (Source: Eoin Clancy, Episode #326)

**Build brand through customer education and career advancement support.**
Build brand affinity by creating content that makes your customers smarter and helps them succeed at their jobs, independent of whether they buy from you. When customers associate your brand with education and value that helped them advance their career or solve problems, they develop long-term loyalty and affinity. This can take years to convert to a sale, but the brand association is durable and powerful. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Position brand and thought leadership as the primary go-to-market strategy.**
Shift from treating brand marketing as a separate, discretionary function to making it the core marketing strategy. SEO and paid ads become supporting channels that feed the audience-building engine, not the primary demand drivers. This requires organizational alignment: the entire marketing team executes around content, thought leadership, and audience growth as the main lever for demand generation. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145)

**Continue nurturing prospects during buying slowdowns.**
When buying slows due to budget freezes or economic conditions, maintain relationship-building through content and thought leadership rather than aggressive sales tactics. Prospects may not buy today due to external constraints, not lack of interest. Position your company as a trusted resource during difficult times; when budgets reopen, you'll be top-of-mind because you stayed present and helpful. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145)

## Executive and Founder Voice

**Attribute all content to a specific person and write from their perspective.**
Stop publishing generic, company-attributed content. Every piece of content—articles, videos, editorials—should be attributed to a specific person on your team and written from their perspective. Include personal anecdotes or experiences that connect to the marketing lesson or insight. The story or anecdote is the hook that gets people to read; the tactical insight is what they stay for. (Source: Sylvia LePoidevin, Episode #306)

**Develop founder or executive thought leadership on LinkedIn if your customers are on the platform.**
If your target customers are on LinkedIn, invest in founder or executive thought leadership on the platform. Founders have authenticity and interesting things to say. Help them humanize their voice and post regularly. If your customers are not on LinkedIn or you operate in a vertical with strong trade media, prioritize PR and trade media instead. (Source: Ido Mart, Episode #229)

**If the CEO lacks a compelling founding story, position them as an industry curator.**
If your CEO or executive doesn't have a compelling founding story or personal narrative, take a curator path instead of a creator path. Position them as the go-to source for industry news, insights, and trends. They become known for aggregating and commenting on what's happening in their industry, rather than sharing personal origin stories. Start by reading articles, surfacing stats, and sharing strong points of view about industry direction. Over time, this curator role can help them develop a voice and become a source of meetings for the industry. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #196)

**Share what you're learning and observing rather than positioning yourself as an expert.**
When starting on LinkedIn, avoid faking expertise or positioning yourself as an authority in areas where you lack real experience. Instead, share what you're actually learning in your current role, analyze case studies of companies doing interesting work, or document your observations without claiming to have all the answers. This builds authentic relationships and attracts both people behind you (learning from your journey) and people ahead of you (who respect your honesty). (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Write case study-style posts analyzing how other brands execute marketing well.**
Build your LinkedIn audience by analyzing and writing about case studies of companies or brands you think are doing interesting marketing work. Rather than positioning yourself as an expert prescribing solutions, share your observations and interpretations of what you see them doing well. This approach builds your audience and attracts one-to-one relationship opportunities with people at those companies. It is particularly effective for early-career professionals who want to build credibility without claiming expertise they don't have. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Generate LinkedIn post ideas by writing your own opinion on relevant industry conversations.**
When building LinkedIn content, identify posts from relevant industry voices on topics you care about. Rather than engaging directly, write your own separate post sharing your perspective on the same topic. Use the original post as a prompt for your thinking, not as something to directly reference. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Use video in thought leader ads to build prospect relationships.**
Boost video content from founders, salespeople, and subject matter experts as thought leader ads on LinkedIn. Video increases facetime with prospects, which is valuable for relationship building and typically only available through webinars or calls. Match the persona of the person in the video to your target audience personas (e.g., if targeting sales leaders, boost videos from your sales leaders). This accelerates relationship building compared to company page ads. (Source: Anthony Blatner, Episode #228)

## Category Ownership and Market Education

**Own the broader category narrative through thought leadership and content.**
Beyond product positioning, take ownership of the category itself through thought leadership, content, and playbooks. This includes writing books on the topic, hosting summits, and creating frameworks that help the market understand the category. This elevates the company's position from vendor to category leader. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)

**Build category consensus through analyst relations, peer communities, and thought leadership.**
To establish a new category, execute a multi-channel consensus-building strategy: (1) Analyst relations—bring Gartner, Forrester, IDC along the journey to give the category credibility; (2) Buyer community engagement—participate in roundtables, forums, and Chatham House rule conversations to provoke discussion of the problem; (3) Thought leadership—lead with category point of view, not product messaging. The goal is to create herd mentality where if you can get the first 1,000 customers, the next 10,000 follow. Without this consensus, you face a 10-year street fight for every deal. (Source: Melton Littlepage, Episode #223)

**Embed a former buyer persona as marketing leadership to access and influence target communities.**
Hire a former member of your target buyer persona (e.g., a former CISO) as a direct report to the CMO and member of marketing leadership. Position them to participate authentically in target buyer communities as a peer, not as a vendor. Their role is to agitate for the problems the category solves, highlight pain points, and provoke conversation about solutions—without pitching the product. This builds credibility and insider access that pure marketing cannot achieve. (Source: Melton Littlepage, Episode #223)

**Scale thought leadership with in-person lunch-and-learn ABM.**
Develop a roster of internal subject matter experts with prepared content on topics relevant to your target accounts. Train these experts to deliver lunch-and-learn sessions at target account HQs, paired with local food and hospitality. This tactic is hard to replicate at scale and creates credibility in a crowded digital landscape. Works especially well when there is market uncertainty and buyers are eager to learn. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #331)

## Channel-Specific Tactics

**Build B2B Instagram presence around educational content.**
Create Instagram content (70%+ of posts) focused on tips, tutorials, tactics, and industry insights rather than product promotion. Educational content is highly shareable, builds trust and credibility, and attracts an audience that views your brand as a go-to resource. This works across serious industries (legal, accounting, HR) and doesn't require memes or entertainment-focused content—education alone can drive engagement and loyalty. (Source: Jenn Herman, Episode #168)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not publish generic, company-attributed content.** Content without a human byline and personal perspective underperforms. People respond to human voices, not corporate messaging. (Source: Sylvia LePoidevin, Episode #306)
- **Do not treat a positioning launch as a one-time event.** Expecting the market to absorb new positioning immediately is a mistake. Repetition across channels over 6–12 months is required. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #254 and #184)
- **Do not fake expertise you don't have.** Positioning yourself as an authority in areas where you lack real experience damages credibility and produces generic content. Share what you're learning, not what you wish you knew. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)
- **Do not abandon prospects during buying slowdowns.** Pulling back on content and relationship-building when budgets freeze means you'll be forgotten when budgets reopen. Stay present and helpful. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145)
- **Do not try to own a category name before naming the problem.** Category creation is harder and less defensible than naming a specific, recognizable problem your audience already experiences. (Source: Brendan Hufford, Episode #242)
- **Do not create content that is purely educational or purely entertaining.** Content that is only nutrient-dense gets ignored; content that is only entertaining doesn't build authority. Balance both. (Source: Katelyn Bourgoin, Episode #344)
- **Do not dismiss an idea as too obvious to share.** Obvious insights are competitive advantages when stated publicly and consistently. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #344)
- **Do not pitch the product when participating in buyer communities.** When embedding a former buyer persona in marketing leadership to access peer communities, their role is to agitate for the problem—not to sell. Pitching destroys the credibility the tactic is designed to build. (Source: Melton Littlepage, Episode #223)
