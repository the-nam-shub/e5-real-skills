# Video Content Marketing

## Overview
This skill covers B2B video content marketing across the full production and distribution lifecycle: concept development, production approach, platform-specific strategy, AI-assisted workflows, repurposing, and paid video advertising. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 24 episodes. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved.

---

## Strategy: Start With Concept, Not Production

Before touching a camera or editing tool, establish the creative foundation.

- **Define your concept and hook first.** Before considering cameras, editing software, or production methods, identify what your video series is actually about: the hook, angle, niche, and core concept. Only after you have a clear concept should you think about production tools and methods. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Ask yourself: "Would I actually watch this?"** Before making a video, apply this test. Focus on creating content that stops people in their feed and makes them pay attention, rather than content that fulfills a calendar requirement but never gets watched. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Integrate video into your existing marketing strategy—don't change strategy for video.** Layer video into what's already working: if SEO is your strategy, create how-to videos from top blog posts; if you're product-led growth, create educational product update videos; if you're social-led, experiment with building in public or edutainment content. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Test video ideas on organic social before investing in production.** Analyze your last 60+ posts to find the highest-engagement topics, then use those signals to inform which video concepts are worth producing. This reduces the risk of investing in expensive production for videos that won't resonate. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Start with one or two specific video use cases before expanding.** Identify one or two areas where video can make immediate impact (e.g., social media content, tutorial videos, product demos). Measure results and refine before expanding to other use cases. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #279)

- **Test video with a contained 4-part limited series.** Instead of committing to an ongoing series, run a limited 4-episode series on a specific topic with a guest or collaborator. Measure which episode performed best and whether the series outperformed other marketing materials. Use results to decide whether to continue, pivot, or try a different format. This reduces the psychological barrier to starting video. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

## Production: Hooks, Authenticity, and Iteration

### Hook and Storytelling

- **Prioritize hook and storytelling over production quality.** Video success is determined primarily by the hook, angle, and storytelling—not production quality or editing. A simple, authentic video with a strong hook will outperform a highly polished video with weak messaging. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279) *(Note: production approach is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Spend equal time perfecting the first five seconds as the rest of the video.** A strong hook should: (1) succinctly tell viewers what the video is about, and (2) probe on a pain point or problem relevant to the audience. Include on-screen text in the first five seconds to contextualize the content. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Design video hooks for the first 2 seconds using visual action or text.** Use either a visual hook (a striking action or unexpected visual element) or a text-based hook (a provocative question or statement). Test both approaches to see what stops your audience. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

- **Use the Reverse Rambo hook format for video openings.** Structure hooks in three parts: (1) State a common belief in your industry (e.g., "Everyone says cold emailing is dead"), (2) Say "but" and present your contrarian point of view backed by a stat or personal experience, (3) Conclude with "Here's how you do it." (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Use "How I" framing instead of "How To" in video posts.** Posts using "How I" framing perform approximately 3x better than "How to" equivalents because they feel more authentic and less like the creator is grandstanding. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Add text overlay thumbnails to LinkedIn videos for hook and brand recognition.** Create a consistent visual identity by adding a text overlay at the start (first 2 seconds) with a title or hook. Use a consistent color scheme so viewers immediately recognize your content. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

### Production Approach and Equipment

*(Note: iPhone vs. professional camera is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Shoot the majority of short-form social content on iPhone rather than professional cameras.** iPhone footage performs better because it looks raw and authentic—similar to user-generated content. Reserve professional cameras only for high-end music videos or special productions. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

- **Film in non-traditional settings to create pattern interruption.** Shoot outdoors, while walking, or in casual clothing rather than at a desk with a polished background. Authenticity and conversational tone matter more than production quality—stiff, overproduced videos kill reach. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Embrace imperfect, scrappy content over polished production.** Create and publish content that is real and imperfect rather than waiting for perfect production. Audiences respond to authenticity and directness. (Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)

- **Prioritize multiple video iterations over one high-production video.** Instead of spending months and significant budget on a single polished video, create multiple videos with lower production overhead to gather feedback and iterate. Successful creators accumulate reps over time. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Start creating content without waiting for perfection.** Begin posting even if your first attempts are poor quality. Consistency and iteration matter more than perfection at the start. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

- **Launch video content production with minimal budget using in-house equipment.** Start with under $1,000 in equipment (DSLR camera on desk, Amazon lighting kit) rather than outsourcing to agencies. This allows experimentation with show concepts and audience feedback before committing to higher production budgets. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145) *(Note: production setup approach is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### Subject Matter Expert Video

*(Note: scripted vs. unscripted approach is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Create short-form video by having a subject matter expert answer prompts from a skilled interviewer.** Partner a subject matter expert (e.g., sales leader, founder) with a skilled video producer/interviewer who asks smart questions. Have the expert respond conversationally in 60 seconds or less with a clear structure: here's the conventional belief, here's what taught me it was wrong, here's what I do instead. The producer handles shooting and editing; the expert just shows up and speaks naturally. (Source: Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #232; Jen Allen Knuth, Episode #170)

- **Generate video content by conducting structured content interviews with founders/executives.** Conduct structured "content interviews" where you ask 15 pre-prepared prompts that the founder can answer conversationally. Record the conversation, then extract short video clips (30-60 seconds) or transcribe the audio into text-based posts using tools like Fireflies AI or Otter. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

- **Partner with product marketers for video script and messaging.** Product marketers understand messaging, positioning, and audience pain points, making them natural partners for developing video scripts and ensuring messaging consistency across channels. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

### Production Cadence and Batching

- **Batch content production into 1-2 day sessions per month where you film 6+ episodes at once.** This reduces logistical overhead, allows you to build momentum with actors and crew, and creates a predictable content bank. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

- **Run a weekly writers room and shoot day to produce 12-15 videos per week.** Establish a repeating weekly production cycle: Monday (writers room with 3 ideas per person, vote on top ideas), Tuesday (fine-tune scripts and assign roles), Wednesday (quality check), Thursday (shoot 12-15 videos), Friday (analyze performance and plan next week). This creates a predictable content bank and allows you to stay 4-6 weeks ahead of publishing schedule. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

## Platform Strategy

### LinkedIn

- **Prioritize video content on LinkedIn as the algorithm heavily favors it.** LinkedIn's algorithm is shifting toward video content, prominently featuring it on the homepage and mobile app. If you want to maximize reach on LinkedIn, focus on creating and distributing video content. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

- **Focus on three video types that perform best on LinkedIn in 2025:** (1) Building in public—showing how your business is actually doing and what you're working on, (2) Edutainment—educational content presented in an engaging, fun way, and (3) Collaborations—partnering with other creators or brands to share audiences and expand reach. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Target twice-daily LinkedIn video posting for optimal reach and engagement.** Aim for posting LinkedIn videos twice per day as your ideal frequency. Consistency matters more than perfection; posting frequently with good-enough content outperforms posting rarely with perfect content. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

- **Understand platform-specific video length requirements when repurposing content.** LinkedIn videos can be 1.5+ minutes, but YouTube Shorts requires 1-minute videos. Accept that you may need to create platform-specific content rather than forcing repurposing across platforms. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

- **Edit LinkedIn videos on mobile using CapCut for speed and accessibility.** CapCut supports captions, animated captions, cuts, transitions, audio, and full video editing. This mobile-first workflow allows you to edit quickly without needing desktop software. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

- **Use horizontal video format on LinkedIn for better engagement, not vertical.** Despite the industry trend toward vertical video, LinkedIn's algorithm and user experience actually favor horizontal videos. Vertical videos display poorly in the feed, showing only your face with no context. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #304) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### Instagram

- **Use Instagram Trial Reels to A/B test video hooks and headlines before publishing.** Create three variations of each video with different hooks, headlines, or text-on-screen elements (takes ~4 minutes per variation). Post all three versions to Instagram Trial Reels first—a testing feature that doesn't distribute to your followers. Identify which version performs best, then publish only the winning version to your main feed. This approach can increase reach from 60K to 10-20M impressions on a single video. Trial Reels testing is worth doing even for small accounts (1K followers). (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

- **Use reel length strategically: short reels for reach, longer reels for engagement.** Short reels (under 20 seconds) are top-of-funnel brand awareness content that appear in Explorer and Recommended feeds; they have no call-to-action and focus on reach. Longer reels (30-90 seconds) serve existing audience and contain tutorials, education, and value-add content. Reels overall have lower conversion rates than stories and feed posts, so reserve direct CTAs for stories and feed posts instead. (Source: Jenn Herman, Episode #168)

### YouTube

- **Repurpose long-form content into YouTube Shorts to drive awareness and build audience.** Take podcast episodes or long-form video content and clip them into 60-second YouTube Shorts. People can become fans of your brand by watching short clips without consuming the full episode. Track which clips drive the most engagement to inform future content topics. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

- **Use YouTube as a channel to offset declining organic search traffic from AI search.** As AI search tools reduce traditional search traffic, repurpose blog posts and landing pages into YouTube videos. YouTube remains a strong search and discovery channel. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #279)

- **Turn top blog posts into YouTube videos using SEO ranking tools.** Identify your best-performing blog posts, use a YouTube SEO tool like TubeBuddy to test different video title variations that rank well, then create a video backed by that validated title. Embed the video back into the original blog post with an updated "last written" date to refresh its Google ranking. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Generate YouTube SEO tags and descriptions using AI and validation tools.** Use Descript to upload finished YouTube videos and generate SEO-optimized tags and descriptions via custom prompts. Then validate and refine these suggestions using the VidIQ plugin on your YouTube channel before publishing. (Source: Carter, Episode #345)

- **Discover video content formats by observing what you consume on other platforms.** When running out of ideas, study what you're watching on TikTok, YouTube, or other platforms and identify formats that resonate with you. Adapt those formats to your niche and platform. Test multiple formats and double down on whichever gets the most traction. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

## Video Ads and Paid Distribution

- **Use video ads across all funnel stages, not just top-of-funnel.** Video ads are effective across the entire funnel—top, middle, and bottom. LinkedIn data shows video performs well for consideration and conversion stages as well. Use video to tell stories, demonstrate expertise, build trust, and drive action at every stage. (Source: Davang Shah, Episode #338) *(Note: when to invest in video ads is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Lead with video as the primary creative format for LinkedIn ads.** Video allows you to convey more information, show and hear from a person (which increases engagement), and creates higher-fidelity interactions than static images. Pair video with a mix of long-form content and still images for a holistic campaign, but prioritize video as your lead creative. (Source: Tagg Bozied, Episode #243) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Avoid video ads until you've validated messaging.** Don't invest in production-heavy video ads (with actors, locations, professional lighting) until you've validated what messaging works for your audience. Start with virtual production ads (animation, text-on-screen) or non-paid video content to test messaging first. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

- **Use Connected TV ads for guaranteed full-view impressions and dwell time lift.** LinkedIn offers CTV inventory with guaranteed full-view impressions. Videos must be exactly 6, 15, 30, or 60 seconds with standard bit rates. CTV ads boost average dwell time metrics and have shown lift in organic branded search impressions. Use CTV when you have high-quality video content and want guaranteed viewership beyond LinkedIn's feed. (Source: Tagg Bozied, Episode #243)

- **Boost founder and sales team videos as thought leader ads.** Use LinkedIn's thought leader ads feature to boost video posts from founders, salespeople, and subject matter experts rather than only company page content. Video boosts increase face-time with prospects, which accelerates relationship building. Match the persona of the person being boosted to your target audience personas. (Source: Anthony Blatner, Episode #228 and Episode #152)

- **Leverage LinkedIn partner programs for free video production.** When spending a significant budget on LinkedIn advertising, unlock access to LinkedIn's value-added partner network (e.g., Quickframe). These partners provide free or heavily discounted video production services as part of the partnership. (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

## Repurposing and Content Extraction

- **Repurpose webinar Q&A into short-form social content.** Extract the Q&A section (not the main content) from webinars and repurpose the top audience questions into short-form videos for social and email. Display the question as on-screen text in the first few seconds, then show the answer. Use the question as the video title to drive clicks from people who have that same question. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Use AI-powered video content labs to automatically extract and repurpose webinar clips.** Implement tools like Goldcast's Content Lab to automatically extract relevant clips, timestamps, and snippets from recorded webinars and events using AI, reducing the need for manual video editing and enabling faster content distribution. (Source: Kris Rudeegraap, Episode #159)

- **Write scripts and messaging first, then generate video from written content.** Start with written content (scripts, blog posts, articles) before producing video. This allows you to test messaging cheaply and iterate on the story before investing in video production. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279) *(Note: scripted vs. unscripted approach is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

## Specific Video Formats and Use Cases

- **Create a launch video as your primary anchor asset.** Develop a 1-2 minute launch video as the foundational asset for your product launch campaign. The video should condense your entire story, positioning, and key features into a concise narrative. This video serves multiple purposes: it forces you to nail your story and messaging, and it provides visual and audio assets you can repurpose across other channels (email, social, sales enablement). (Source: Natalie Taylor, Episode #163)

- **Create 15-20 second speaker promo videos for events.** Have key speakers record short 15-20 second promotional clips for upcoming virtual or in-person events. Keep them casual and conversational—include the producer asking the question, capture laughter, and focus on what attendees will experience rather than stiff recitations of session topics. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Create low-cost iPhone video webinar promotions with pattern interrupt.** Create 30-second to 1-minute webinar promotion videos shot on iPhone with minimal production cost, featuring team members in unexpected or humorous scenarios (e.g., eating hot dogs on the street, wearing costumes). These videos are designed to stop the scroll through pattern interrupt and human authenticity. (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

- **Use educational product update videos for product-led growth companies.** For PLG companies, create educational videos showing product updates and features using minimal production: phone on a tripod, PowerPoint slides, casual delivery. The founder or product person explains what was built, why it matters, and how users can get started. These videos perform well in nurture sequences and in-product. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Use AI video to create personalized event promotion videos addressing registrants by name.** Create personalized video messages thanking event registrants and inviting them to attend. Use HeyGen to generate a video of a speaker thanking a specific registrant, mentioning the specific topics that will be covered. This drives higher engagement than generic email invitations. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #270)

## AI-Assisted Video Production

### Workflows and Tools

- **Produce full brand videos with one person in one day using AI tools.** A single person can now produce a complete brand video from concept to final cut in one day by combining AI tools: voice cloning or licensed voices for voiceover, image generation models (Nano Banana) for B-roll, AI video tools to animate stills, and music/sound effects generation. This approach works best for narrative-driven content; avoid using AI for talking-head footage of real people on camera. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Use an AI-powered video production workflow: script to final cut in two weeks.** Create full-length marketing videos using a structured AI workflow: start with Claude to develop and iterate the script, generate storyboards using image models (Nano Banana), create individual scene clips with AI video tools, then move into traditional post-production (editing, color, sound). Use image references as anchors for video generation to ensure consistency across clips. (Source: Vicente, Episode #345)

- **Use AI tools to compress video production cycles from weeks to days.** Leverage AI video generation and editing tools to dramatically reduce the time between creative concept and finished video. This enables faster feedback loops and more frequent testing of video concepts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Use AI video generation to test multiple video variations before committing to expensive production.** Generate multiple video variations using AI tools to test different hooks, angles, and messaging at low cost. Once you've validated a concept through AI-generated variations, you can then invest in more polished production if needed. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Create animated brand characters using Meshy and VEO 3.** Convert a 2D brand mascot or character into a 3D animated asset using Meshy (AI 3D rendering tool) and VEO 3 (Google's video generation model). Upload the original character image to Meshy, generate 3D renderings, take screenshots from multiple angles, then upload those screenshots to VEO 3 with prompts to animate the character in specific ways. Remove backgrounds and integrate the resulting video clips into marketing assets, landing pages, and ads. (Source: Jake Heap, Episode #278)

- **Generate custom B-roll for specific event venues using AI image and video tools.** Instead of being constrained by available stock footage, use AI image generation to create specific B-roll shots that match your event venue and branding. Generate images of your specific theater or venue with your branding, then use those as references for AI video generation. (Source: Carter, Episode #345)

- **Evaluate AI video generation cost against traditional production.** When assessing the cost of AI video generation (typically $3 per generation for tools like Runway), compare it to the cost of traditional production (renting equipment, hiring crew, location fees). AI generation at $3 per iteration is significantly cheaper than even a single day of traditional video production. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

### Voice and Localization

- **Clone executive voice for multilingual ad campaigns.** Use voice cloning technology to record a CEO or executive speaking in their native language, then clone that voice to create ads in other languages. This maintains brand consistency and executive presence across markets without requiring the executive to record in multiple languages. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Test voice cloning vs. dubbing for video localization based on language pair.** When localizing videos across languages, test both voice cloning and dubbing approaches to determine which sounds more natural for each language pair. Voice cloning works better for romance-to-romance language pairs (e.g., Spanish to French) but sounds unnatural when cloning across distant language families (e.g., American English to Asian languages). In those cases, use pre-recorded native voices for dubbing instead. Document which approach works best for each language combination to build a localization playbook. (Source: Carter, Episode #345)

- **Use a licensed voice marketplace for brand video voiceovers with proper rights management.** Instead of hiring voice actors or using unlicensed voices, use a voice marketplace where real voice actors have uploaded their voices and receive ongoing payouts as their voice is used. For celebrity or famous voices, implement a request-based approval system. This ensures legal compliance, supports voice actors financially, and gives brands access to high-quality, recognizable voices. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

### AI Team and Hiring

- **Hire AI creative producers and evaluate with portfolio plus take-home video challenge.** Create a new role called "AI Creative Producer" to manage video production using AI tools. When hiring, evaluate candidates on two criteria: portfolio (showing their creative taste and past work) and a take-home challenge where they produce a full video in 30 minutes using AI tools. This hiring approach filters for people who understand both creative direction and AI tool fluency. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Hire generalist creatives instead of specialists for AI-powered video production.** When building AI video teams, prioritize generalist creatives who understand direction, cinematography, and copy over specialists in single disciplines. The ideal candidate understands the full creative workflow and can move from idea to execution without needing to hand off to multiple specialists. (Source: Vicente, Episode #345)

- **Test AI creative tools by hiring a contractor or agency as your first AI creative role.** Before building an internal AI creative team, hire a contractor or work with an agency to test and validate AI creative workflows. Use this testing phase to document processes and build confidence in the approach before committing to a full-time hire. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Survey your audience to validate acceptance of AI-generated video content.** Conduct a consumer survey to test whether your audience has concerns about AI-generated video content. HeyGen's survey of 2,500+ consumers found that over 90% don't have a problem with brands using AI-generated videos, and quality is the primary concern rather than the fact that it's AI-generated. Use this data to inform your AI video strategy and address stakeholder concerns. (Source: Holly Xiao, Episode #279)

## Product Page Video

- **Avoid animated explainers on product pages; use actual product video or screenshots instead.** Do not use generic animated explainer videos on product pages. Instead, use real product screenshots, GIFs, or actual product video (e.g., Loom recordings) that show the real interface. Animated explainers distract from the core goal of helping prospects understand the actual product they would use. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

- **Keep product page videos under 1 minute, preferably under 30 seconds.** Longer videos (2+ minutes) significantly reduce engagement and watch-through rates. This applies to both product videos and customer testimonial videos. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

- **Avoid embedding YouTube videos on product pages; host video natively or use alternative platforms.** YouTube embeds can display unrelated video recommendations and distract prospects from your product page. Instead, host videos natively on your site or use alternative video platforms that don't introduce external distractions. (Source: Dan, Episode #222)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should B2B marketers shoot video on iPhone or professional cameras?
**Support summary: 5 vs 1 in favor of iPhone/scrappy production**

**Position A — Shoot on iPhone; raw and authentic outperforms polished:**
Chris Cunningham (Episode #347) states explicitly that iPhone footage performs better on social because it looks raw and authentic, similar to user-generated content, and that professional cameras should be reserved only for high-end music videos or special productions. Ryan Narod (Episode #330) reports that low-cost iPhone videos featuring team members in unexpected scenarios often outperform static, polished promotional content despite near-zero production cost. Connor Lewis (Episode #240) argues that authenticity and conversational tone matter more than production quality, and that stiff, overproduced videos kill reach. Max Van Den Ingh (Episode #161) observes that imperfect, scrappy content often resonates more than heavily produced, formal content. Dave Gerhardt (Episode #279) states that video success is determined primarily by hook, angle, and storytelling—not production quality or editing.

**Position B — Start with a proper in-house equipment setup (DSLR + lighting):**
Anthony Kennada (Episode #145) recommends starting with under $1,000 in equipment including a DSLR camera on desk and an Amazon lighting kit, rather than outsourcing to agencies. This implies a step up from phone-only production as the baseline for building a credible video content program.

**Context dependency:** The iPhone-first advice is most clearly directed at short-form social content (LinkedIn, Instagram Reels), while Kennada's DSLR recommendation is aimed at building a show or content program. However, both contexts involve B2B social video content, making this a genuine disagreement on approach.

**Trend note:** More recent guests (2025–2026) consistently favor iPhone/scrappy production, while the DSLR recommendation comes from a 2024 episode. This may reflect a genuine shift in platform norms toward authenticity.

**What this means for you:** If you're producing short-form social content, the weight of evidence favors iPhone and authenticity over polished production. If you're building a structured show or content program, a modest equipment investment may still be warranted. Do not assume polished production is universally better—test both approaches with your specific audience.

### 2. Should you invest in video ads before or after validating your messaging?

**What this means for you:** If you have limited budget or are early in your video program, validate messaging through organic content or low-cost AI-generated variations before committing to expensive video ad production. If you have an established budget and LinkedIn is a core channel, the platform data supports leading with video. These positions are not necessarily mutually exclusive—you can validate messaging cheaply first, then invest in video once you know what works.

- **Don't invest in production-heavy video ads (actors, locations, professional lighting) before validating what messaging works.** Start with virtual production or non-paid video content to test messaging first. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Don't use generic animated explainer videos on product pages.** Use real product screenshots, GIFs, or actual product video instead. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

- **Don't embed YouTube videos on product pages.** YouTube embeds can display unrelated video recommendations and distract prospects. Host video natively or use alternative platforms. (Source: Dan, Episode #222)

- **Don't put videos longer than 1 minute (preferably under 30 seconds) on product pages.** Longer videos significantly reduce engagement and watch-through rates. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

- **Don't change your core marketing strategy just to do video.** Layer video into what's already working rather than adopting video as a standalone tactic. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

- **Don't bet everything on one "perfect" launch video that hasn't been tested.** Create multiple videos with lower production overhead to gather feedback and iterate. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

- **Don't use AI for talking-head footage of real people on camera.** AI video generation works best for narrative-driven content with B-roll and voiceover; avoid using it to simulate real people speaking directly to camera. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Don't use unlicensed voices for brand video voiceovers.** Use a licensed voice marketplace where voice actors receive ongoing payouts and maintain control over their likeness. (Source: Luke, Episode #345)

- **Don't assume voice cloning works equally well across all language pairs.** Test both voice cloning and dubbing for each language combination; cloning across distant language families (e.g., American English to Asian languages) often sounds unnatural. (Source: Carter, Episode #345)

- **Don't sit on content waiting for it to be perfect.** Post it and move on to the next piece. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)
