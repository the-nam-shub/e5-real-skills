# Demand Generation

## Overview
This skill covers B2B demand generation practices across interactive demo deployment, nurture program design, channel strategy, team structure, and messaging prioritization. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across four episodes. No general marketing knowledge has been added — gaps in coverage are intentional.

---

## Interactive Demo Strategy

### Placement and Distribution
When advising on interactive demo strategy, recommend the following placements based on data and guest experience:

**Website placement:** Position an ungated interactive demo as a prominent secondary CTA (e.g., "Take a Tour") alongside the primary CTA (e.g., "Get a Demo"). Place it above the fold and include it in the navigation bar. Data cited by the guest shows 84% of top-performing demos use this placement, and ungated demos drive 40–60% visitor engagement versus 10–15% for product videos. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

**LinkedIn ads:** When running LinkedIn campaigns, drive traffic to a landing page featuring the interactive demo rather than asking users to book a demo directly. LinkedIn users are in a passive browsing mindset and prefer low-friction engagement. The demo serves as a self-serve entry point before a sales conversation. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

**Review sites:** Embed interactive demos on third-party review sites like G2 where prospects are actively comparing vendors. This is a high-intent moment — prospects are already evaluating alternatives — and an interactive demo lets you show differentiation directly rather than relying on written descriptions. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

**Email campaigns:** Embed or link to interactive demos in email campaigns, particularly for product-led growth onboarding sequences. Use demos to re-engage users who haven't logged in after day one by educating them about the product outside the product itself. Also use demos in nurture campaigns to highlight specific features or use cases. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

### What to Avoid with Demos
- Do not gate interactive demos behind a form if the goal is broad top-of-funnel engagement. Ungated demos dramatically outperform gated product videos on visitor engagement. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)
- Do not treat demos as a single-placement asset. Distribute them across website, ads, email, and review sites to maximize reach across the buyer journey. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

## Nurture Program Design

### Align Nurture Goals to Your Sales Motion
Before designing nurture sequences, establish what success looks like based on your go-to-market model:

- **PLG companies:** Nurture emails should drive direct conversion. Write compelling hooks and include clear CTAs. If open rates are high but conversion is low, diagnose the email content itself. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)
- **Enterprise / sales-led companies with long cycles:** Nurture emails should build trust, authority, and top-of-mind presence — not drive direct conversion. Do not expect nurture emails alone to close deals in long-cycle sales. High open rates with low conversion in this context may reflect correct behavior, not a failure. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)

### Nurture Across Channels, Not Just Email
Reframe nurturing from "how many emails should we send" to "how do we maintain presence across all channels our audience consumes." A prospect who listens to your podcast for six months and converts after seeing a LinkedIn post has been nurtured — just not through email. Recognize that 95% of your audience is not ready to buy right now; your job is to stay top-of-mind across podcast, LinkedIn, content, and other channels so you're the first company they think of when they are ready. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #120)

## Funnel Optimization: Micro-Surface Improvements

### Use a Structured Benchmark to Find Gaps
When helping a team identify demand gen improvement opportunities, recommend conducting a structured evaluation across messaging alignment, experimentation, customer focus, and related categories using a framework like the Demand Efficiency Benchmark. This surfaces specific, actionable gaps — missing lead routing, absent middle-of-funnel nurture sequences, lack of use-case-specific messaging — without requiring major campaign overhauls. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)

### Prioritize and Execute Incrementally
Once gaps are identified, map each micro-surface opportunity and estimate effort (small / medium / large) and potential impact. Stack rank by impact, cluster related items, and commit to a sustainable execution cadence — for example, one large + one medium + one small improvement per month, or three small improvements per week. Execute each item to 80% completion before moving to the next. Do not pursue perfection before shipping. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)

## Channel Strategy and Awareness Building

### Build Audience Before You Launch
When advising early-stage companies or teams launching new products, recommend building an email list, social following, and market credibility before launch. This audience becomes a distribution channel that provides initial signups and validation without requiring paid acquisition. Note: an existing audience in an adjacent category does not automatically convert to customers in a new category, but it provides a meaningful head start and reduces CAC. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

### Focus on One Channel as a Startup
For startups with limited resources, recommend committing fully to one primary awareness channel rather than spreading effort across PR, events, SEO, and paid ads simultaneously. For B2B SaaS, LinkedIn is cited as highly effective. Set a specific, measurable goal for that channel (e.g., grow company page followers to X) and direct all effort there until traction is established. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #119)

### Build Referral Pipeline Through Intentional Networking
Advise founders to invest in regular, in-person relationship-building with potential customers and market influencers — for example, one dinner per week with someone new. The goal is genuine relationship-building, not transactional networking. When influential people in your space understand what you do, they will casually recommend your product to others they work with, creating an organic referral pipeline. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

### Prioritize Mental Availability When You Have No Competitors
When a company is the only product in its category, advise focusing marketing on making target buyers aware that the solution exists and what problem it solves — rather than optimizing for conversion. The goal is to create a mental association between the buyer's problem and the product name. Conversion optimization is secondary when awareness is the binding constraint. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Team Structure and Messaging

### Structure Demand Gen and Creative/PMM as Interdependent Teams
When advising on marketing org design, recommend organizing so that demand gen (growth marketing) owns distribution channels and optimization, while product marketing and creative own messaging and asset development. Make these teams explicitly interdependent:

- Demand gen identifies creative wear-out and requests fresh messaging from PMM/creative.
- PMM/creative uses performance data from demand gen to iterate on messaging.
- Compensate senior members of both teams on revenue outcomes to align incentives toward collaboration.

This structure prevents demand gen from being blamed for poor performance when the real bottleneck is weak creative, and prevents PMM/creative from being disconnected from performance impact. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

### Prioritize Messaging Before Optimizing Distribution
Advise teams to invest heavily in developing strong, memorable messaging and creative concepts before optimizing channels or targeting. Strong messaging can drive 3–5x performance improvements and has inherent self-distribution because people share and discuss it. Weak messaging cannot be fixed by better ad targeting or channel selection. Once messaging is locked, demand gen teams can optimize distribution. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

When helping evaluate underperformance, check messaging quality before recommending channel or budget changes. Poor demand gen results are frequently a messaging problem, not a distribution problem. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not gate interactive demos.** Ungated demos drive 40–60% visitor engagement; gated product videos drive 10–15%. Gating reduces the reach of your highest-converting demo asset. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)
- **Do not ask LinkedIn users to book a demo directly from an ad.** LinkedIn users are in a passive browsing mindset. Drive them to a low-friction landing page with a demo instead. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)
- **Do not expect nurture emails to drive direct conversion in enterprise/long-cycle sales.** Nurture in this context is a trust-building vehicle, not a conversion mechanism. Misaligned expectations lead to incorrect diagnosis of underperformance. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)
- **Do not spread startup marketing effort across multiple channels simultaneously.** Diluted effort across PR, events, SEO, and paid ads is less effective than full commitment to one channel. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #119)
- **Do not polish micro-surface improvements to perfection before shipping.** Execute to 80% completion and move to the next item. Perfectionism slows compounding gains across the funnel. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)
- **Do not blame demand gen for poor performance before auditing messaging.** The most common root cause of underperforming campaigns is weak creative or messaging, not distribution failure. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
- **Do not silo demand gen from PMM/creative.** When these teams operate independently, demand gen lacks fresh messaging and PMM/creative lacks performance feedback — both teams underperform. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
