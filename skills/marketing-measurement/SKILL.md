# Marketing Measurement

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketing leaders should approach measurement: from budget allocation and channel validation to attribution methodology and brand campaign measurement. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests. No general marketing knowledge has been added — gaps in coverage are intentional.

---

## Budget Allocation and Channel Investment

**Start spending to learn, not after you've learned.**
When allocating budget to a new channel, begin spending to gather data points and understand efficiency curves rather than waiting for perfect information. You cannot know how efficient a channel will be without spending and testing. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Apply a 70/30 split between proven and experimental channels.**
Allocate 70% of marketing budget to mature, proven channels and reserve 30% for testing new channels and experimental initiatives. This ensures consistent performance on core channels while maintaining a dedicated experimental budget that won't tank overall metrics if tests fail. Track experimental spend separately and align with finance so experimental losses don't penalize overall marketing performance. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Match measurement sophistication to spend scale.**
For early-stage companies with limited channels, simple month-over-month metric tracking is sufficient. Only invest in sophisticated measurement infrastructure once you're spending hundreds of thousands of dollars monthly across multiple channels. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

## Channel Validation: The Maturity Framework

**Require channels to earn their way into core budget through a structured progression.**
Implement a channel maturity framework with explicit gates before any channel receives core budget allocation:

- **Early stage:** Establish solid optimization events and baseline reporting.
- **Mid stage:** Run in-platform incremental tests (cheaper, less rigorous, but directionally useful).
- **Mature stage:** Pass rigorous geo-based incremental tests and appear organically in customer surveys and sales call transcripts.

This prevents premature scaling of unproven channels. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Use cheaper in-platform tests first, then graduate to geo-based tests.**
Start with in-platform holdout tests (e.g., Meta's holdout testing) to validate that a channel works before committing to more expensive and rigorous geo lift tests. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

## Attribution: What to Use and What to Avoid

**Do not use MTA or last-touch models for cross-channel budget allocation.**
Multi-touch attribution and last-touch models are unreliable for cross-channel budget decisions in a privacy-constrained world due to data limitations and privacy regulations. Reserve MTA for a narrower purpose: setting goals within a single channel and understanding which campaigns perform best within that channel. Use incremental testing and customer research for cross-channel budget decisions. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Use AI to scan sales call transcripts for attribution signals.**
Pipe Gong call transcripts through an LLM to identify how customers heard about your company and which marketing touchpoints they mention. This surfaces attribution data that often contradicts traditional MTA models and reveals which channels are actually driving awareness and consideration. This is particularly valuable for B2B companies with sales-led motions where customers naturally discuss their discovery journey during calls. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Run weekly brand awareness surveys with a geographic component.**
Conduct weekly brand awareness surveys that include geographic data to measure the impact of regional campaigns (e.g., billboards, geo-targeted ads). Track how brand awareness changes in test geographies versus control geographies. Use historical data to estimate the downstream value of a one-point increase in brand awareness to inform ROI calculations for brand-building activities. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

## Incremental Testing

**Use incremental testing to measure causal impact, not correlation.**
Run incremental tests to isolate the causal impact of marketing spend by comparing outcomes between audiences exposed to marketing versus control groups that don't see the ads. Methods vary by channel:

- **Billboards / regional campaigns:** Geo lift tests
- **Paid social:** In-platform holdout tests (e.g., Meta)
- **Full-funnel validation:** Rigorous geo-based incremental tests

This reveals true ROI rather than correlation-based attribution. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Use event studies for brand stunts and creative campaigns.**
Measure the impact of brand campaigns and creative stunts by forecasting baseline marketing performance, executing the campaign, then comparing actual results to the forecast. This isolates the causal impact of the specific marketing activity without requiring direct response attribution. Applicable to brand stunts, sponsorships, and other initiatives where traditional attribution fails. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

## Measurement Philosophy and Team Structure

**Align measurement approach to the type of marketing — not the other way around.**
Design measurement frameworks that match the nature of the marketing activity. Brand and creative campaigns should not be measured with direct response metrics. Develop flexible measurement approaches (surveys, event studies, customer interviews) that fit the marketing objective. Forcing all marketing into a single measurement model is the fastest way to kill creative marketing ideas. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Use hypothesis-driven testing instead of waiting for perfect data.**
Form clear hypotheses about marketing effectiveness and test them using methods appropriate to the channel:

- Direct mail → A/B tests
- Podcasts → surveys or customer interviews
- Regional campaigns → geo lift tests

This approach does not require a data science team — it is rooted in the scientific method. Do not avoid action because measurement is complex. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

**Clarify the roles of marketing leadership and the data team.**
Frame the relationship as a pilot and instrument providers: the marketing leader makes decisions and takes actions (flying the plane), while the data team provides better instruments and tools to inform those decisions. The data team's job is to give the marketing leader more visibility — not to make the decisions. This prevents data teams from becoming bottlenecks. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not use MTA or last-touch attribution for cross-channel budget decisions.** These models are unreliable in a privacy-constrained environment. Use them only for within-channel goal-setting. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)
- **Do not measure brand and creative campaigns with direct response metrics.** This kills creative marketing ideas and produces misleading conclusions. Match the measurement method to the marketing type. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)
- **Do not wait for perfect data before spending on a new channel.** You cannot understand a channel's efficiency curve without spending and testing. Start small, gather data, then optimize. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)
- **Do not scale a channel before it has passed through the maturity framework.** Premature scaling of unproven channels wastes budget and distorts overall performance metrics. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)
- **Do not let the data team make marketing decisions.** Their role is to provide instruments and visibility, not to pilot the plane. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)
