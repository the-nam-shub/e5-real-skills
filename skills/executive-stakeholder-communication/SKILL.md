# Executive & Stakeholder Communication

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketers should communicate with CEOs, boards, and other executive stakeholders — including how to frame marketing investments, what metrics to report, how to manage idea flow from leadership, and how to calibrate communication to your audience. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests and reflect their direct experience leading or advising B2B marketing teams.

---

## Framing Marketing Investments for Executive Buy-In

When pitching brand or design work to stakeholders, lead with conversion impact rather than aesthetic appeal. Ask the stakeholder directly: *"If we could increase conversions by 25% through design changes, would you care what it looked like?"* This reframes the conversation from "we want to look cool" to "this is a performance lever." Once executives see design as a measurable ROI driver, they become more willing to take creative risks and invest in brand work that differentiates the company. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)

**Do:**
- Anchor brand and design requests to conversion outcomes and revenue impact
- Use concrete hypotheticals ("If X% lift, would you invest?") to shift the frame before asking for budget
- Position creative risk-taking as a business decision, not an aesthetic preference

**Don't:**
- Lead with how something will look or feel — executives who aren't marketing-native will not find this compelling on its own

## Calibrating Communication to the CEO's Marketing Literacy

Before reporting to or discussing marketing strategy with the CEO, assess their familiarity with marketing concepts and terminology. Technical founders or those new to marketing may need first-principles explanations of concepts like storytelling, messaging, and channel strategy. Marketing-experienced CEOs may not need this context. Tailor your communication depth and terminology to match their baseline understanding — this prevents talking past each other and helps the CEO understand why marketing decisions matter. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Do:**
- Diagnose your CEO's marketing background before your first substantive conversation
- Adjust vocabulary and explanation depth based on what they already know
- Treat first-principles explanations as a feature, not a condescension — some CEOs genuinely need them

**Don't:**
- Assume a shared vocabulary around marketing concepts; terms like "MQL" or "brand storytelling" may mean nothing or something different to a technical founder

## Reporting the Right Metrics to Executives and Boards

When reporting marketing performance to the CEO and board, avoid intermediate funnel metrics like demo volume, MQL count, or other activity-based measures. Instead, report on revenue (new revenue added or all-up revenue) or the specific metrics in your revenue supply chain that you directly own — for example, site visits or demo requests — only when those are clearly connected to revenue outcomes. Vanity metrics create misalignment because they don't reflect what the company actually cares about, and they can make marketing appear successful even when the business is struggling. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Do:**
- Report on revenue or revenue-influenced metrics as the primary signal
- If reporting on intermediate metrics, explicitly connect them to revenue impact
- Ask yourself before any executive report: "Does this metric tell them whether the business is winning?"

**Don't:**
- Bring demo volume or MQL counts to a board meeting as headline metrics
- Use metrics that can look good while the business is underperforming — this erodes trust when the gap becomes visible

## Managing CEO Idea Flow Without Burning Out Your Team

Create a centralized system — for example, a Notion database — where all CEO ideas and requests are logged, prioritized, and tracked. Organize by discipline (e.g., separate databases for product marketing vs. growth marketing). Groom the backlog regularly in team meetings. When the CEO proposes a new idea, explicitly surface the trade-off: *"We're currently committed to X; adding Y means we drop X or delay it."* This prevents the CEO from assuming ideas are already in motion and allows them to make informed prioritization calls. The system channels founder energy without stifling it — ideas are never rejected, but they are sequenced and made visible. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Do:**
- Log every CEO idea in a shared, visible system immediately
- Make trade-offs explicit every time a new request comes in
- Groom the backlog with your team on a regular cadence so nothing silently falls through

**Don't:**
- Try to absorb CEO requests informally — this leads to team burnout and the CEO believing work is happening when it isn't
- Reject ideas outright; sequence them instead

## Reading the CEO's Context Before Bringing Issues or Requests

Before reporting a marketing problem or requesting resources from the CEO, consider what other major issues they may be dealing with — runway concerns, competitive threats, board pressure, or other crises. The CEO may have information you don't (e.g., the company is running low on cash) that completely changes how they will prioritize your request. Calibrate your ask and the altitude of your conversation to match their likely context. Avoid bringing small tactical issues when the company may be in crisis mode. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

**Do:**
- Develop situational awareness about what the CEO is managing before scheduling a conversation
- Match the altitude of your ask to the altitude of their current concerns
- Ask yourself: "Is this the right moment to bring this, given what I know about the business?"

**Don't:**
- Escalate tactical issues (e.g., a broken form, a delayed campaign) to the CEO when larger business concerns are likely dominating their attention
- Assume your marketing context is the most important thing on their plate

## What NOT To Do

- **Don't lead brand or design pitches with aesthetics.** Framing creative work as "looking better" fails to connect to what executives care about. Lead with conversion and revenue impact instead. (Source: Eli Rubel, Episode #120)
- **Don't report vanity metrics to boards or CEOs.** MQL counts and demo volumes can mask business underperformance and erode credibility when the gap becomes visible. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
- **Don't assume a shared marketing vocabulary with your CEO.** Technical founders in particular may need first-principles explanations of concepts that feel basic to marketers. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
- **Don't absorb CEO ideas informally.** Without a visible prioritization system, teams burn out, work gets duplicated or dropped, and the CEO loses visibility into what's actually happening. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
- **Don't bring tactical problems to a CEO who may be in crisis mode.** Read the room and calibrate the altitude of your conversation to their current context. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
