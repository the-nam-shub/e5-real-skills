# B2B Copywriting

## Overview
This skill covers B2B copywriting best practices for website messaging, homepage structure, messaging evaluation, and social content (specifically X/Twitter threads). All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests; no general copywriting knowledge has been added. Where the source material does not address a topic, this skill leaves it uncovered.

---

## Website Messaging: Clarity and Specificity

**Avoid vague, generic copy that tries to satisfy everyone.**
When internal stakeholders — CEO, sales, marketing — have conflicting priorities, the temptation is to write copy that hedges toward all of them. Resist this. The result is messaging that sounds professional but communicates nothing specific. Be explicit about what you do, who you serve, and what problem you solve. Specificity is more persuasive than breadth. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Evaluate messaging across five components, not subjective preference.**
When reviewing or improving copy, assess it against these five dimensions:
1. **Clarity** — Do visitors immediately understand what the product is and how it works?
2. **Relevance** — Do the promised benefits match what customers actually care about?
3. **Value** — Is the perceived value clearly communicated?
4. **Differentiation** — Is it clear why a buyer should choose you over competitors?
5. **Brand perception** — Does the tone match the intended brand personality?

Using this framework moves messaging discussions from "I like/don't like this" to objective, structured critique. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Homepage Structure and Information Hierarchy

**Structure the homepage to answer visitor questions in the order they ask them.**
Visitors arrive with a sequence of questions: *What is it? Why should I care? Why you?* The homepage should answer them in exactly that order:

1. **Category definition** (headline or preheader) — Immediately establish what the product is so visitors don't have to infer it.
2. **Value proposition** — Communicate your "onliness": why you, not a competitor.
3. **Proof, examples, and differentiation** — Support the value proposition with evidence.

The information hierarchy should reflect customer priorities, not internal stakeholder preferences. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Write copy before designing the website.**
Complete all copy — headlines, subheads, body copy, CTAs — before design work begins. Copy drives the information hierarchy, which then tells the designer how to allocate space and visual emphasis. Design's job is to communicate the message; it should not precede or constrain the message. Starting with design and filling in copy later produces layouts that fight the content. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Social Content: X (Twitter) Thread Structure

**Use a four-part formula for X threads that drive engagement and downstream conversions.**
Structure every X thread as follows:

1. **Eye-catching opening image** — Use a screenshot of a company or person, a growth chart, or another relevant visual. This stops the scroll.
2. **Hook with a compelling stat or data point** — Lead with something specific and surprising that earns the click to expand.
3. **Core content that is ridiculously valuable** — Deliver enough value that the reader thinks, *"Why is this free?"*
4. **Soft call-to-action at the end** — Reference another thread, invite newsletter subscription, or offer a low-friction next step. Do not use a hard sell.

This format is designed to drive both engagement and downstream conversions such as newsletter signups and customer inquiries. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not write copy to satisfy internal stakeholders at the expense of the customer.** Copy shaped by committee pressure to include everyone's priorities becomes vague and unconvincing. The audience is the customer, not the CEO or sales team. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
- **Do not let designers start before copy is finished.** Handing designers placeholder text ("lorem ipsum" or rough notes) and asking copywriters to fill in later forces copy to conform to a layout rather than the layout serving the message. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
- **Do not bury the category definition.** If visitors cannot immediately tell what the product *is*, they will not stay long enough to learn why they should care. The category must come first. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
- **Do not end X threads with a hard sell.** A soft CTA (another thread, a newsletter invite) outperforms aggressive promotional asks in this format. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
