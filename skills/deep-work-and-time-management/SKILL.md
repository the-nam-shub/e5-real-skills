# Deep Work and Time Management for B2B Marketers

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketing leaders and practitioners should structure their calendars, protect focused work time, manage communication tools, and allocate attention across competing demands. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 9 episodes. No general productivity advice has been added — every recommendation traces to a named guest and episode.

---

## Structuring Your Calendar for Strategic Output

**Separate manager days from maker days.** Do not allow meetings and deep work to coexist on the same days. Stack all internal meetings on a single day (e.g., Monday), reserve separate days (e.g., Wednesday and Friday) entirely for uninterrupted thinking, writing, and strategic work. Chris Walker's specific structure: Monday for internal meetings, Tuesday for podcast recording, Thursday for sales calls, Wednesday and Friday as zero-meeting maker days. For leaders responsible for strategy and thought leadership, protecting 2+ full days per week for uninterrupted thinking is essential. (Source: Chris Walker, Episodes #211 and #281)

**Structure your week day-by-day with specific focus areas.** Assign each day of the week a defined purpose to prevent your calendar from owning you. Example structure: Monday for week planning and cross-functional alignment; Tuesday for a weekly leadership call with direct reports (1 hour) plus a pipeline call with sales and RevOps; Wednesday for one-on-one meetings with all direct reports (30–45 minutes each), framed as service to them to unblock challenges. This creates a predictable rhythm and ensures consistent time for people management, strategy, and operations. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)

**Organize each day around 1–2 big focus projects with 2–3 hour blocks.** Spend the first 2–3 hours of your day on one or two major priorities. Once those are completed, your day is considered a success. Only after completing your big work blocks should you handle Slack, email, and team check-ins — in a dedicated block of approximately one hour, once or twice daily. This prevents reactive work from dominating your day and creates a sense of accomplishment early, which improves overall mood and productivity. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295 and #234)

## Protecting Deep Work for Creative and Strategic Thinking

**Time-box deep work separately from reactive tasks.** Designate specific times in the morning and afternoon for reactive work (Slack, email, ad hoc requests), and protect the remaining time for deep work on one or two significant projects. Deep work produces better results than multitasking, even though multitasking feels productive. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #257)

**Protect deep work blocks specifically for creative messaging and writing.** Good writing and creative messaging require space and breathing room. Constant context-switching — Slack, email, back-to-back meetings — prevents the kind of thinking needed for inspired ideas and great headlines. Creativity does not happen in fragmented time or under constant pressure to deliver. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episode #295)

**Take regular 3–4 day breaks away from your workspace to solve strategic problems.** Schedule breaks at regular intervals (Chris Walker uses 6-week cycles) where you leave your workspace entirely for 3–4 days. Avoid active work during these breaks, but allow space for thinking and writing. The return to your workspace typically produces a surge of clarity — solving problems that had been stuck for months. Treat these breaks as business investments, not expenses. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #211)

## Managing Communication Without Constant Interruption

**Check email and Slack on a fixed schedule, not continuously.** Batch communication review into 1–2 dedicated blocks per day rather than checking every few minutes. Continuous checking creates a stress response in your brain — your nervous system perceives it as being in a state of threat — even when your actual job is low-stress. Limiting frequency reduces anxiety and improves focus and creative output. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Conduct Slack and email sweeps twice daily — morning and afternoon.** Treat Slack like an inbox with defined review windows rather than a real-time notification stream. This reduces the compulsion to check constantly while ensuring you don't miss critical messages. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

## Planning and Prioritization Habits

**Write daily priorities in a physical notebook before bed.** Each night before bed, write out 3–4 big priorities for the next day in a physical notebook. This gets tasks out of your head, reduces mental load, and allows you to be present and relaxed in the evening. The act of writing clarifies what matters most and creates a clear plan for the next day. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Protect calendar-blocked experimentation time for AI learning.** Allocate and protect a minimum of one to two hours per week on team members' calendars specifically for AI tool exploration and learning. Without protected time, learning gets crowded out by urgent work. This also signals organizational permission to invest in AI capability building. (Source: Bill Glenn, Episode #328)

## CMO-Specific Time Allocation

**Recognize and allocate time across all three jobs of the CMO — not just one.** The CMO role has three distinct jobs: (1) Run marketing, (2) Help your boss run the company, and (3) Market marketing internally. Most CMOs spend 100–120% of their time on job one and neglect jobs two and three, which leads to failure. Job two requires active involvement in CEO-level business decisions and strategy. Job three requires proactive internal communication about what marketing does and why it matters — treat it like a political campaign: hold town halls with sales, meet one-on-one with key stakeholders, and consistently communicate marketing's value inside the organization. Allocate time deliberately across all three. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not allow meetings and deep work to share the same days.** Context-switching between meeting mode and maker mode kills creative productivity. (Source: Chris Walker, Episodes #211 and #281)
- **Do not check Slack and email continuously throughout the day.** Constant checking creates a neurological stress response and fragments the attention needed for strategic and creative work. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295, #234, and #298)
- **Do not let urgent reactive work crowd out scheduled experimentation time.** Without calendar protection, AI learning and capability building will always lose to immediate demands. (Source: Bill Glenn, Episode #328)
- **Do not spend all your time as a CMO running marketing.** Neglecting internal positioning of the marketing function and involvement in company-level strategy are primary causes of CMO failure. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
- **Do not attempt creative writing or strategic messaging in fragmented time.** Creativity requires uninterrupted space; trying to produce it between Slack pings and meetings produces inferior output. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episode #295)
- **Do not treat strategic breaks as a luxury or expense.** Leaving your workspace periodically for 3–4 days generates strategic clarity that cannot be produced by grinding through the calendar. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #211)
