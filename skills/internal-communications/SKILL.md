# Internal Communications for B2B Marketing Teams

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketing leaders and practitioners should communicate internally—with leadership, sales, product, engineering, and the broader company. It addresses recurring communication cadences, presentation strategy, cross-functional alignment, recognition programs, and how to position the marketing function inside the organization. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 19 episodes.

---

## The CMO's Internal Communication Mandate

Recognize that the CMO role contains three distinct jobs, not one: (1) Run marketing, (2) Help your boss run the company, and (3) Market marketing—the internal positioning of the marketing function itself. Most CMOs spend 100–120% of their time on job one and neglect jobs two and three, which leads to failure. Allocate time deliberately across all three. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

Job three—marketing marketing—requires proactive internal communication about what marketing does and why it matters. Treat it like a political campaign: hold town halls with sales, meet one-on-one with key stakeholders, and consistently build visibility for the marketing function across the organization. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

Prioritize internal alignment and enablement—ensuring sales understands what marketing is doing, why, and what to expect from prospects—as equally important as external marketing. Misalignment causes buyers to arrive at sales conversations confused about what they signed up for. Internal marketing (roadmaps, enablement materials, messaging guides) prevents this disjointedness and makes sales advocates for marketing efforts. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #198)

## Recurring Communication Cadences

### Weekly Visual Highlight Reels
Create a weekly deck on a Monday cadence with two sections: what shipped last week and what is shipping next week. Make it completely visual with minimal text—use screenshots of blog posts, announcements, customer comments, and product launches rather than metrics or frameworks. This makes marketing's output visible and tangible to the broader organization. Avoid overly lofty, numerical, or framework-heavy language; keep it simple and visual. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episodes #283 and #199)

### Same-Day Launch Recap Emails
When a product launch or major content initiative ships, send an email to the entire company at end of day with a recap including: what went out, early comments and reactions, view counts across channels, and website traffic spikes. This keeps the organization informed of marketing wins and helps non-marketing teams—especially engineering—see the downstream impact of their work. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episodes #283 and #199)

### Weekly Sales Enablement Slack Channel
Create a dedicated, low-noise Slack channel where marketing posts exactly three items every Friday at a consistent time (e.g., 10am): (1) a current campaign sales can push in outbound, (2) a new asset or piece of content, and (3) something relevant but not new that they can still leverage. Keep messages short with just links; replies go in threads. This reduces information overload, makes expectations clear, and aligns with when sales teams plan their weekly cadences. (Source: Talya Heller, Episode #246)

### Full-Team GTM Alignment Meetings
Run two recurring meetings: (1) a full-company weekly wins meeting where everyone shares a win and gives a shout-out to create visibility across departments, and (2) a full-team sprint meeting at the top of the week where marketing, sales, and CS align on what everyone is working on. These meetings create an open forum where team members can identify how to help each other and ensure everyone is working toward the shared north star of revenue. (Source: Natalie Taylor, Episode #306)

### Weekly Transparency Rituals
Create recurring company meetings—for example, a Monday all-hands to discuss weekly commitments and a Friday retrospective to review wins and misses—where you openly discuss what the marketing team accomplished, what they missed, and what they learned. Encourage discussion of failures and mistakes, not just wins. Model this transparency from leadership down so team members feel safe admitting when something isn't working. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

## Formal Presentations and Kickoffs

Work closely with your CRO to determine what the revenue organization wants to hear about marketing. Secure a dedicated 20-minute slot at yearly and half-year company kickoffs to present marketing updates. Use ad-hoc all-hands presentations for major announcements such as partnerships or process changes. Treat internal communication as core work, not an afterthought. (Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #302)

Recognize that creating and delivering marketing presentations to the broader company is core work, not a distraction from "real" marketing work. Treat presentations as trust-building and stakeholder alignment activities. Put significant effort into these presentations, especially when representing other people's work. (Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episodes #302 and #193)

Supplement formal presentations with ad hoc internal communications to educate the broader company on specific marketing initiatives—announcing major partnerships, explaining process changes, or highlighting underutilized assets like case studies. These can be brief, focused messages that help the company understand and support marketing efforts without requiring formal all-hands presentations. (Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #193)

When conducting a rebrand or repositioning that changes both category and brand identity simultaneously, conduct separate training sessions for each. Category training should cover the new industry/competitive landscape and value proposition; brand training covers the new name, visual identity, and messaging. Separating these prevents confusion and allows you to measure the impact of each initiative independently. (Source: Clare Schmitt, Episode #333)

## Structuring and Tailoring Internal Messages

### Apply Audience-Centric Discipline
Apply the same audience-centric discipline to internal communication that you use for external marketing. For each communication, ask: Who is this person? What message do they need to hear? How do they need to hear it—Slack, Loom, Confluence, a meeting? Tailor your communication accordingly, whether you are talking to leadership, peers, or your team. (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)

### Use a Four-Part Structure for Slack Updates
When sharing updates in Slack or other internal channels, use a consistent four-part structure: (1) Topic—a three-word summary of what you are sharing; (2) Who it is relevant for—tag specific people or teams; (3) Action requested—what you need from them, if anything; (4) Context—supporting details, which can be bullet points, a Confluence page, or a Loom video. This format lets people quickly determine if they need to engage and signals respect for their time. (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)

### Anchor All Communication on Goals
When communicating across a marketing organization, start by anchoring everyone on shared goals. Make goals transparent and easy to find across the whole organization, from company-level down to team-level. Standardize how you share information so people can quickly filter what is relevant to them. Every communication should answer: Why does this matter? Who should care about this? Why should they care? (Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)

### Apply Clarity as Kindness
Treat clarity as a form of kindness when communicating internally. Say what you mean directly and thoughtfully, without making people sort through unclear language to figure out your intent. Direct, clear communication signals respect for people's time and makes them more willing to engage with you and your work. (Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)

### Connect Metrics to Business Outcomes for Non-Marketers
When sharing marketing updates with engineers, product leaders, or other non-marketers, do not lead with marketing metrics such as click-through rates or impressions. Instead, ask: What action will they take with this information? If they will not take action, do not share it. If they will, connect the metric to something they care about. For example, instead of saying "CTR increased 2%," say "The feature you built launched three months ago with X users; it has now grown by 200–300%—thank you for building this." (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)

### Manage Up by Extracting Key Highlights
When your boss asks you to remind them of something you have already shared, do not re-send the full document. Instead, extract the three key highlights that are relevant to their question and present those. This shows you understand their priorities, makes it easy for them to use the information, and demonstrates respect for their time. (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)

## Sharing Research and Customer Intelligence Internally

Share market intelligence and customer insights in real-time as they emerge, rather than batching them into formal weekly reports. Post quick findings from secret shopping, customer calls, or events to leadership updates within days. Attach intelligence to existing forums where strategic decisions are being made—product meetings, customer success reviews, leadership meetings—so insights directly inform decisions rather than sitting in a document. (Source: Jennifer Cannizzaro, Episode #267)

After each customer interview, post the top 2–3 takeaways to a company or team Slack channel immediately. Share positive feedback publicly to boost morale across all teams, including product and R&D. Share critical feedback or product complaints directly with the relevant team rather than in public channels. (Source: Shoshana Kordova, Episode #250)

Do not let research findings die in a slide deck. Actively share insights through newsletters, link them to tests, present findings in person, and tell the story around the data. Avoid reading slides verbatim; instead, make findings exciting and accessible. Maintain an insights repository or knowledge base where all research deliverables live for easy reference and reuse. (Source: Haley Carpenter, Episode #282)

Conduct emotional targeting research and share the findings—pains, desired outcomes, emotional drivers—with product, sales, customer success, and leadership. This creates a common language across teams about who you are selling to, why they buy, and how to talk about it. The research breaks down silos and makes it easier to get buy-in for messaging changes because everyone is working from the same customer data rather than opinions. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #231)

## Cross-Functional Storytelling and Engagement

Attend regular cross-functional team meetings—customer success, sales, engineering, and others—and deliver carefully prepared presentations that tell a compelling story about marketing initiatives rather than simply listing projects and timelines. Focus on narrative arc and impact rather than process. This approach generates organic interest and volunteers from other teams who want to get involved in marketing programs or support future initiatives. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episodes #283 and #199)

Instead of relying on Slack channels, which become noise, present event updates, new speakers, agenda changes, and strategy in larger team meetings such as weekly marketing or sales meetings. Use this time to also brainstorm with teams on how to get specific target accounts to attend. (Source: Kristina DeBrito, Episode #227)

Use humor, storytelling, and creative formats—such as a talk show format, SNL-style skits, or customer case study videos—to make strategy rollouts and sales enablement engaging and memorable. Sales teams are more likely to remember and use messaging that was delivered in an entertaining way versus a standard presentation. (Source: Kimberly Storin, Episode #229)

## Goal Visibility and Team Alignment

Ensure every team member, not just leadership, understands how business goals cascade to marketing goals and campaigns. Make this visible in weekly one-on-ones and team meetings. This allows individual contributors to understand where their work fits in the puzzle, see how they are moving the needle, and make better decisions about trade-offs. Without this, team members feel like they are just executing tasks without purpose. (Source: Hannak Rankin, Episode #210)

Work with your marketing leadership team to co-create a manifesto that defines how your marketing team operates and collaborates. This is distinct from corporate values but specific to marketing culture. Use this manifesto as a starting point for team alignment and decision-making, and to cascade strategy and priorities down through the organization. (Source: Kimberly Storin, Episode #229)

## Async Communication Tools

Use Loom videos with emoji reactions enabled so viewers can provide feedback asynchronously without needing a meeting. Emoji reactions signal understanding, agreement, confusion, or concern, giving you a sense of how the message landed while still respecting async work patterns. (Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)

Build or use AI agents that help people communicate in more structured ways—taking unstructured thoughts or implicit knowledge and formatting them into clear, concise communication. This is more useful than simple summarization because it preserves the context and insight that only you have while applying structure and clarity. (Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)

## Employee Recognition Programs

Prioritize public recognition as the number-one non-financial motivator. Research on non-financial rewards shows public recognition and communication ranks first in motivating employees. Use channels like newsletters, town halls, Slack channels, and social walls to make recognition visible across the organization. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)

Design your recognition program to highlight and reward employees who demonstrate your company's core values in their work and behavior, not just performance metrics. Values-driven recognition creates a culture where employees understand what behaviors matter most and are motivated to embody them. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)

Create an annual awards program where employees vote on peers who exemplify company values. Use simple tools like Google Forms to collect nominations, set up a dedicated website for voting, and announce winners at a company meeting or event. This leverages peer recognition, makes the program feel inclusive, and creates a memorable annual tradition. Minimal technology investment is required. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)

Treat your recognition program like a marketing initiative: create a program name, develop a logo and visual identity, design e-cards or visual assets, build a dedicated webpage or microsite on your intranet, and establish distribution channels such as Slack, email newsletters, town halls, and social walls. Marketing teams should lead the creative and distribution aspects in collaboration with HR. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)

Assign recognition ambassadors on each team and challenge them to send a specific number of recognition messages—for example, two e-cards every Friday. Once peer recognition starts flowing, introduce gamification elements like inter-departmental competitions for giving or receiving recognition. This drives organic participation and makes recognition a cultural norm rather than a top-down mandate. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not neglect jobs two and three of the CMO role.** Spending all your time running marketing while ignoring internal positioning and CEO-level business involvement is a primary cause of CMO failure. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
- **Do not treat internal presentations as a distraction from real marketing work.** They are trust-building activities that directly enable your ability to execute strategy and gain organizational buy-in. (Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episodes #302 and #193)
- **Do not share marketing metrics with non-marketers unless they will take action on them.** CTRs and impressions mean nothing to an engineer or product leader. Connect data to outcomes they care about. (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)
- **Do not re-send full documents when managing up.** Extract the three relevant highlights instead of forcing your boss to re-read everything. (Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)
- **Do not batch customer intelligence into formal weekly reports.** Share insights in real-time and attach them to existing decision forums where they will actually be used. (Source: Jennifer Cannizzaro, Episode #267)
- **Do not let research findings sit in a slide deck.** Actively distribute them through newsletters, presentations, and repositories. (Source: Haley Carpenter, Episode #282)
- **Do not rely on Slack channels alone for event or campaign updates.** Slack becomes noise; use team meetings for visibility and engagement at scale. (Source: Kristina DeBrito, Episode #227)
- **Do not deliver status-update-style presentations in cross-functional meetings.** Use storytelling and narrative arc to generate genuine interest and voluntary support from other teams. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episodes #283 and #199)
- **Do not use unclear or indirect language internally.** Clarity is a form of kindness and signals respect for people's time. (Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)
- **Do not run recognition programs as top-down mandates.** Use peer recognition, ambassadors, and gamification to make participation organic and culturally embedded. (Source: Rachel Weeks, Episode #273)
- **Do not combine category training and brand training into a single session during a rebrand.** Separate them to prevent confusion and enable independent measurement. (Source: Clare Schmitt, Episode #333)
- **Do not keep goal visibility limited to leadership.** Every individual contributor needs to understand how their work connects to business goals. (Source: Hannak Rankin, Episode #210)
