# LinkedIn Content Strategy for B2B Marketers

## Overview
This skill covers how to build and execute a LinkedIn content strategy for B2B brands and executives, including posting cadence, content formats, audience building, engagement tactics, amplification, and measurement. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast. No general best practices have been added beyond what guests explicitly recommended.

---

## Foundational Philosophy: How to Think About LinkedIn

**Start with the question: "What would help someone not want to cry at their job today?"** Develop a content strategy rooted in empathy and utility. Post tips, insights, and encouragement aimed at helping your audience feel less lost or overwhelmed. Authentic followership builds over time through genuine usefulness — Jess Cook built to 37k followers over nearly 4 years using this approach. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #321)

**Build LinkedIn engagement through value-first content, then promote when you have something to announce.** If you post ten promotional posts in a row, none will drive significant registrations. But if you post good content repeatedly and then promote, that promotional post will drive hundreds of registrations. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #205)

**Use a 5:1 or 6:1 ratio of value-add content to direct response content.** LinkedIn is not a direct-response channel. Use a "give, give, give, ask" approach where 5–6 out of every 6 posts provide value, build trust, and share perspectives, while only 1 post is a direct call-to-action. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #171)

**Treat LinkedIn like a personal or company blog, not a social media feed.** Write regularly about topics relevant to your audience, build compounding credibility over time, and focus on what works on the platform. Dave's account generated 17 million impressions in 365 days with ~200k followers using this approach. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #316)

**Identify your differentiation angle before committing to a content strategy.** Write down the one thing you know about your market that most people in your space get wrong or won't say publicly — that's your differentiation angle. If you can't articulate it in one sentence, you don't have one yet. Generic, vanilla content won't break through the noise. Your differentiation could be radical transparency about financials and challenges, sharing lessons from past roles that relate to your ICP's pain points, or a unique point of view on your market. Without a clear angle, your content will be "okay" at best — useful for hiring and brand awareness, but not a growth lever. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Expect 12 months to achieve content-market fit on LinkedIn.** Plan for a 12-month runway before your LinkedIn content strategy reliably drives business results. In the early months (0–9 months), your content may build awareness and engagement without directly sourcing sales demos or signups. Around month 9–12, you should begin to see a clear correlation between content performance and business metrics. Founders who quit after 3–4 months of low direct results are leaving money on the table. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

## Posting Cadence and Frequency

*(Note: Guests disagree meaningfully on posting frequency and scheduling approach — see "Where Experts Disagree" for the full breakdown before choosing a cadence.)*

**Post at least 2x per week on LinkedIn to drive a 5x increase in profile views.** This frequency keeps your profile and content visible in your network's feed, builds familiarity, and signals active engagement on the platform. Higher profile views increase the likelihood that prospects will visit your profile and become aware of your company or offerings. (Source: Davang Shah, Episode #338)

**For a sustainable middle ground, post 3 times per week.** This is a viable cadence for CEOs and founders who cannot commit to daily posting. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**Post 5–7 times per week when starting out to gather sufficient performance data.** This frequency is necessary to gather enough data on what hooks, topics, and formats work for your audience. Posting only twice per month creates a 10x slower feedback loop and prevents you from building the posting habit. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Implement a repeatable LinkedIn growth system: post twice daily, engage with ten specific users daily, and spend at least 30 minutes responding to comments on your own posts immediately after publishing.** This creates a consistent, measurable approach to growing followers and engagement. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episodes #244 and #158)

**Before launching a product, commit to posting twice daily on LinkedIn for a fixed period (e.g., 6 months) to build audience and early customer base.** Emir's team converted 35–40 LinkedIn followers into early conversations and customers by launch day using this approach. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Treat LinkedIn posting as a daily habit, not a short-term campaign.** Schedule it as the first thing you do each morning (e.g., before checking email or Slack). The benefits — recruiting, partnerships, customer relationships — emerge naturally over time, not from individual viral posts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**On posting time: Dave Gerhardt recommends the 6–9 AM Eastern window (#298); Tommy Clark recommends 9–11 AM Eastern (#171). Both guests agree that morning posting outperforms afternoon or evening. Either window is defensible — pick one and stay consistent.**

## Content Mix and Format Strategy

**Structure a weekly LinkedIn posting plan with a mix of content types.** Allocate roughly 2–3 posts per week to thought leadership or industry insights relevant to your customers, 1 post to personal/non-business content (stories, lessons, quotes, photos), and reserve promotional content for specific campaigns. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #317)

**Organize your LinkedIn content strategy using a three-tier content funnel:**
- Top-of-funnel (1 out of 5–6 posts): Broad business/entrepreneurship content that helps the algorithm but shouldn't dominate your feed.
- Middle-of-funnel (bulk of content): Industry-specific thought leadership directly related to your core expertise.
- Bottom-of-funnel (1 out of 5–6 posts): Direct response content like feature announcements, demo requests, or calls to action.

(Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Balance evergreen content with timely, trend-jacking posts.** Evergreen content prevents the blank-slate problem and provides consistent value. Timely content allows you to capitalize on relevant industry news. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Focus your LinkedIn content on a specific niche rather than posting about multiple unrelated topics.** Niche focus drives faster growth and audience building than generalist content. You can sprinkle in personal anecdotes that tie back to your core topic. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

**Identify 2–3 content pillars that work well on LinkedIn and test each across multiple formats: video, poll, short-form text, long-form text.** Track which formats perform best for each pillar. This creates a repeatable system for generating content rather than starting from scratch each time. (Source: Dan Cmejla, Episode #134)

**Use a "reach versus teach" framework to balance your content mix.** Reach content is designed to get engagement and impressions (e.g., personal moments, off-site updates, relatable observations). Teach content is directly tied to your core expertise. Test both types, measure which performs better, and iterate. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Incorporate personal, relatable posts about founder life experiences alongside business content.** These posts often outperform purely business content in engagement and help build deeper audience connection. Personal posts should reflect genuine experiences and strong opinions. (Source: Kait Stephens, Episode #156)

**Prioritize content and awareness over direct product promotion on LinkedIn.** Spend 80–90% of your posting on content that builds authority and awareness, and 10–20% on direct promotion. The promotional posts work because they're backed by the credibility and audience you've built through non-promotional content. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

## Writing and Voice

**Authentic, human-written content outperforms AI-generated or polished content as AI slop increases.** Dave has noticed his own LinkedIn performance improving as he writes more casually from his phone with natural language and occasional typos, contrasting with the rise of generic AI-generated content. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #316)

**Write LinkedIn content off-the-cuff with typos and irregular capitalization to stand out from AI-generated content.** Lean into personal writing style rather than attempting to match polished, professional tone that AI can replicate. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**Inject specific personal details and authentic context into posts.** Add small, specific personal details — mention you're writing from 37,000 feet on a JetBlue flight, reference what you're wearing, describe a specific moment from your day. These details signal that the post was written by a real person and cannot be replicated by AI. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Apply proven LinkedIn hook templates to increase post performance.** Effective hooks include definitive statements (e.g., "The golden age of SaaS is over"), cliffhangers with future implications, and contrarian takes. Templates improve engagement significantly compared to rambling posts. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Write LinkedIn posts with zero-click value to work with the algorithm and drive engagement.** LinkedIn's algorithm penalizes posts with external links by reducing their reach. Write posts that deliver a complete, standalone insight without requiring a click. The engagement loop — more engagement → more reach → more followers — compounds over time. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #205)

**When promoting a webinar on social media, lead with a specific, actionable insight that attendees will learn — not a teaser that requires clicking to understand the value.** For example, instead of "What are you going to do about your content strategy? Find out in our webinar," write "Do this one thing to make sure you have a really solid content strategy. By the way, we're also going to give you four more pieces of advice in our webinar." (Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**When promoting a webinar, article, or resource on LinkedIn, write a post that delivers value on its own and can stand alone without the link.** The post should address a related topic or tell a story that makes the reader want to learn more, then include the link as a natural next step. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episodes #262 and #172)

**Shrink focus to 1–2 core topics rather than writing about everything.** Develop a clear point of view in your industry with a strong narrative. Win with depth — focus on expertise and industry knowledge rather than memes or witty content if that's not natural to you. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**Leverage LinkedIn's text-based format as a low-friction entry point for B2B social.** You can build a successful following and drive business results using only text posts. This makes it scalable to empower multiple team members to post without requiring video production skills or equipment. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #171)

## Video Content on LinkedIn

**Prioritize video content on LinkedIn as the algorithm heavily favors it.** Video is prominently featured as a button on the LinkedIn homepage and mobile app. If you want to maximize reach on LinkedIn, focus on creating and distributing video content. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Use horizontal video format on LinkedIn for better engagement, not vertical.** Despite the industry trend toward vertical video on social platforms, LinkedIn's algorithm and user experience actually favor horizontal videos. When you post a vertical video on LinkedIn, it displays poorly in the feed (just showing your face with no context). (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #304)

**The three video formats with the strongest performance on LinkedIn in 2025 are:**
1. Building in public — showing how your business is actually doing and what you're working on.
2. Edutainment — educational content presented in an engaging, fun way (e.g., filming outside instead of at a desk).
3. Collaborations — partnering with other creators or brands to share audiences and expand reach.

(Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Add text overlay thumbnails to LinkedIn videos for hook and brand recognition.** Create a consistent visual identity by adding a text overlay at the start (first 2 seconds) with a title or hook. Use a consistent color scheme so viewers immediately recognize your content. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

**Aim for posting LinkedIn videos twice per day as your ideal frequency.** Consistency matters more than perfection; posting frequently with good-enough content outperforms posting rarely with perfect content. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

## Content Ideation and Sourcing

**Mine internal communications and conversations for LinkedIn content.** Systematically convert internal work communications into LinkedIn posts. When you write Slack messages, internal memos, quarterly updates, or answers to team questions, identify sections that could be repurposed as LinkedIn content. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Extract LinkedIn content ideas from your weekly meetings and work activities.** At the end of each week, review your calendar and meetings to identify takeaways, highlights, or frustrations that can become LinkedIn posts. Build a swipe file in Apple Notes, Google Docs, or Notion to capture post ideas as they occur throughout the day. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Use LinkedIn comments as prompts for longer-form posts.** When you write a thoughtful comment on someone else's LinkedIn post that resonates, save it. Later, expand that comment into a full 500+ word post for your own LinkedIn profile. Maintain a document of high-performing comments to reference when planning content. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #317)

**Mine LinkedIn comments for high-performing post ideas.** When you write a comment on someone else's LinkedIn post that receives more engagement than the original post (e.g., your comment gets 103 likes while the post has 66), save that comment as a future post idea. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #225)

**Generate LinkedIn post ideas by writing your own opinion on relevant industry conversations without directly mentioning the original poster.** Identify posts from relevant industry voices on topics you care about. Rather than engaging directly, write your own separate post sharing your perspective on the same topic. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Create a spreadsheet tracking the most engaged LinkedIn content in your niche.** For each piece of content, record the number of likes and comments. Sort the spreadsheet by top comments and top likes to identify recurring themes and topics that resonate with your audience. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Analyze competitor LinkedIn pages to identify high-performing content formats.** Use LinkedIn's built-in competitor analytics feature to identify which brands are achieving the highest engagement. Review their content patterns to identify what formats work. Peep discovered that memes generated 10–100x higher engagement than other content types by analyzing competitor pages. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Use LinkedIn post engagement as a buying signal for outbound prospecting.** Monitor comments and reactions on relevant LinkedIn posts (from your company, competitors, or influencers) to identify people in-market for your solution. Extract the list of commenters/reactors, filter for ICP fit, and run personalized outbound plays that reference the specific post. (Source: Kevin White, Episode #179)

## Content Production Workflow and Systems

**Maintain a backlog of 500+ post ideas to draw from on days when inspiration doesn't strike.** Keep a separate backlog (Apple Notes or Google Doc) and add to it continuously. This ensures consistent posting without relying on daily ideation. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Maintain a Notion document called "social backlog" where you capture content ideas from comments, conversations, and observations throughout the week.** Once per week, sit down for 1 hour and batch-write 4–5 LinkedIn posts using these prompts as starting points. Post twice daily (e.g., 7 AM and 3 PM Eastern) to maintain consistent presence. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**Establish a structured weekly posting and content production workflow.** Dedicate one day (e.g., Tuesday) to writing 3–4 posts in a Google Doc, schedule a 1-hour idea generation meeting with a content consultant or ghostwriter, and block time for video production (e.g., 1-hour video shoot weekly). Maintain a running Google Doc (150+ pages) where you continuously add post ideas and drafts. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Conduct a 30-minute weekly interview with your CEO or founder on hot industry topics, record the call, transcribe their insights, and convert the interview into 2–3 LinkedIn posts.** Send the drafted posts to the CEO for tone review before posting. This reduces the time burden on the CEO while ensuring authentic voice and consistent content generation. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #131)

**Use AI as a writing assistant to enhance your own content, not replace it.** Feed AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT) your own past posts and voice memos to build a personalized writing style guide, then use AI to help flesh out ideas, add research, and refine hooks — but always write the core content yourself. Never publish AI-generated content wholesale; inject personal details, opinions, and authentic voice that AI cannot replicate. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Commit to carousel posts as your main content format if it suits your style.** Post 3 times per week maximum to maintain quality and avoid audience fatigue. Create carousels yourself to preserve personality and control — do not outsource or scale this to maintain authenticity. Schedule posts 1 week in advance to allow flexibility for timely content. (Source: Sara Lattanzio, Episode #268)

Only schedule posts when you need to batch-write content for consistency, accepting a 10–20% reach reduction as a tradeoff. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #225)

## Repurposing and Recycling Content

**Identify your top-performing posts using LinkedIn Analytics or Shield, then repost them on a recurring schedule (e.g., every 3–6 months).** Most of your followers will not have seen the original post due to feed ephemerality, and the content will perform well again. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Repost high-performing content on a 6-month cycle.** Use LinkedIn's analytics to identify your highest-engagement posts, then schedule them to repost 6 months later. Most of your audience will not have seen the original post (LinkedIn reaches ~10% of followers per post on average). (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #262 and #172)

**Use analytics tools (e.g., Shield analytics) to identify high-performing content from 8–12 months prior, then repost it.** This leverages the Ogilvy principle that you're marketing to a "marching parade," not a standing army — each cohort of new audience members needs to encounter your message multiple times. Reposting is not lazy; it's efficient message reinforcement. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #198 and #123)

**Repurpose top-performing posts by updating the headline, hook, or framing while keeping the core story intact.** This approach reduces the pressure to constantly create new content and allows proven content to reach new audiences. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #156)

## Engagement and Community Building

**Start with commenting on others' posts before publishing your own.** For executives new to LinkedIn, begin by spending 2–3 months actively engaging with others' content (commenting on 5 posts daily) before publishing original posts. This builds familiarity with the platform and establishes some initial credibility. (Source: Finn Thormeier, Episode #317)

**Execute a daily engagement routine: comment on 10 ICPs, post, comment on 10 more, respond to replies within one hour.** This routine signals activity to the algorithm, builds relationships with key accounts, and creates content ideas from engagement. Assign a team member to execute the commenting on behalf of the founder, but ensure the founder responds personally to comments on their own posts. (Source: Brad Zomick, Episode #156)

**Post content on LinkedIn and actively encourage your team to comment within the first hour of publication.** LinkedIn's algorithm prioritizes posts with high comment velocity in the first hour, so orchestrating team engagement during this window significantly increases reach. (Source: David Kline, Episode #138)

**Build your network by strategically commenting on target audience posts.** Actively comment on posts from people you want to be associated with or learn from. Some practitioners have grown 10,000+ followers primarily through high-quality commenting. Top comments can also surface ideas for your own posts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #138)

**Actively comment on other people's LinkedIn posts and reply to comments on your own posts, as this engagement signals to the algorithm and increases post reach.** Additionally, use comments you write on others' posts as content ideation — when a comment you write gains traction, add it to your content backlog as a potential full post topic. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**Respond to all comments on your post within the first hour.** This signals activity to the platform and boosts post performance. (Source: Brad Zomick, Episode #156)

## Personal vs. Company Page Strategy

**Prioritize personal executive profiles over company pages for LinkedIn content.** Personal executive profiles receive approximately 5x more views than company pages on LinkedIn. If your CEO or executive is willing to be active, build the strategy around their personal profile rather than the company page. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**When commenting on others' posts or building presence on LinkedIn, use personal accounts (CEO, founder, team members) rather than company accounts.** Personal accounts generate stronger relationship-building and affinity than company accounts. Company accounts can feel faceless and impersonal. (Source: Dasha Shakov, Episode #317)

**Use your company LinkedIn page as a news feed for all content and product updates, with zero-click value.** Post all company content — webinars, blog posts, product launches — to the company LinkedIn page. Employees then amplify by reposting from their personal pages with their own perspective. This two-layer approach increases reach and engagement while maintaining a consistent company voice. (Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**Treat your company LinkedIn page as a news ticker, not a primary content channel.** Use it to post company news, milestones, webinar announcements, and event details — but don't expect it to drive meaningful business impact. Focus your real engagement and content strategy on personal profiles of 1–3 key executives. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #139 and #131)

**Post the same content (e.g., a carousel post) simultaneously on both your personal LinkedIn page and company page to measure relative engagement.** Track engagement rate (not just absolute numbers) to account for follower count differences. In one test, a personal page with 3,800 followers generated 39% higher engagement than a company page with 67,000 followers on identical content. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #131)

## Executive and Founder LinkedIn Strategy

**Develop founder or executive thought leadership on LinkedIn if your target customers are on the platform.** Founders have authenticity and interesting things to say. Help them humanize their voice and post regularly. If your customers are not on LinkedIn or you operate in a vertical with strong trade media, prioritize PR and trade media instead. (Source: Ido Mart, Episode #229)

**Build CEO LinkedIn presence using five specific tactics:**
1. Shrink focus to 1–2 core topics rather than writing about everything.
2. Develop a clear point of view in your industry with a strong narrative.
3. Win with depth — focus on expertise and industry knowledge.
4. Write 3 posts per week as a sustainable middle ground.
5. Treat LinkedIn as your industry blog where you write about work and lessons learned.

(Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**Rather than ghostwriting comments as the executive, highlight high-value commenters and mentions to the executive to encourage them to engage directly.** Send the executive a message pointing out when target personas (e.g., VPs, CEOs, prospects) are commenting on their posts. Avoid logging in as the executive to comment on their behalf, as this blurs the line between content creation and impersonation. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Enable LinkedIn Creator Mode on the executive's profile to unlock the ability to pin gated content in the featured section.** This creates a prominent visual that drives profile visitors to download gated assets. Additionally, upgrade to LinkedIn Premium to add a website link in the profile. Use this link to drive traffic to a gated resource (e.g., newsletter signup, whitepaper). (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Post regularly on LinkedIn with authentic, strong points of view relevant to your ICP.** Track immediate engagement (likes, comments, warm connection requests) as a leading indicator that your positioning resonates. Use engagement and warm inbound connections as proof that your message is landing. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #259)

## Audience Building and Distribution

**Before ramping up content creation, systematically connect with people on a target account list (named accounts, event attendees, people who commented on relevant posts).** Use personalized connection messages (e.g., "Hi, I'm [Name], the [descriptor], excited to learn from you and your network"). This pre-work ensures that when you start posting, your target audience is already opted in to see your content. (Source: Brad Zomick, Episode #156)

**Systematically connect with 500–1,000 target customers on LinkedIn before launching content campaigns.** This creates an opted-in audience that sees your content in their feed when employees and executives post. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

**Track follower quality weekly — check if new followers match your ICP by title and company.** LinkedIn followers built through consistent, relevant content that your target customers actually want to see represent an opted-in audience that has signaled interest in your content. This is different from a meme page trying to sell B2B software. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #180)

**Once the founder is posting consistently, expand LinkedIn presence by getting other team members (CMO, head of brand, sales leaders, customer success managers) to post regularly.** This creates multiple touchpoints in the feed, makes the company appear "everywhere," and brings diverse perspectives to your audience. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Create a LinkedIn content strategy where the founder, marketing leader, and other team members post regularly about lessons learned from their daily work.** Each person should post in their authentic voice and lane — founder posts about sales strategy, marketer posts about data and facts, SDR posts with personality and humor. (Source: Taylor Udell, Episode #190)

**Focus 97–100% of your social effort on LinkedIn before attempting to build presence on other platforms.** LinkedIn has enough sub-channels (founder account, company page, team member accounts, video, text) to keep you busy for years. Spreading effort across multiple platforms from the start dilutes your impact. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Run weekly persona-based title takeovers to expand product relevance perception.** Change company LinkedIn profile title daily or weekly to highlight how the product serves different personas (e.g., "Best Friend of Sales," "Best Friend of Marketing," "Best Friend of RevOps"). This educates the market about product use cases beyond the primary perception while creating novelty that drives engagement. (Source: Dan Cmejla, Episode #134)

## Measurement and Analytics

**Track LinkedIn content performance using these metrics:**
1. Post publishes — benchmark 3 per week (36 annually); target 20% lift from baseline.
2. Impressions — benchmark 10–15% lift (accounting for algorithm variability).
3. Content downloads — enable creator mode and gate content in the featured section; track gated content downloads as a lead source.
4. Website traffic from organic — measure clicks from LinkedIn profile link (requires LinkedIn Premium).
5. MQLs from gated content — specifically track MQLs that originate from downloading gated content on the LinkedIn profile.
6. Self-attributed pipeline — track mentions of the executive's name in self-reported attribution.

Review metrics weekly for wins and learnings, every two weeks for performance trends, and monthly for deep dives into top and low-performing posts. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Measure LinkedIn success across two buckets:**
1. Social metrics (leading indicators): follower growth, impressions, and follower quality (check if new followers match your target ICP by title and company). These should trend upward week-over-week if your content strategy is working.
2. Business metrics (lagging indicators): inbound demos, sales calls, and mentions of your LinkedIn content in sales conversations. Business metrics may take 1–3 months to materialize, so don't abandon the channel if you don't see immediate sales.

(Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Recognize that high-engagement posts (likes, comments) don't always correlate with conversions or pipeline impact.** A post promoting a specific offer may have low engagement (30 likes, 3 comments) but generate significant contacts or pipeline. Track both metrics separately and understand that different content types serve different purposes in the funnel. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #317)

**Create a spreadsheet plotting weekly demo bookings (or your key conversion metric) against LinkedIn organic impressions over a 52-week period.** Map both metrics on the same chart to identify correlation patterns. Present this quantified relationship to finance teams as evidence of channel effectiveness. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #191)

**Don't rely solely on like counts to measure content performance.** Read the comments and assess who is commenting (is it your ICP? Are they customers? Are they competitors?). A post with 500 likes and 30 comments from your target customers is more valuable than a post with 500 likes and 30 generic comments. Look for patterns like "this CMO has never commented before but just did" — that's a signal of resonance. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #139)

**Optimize for comment quality and ICP relevance rather than raw impressions.** Track which posts attract comments from your actual ideal customer profile versus broader audiences. A post with lower impressions but comments from decision-makers is more valuable than a post with high impressions but comments from tangential audiences. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

## Amplification and Paid Strategy

**Identify organic posts that perform well (50+ likes, strong engagement from target audience), then advertise those same posts to specific target accounts and job titles using LinkedIn's Thought Leader Ads feature.** This approach leverages authentic, already-validated content and appears organic while being promoted by your company. You can edit the post before advertising to add links or UTM parameters for tracking. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

**Use LinkedIn content to create demand awareness and credibility, then layer a targeted outbound sales motion on top (inbound-led outbound).** Identify high-value prospects who engage with your content or visit your website, then use a quick 2-touch sales sequence to book demos with people you're 99% confident will convert. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Use semi-automated LinkedIn DM sequences to convert engaged commenters into leads.** Identify high-value prospects who comment on your posts (especially those matching your ICP), and trigger a semi-automated DM sequence that thanks them for the comment and introduces your product/service. Organize incoming messages into separate folders to keep your inbox manageable. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Use LinkedIn post engagement as a buying signal for outbound prospecting.** Monitor comments and reactions on relevant LinkedIn posts (from your company, competitors, or influencers) to identify people in-market for your solution. Extract the list of commenters/reactors, filter for ICP fit, and run personalized outbound plays that reference the specific post and quote comments from other engaged users. (Source: Kevin White, Episode #179)

## Where Experts Disagree

### Posting Frequency: How Often Should You Post?

Guests recommend a wide range of posting frequencies, and the right answer depends on your goals, resources, and stage.

**The positions:**

- **2x per week minimum** — Davang Shah (Episode #338) recommends at least two posts per week as a foundational baseline to drive a 5x increase in profile views.
- **3x per week** — Dave Gerhardt (Episode #131) recommends three posts per week as a sustainable middle ground for CEOs and founders.
- **Once daily (5–7x per week)** — Tommy Clark (Episode #171) recommends posting at minimum 3x per week, ideally 5–7x per week, to gather sufficient performance data. Dave Gerhardt (Episodes #317, #225, #298) also clusters around once daily as his personal practice.
- **Twice daily** — Amanda Goetz (Episodes #244, #158) recommends posting twice daily as part of a repeatable growth system. Tim Davidson (Episode #127) recommends twice-daily video posting as the ideal frequency. Emir Atli (Episode #165) recommends twice daily for 6 months pre-launch.

**Support summary:** Multiple Gerhardt episodes cluster around once daily as a practical baseline. Twice daily is the aggressive growth mode recommended by Goetz and Davidson. Three times per week is the sustainable floor for executives. Two times per week is the minimum to see any meaningful profile view lift.

**Context dependency:** Twice daily is most appropriate for founders in aggressive audience-building phases or pre-launch. Once daily is the standard for established practitioners. Three times per week is a defensible floor for executives with limited time. The right cadence also depends on content quality — posting twice daily with weak content is worse than posting three times per week with strong content.

**Practical synthesis:** Start at 3x per week to build the habit and gather data. Move to once daily when you have a content backlog and workflow in place. Consider twice daily only if you have a dedicated content system and are in an active growth phase.

### Scheduling: Manual Posting vs. Native Scheduler vs. Third-Party Tools

Guests also disagree on how to handle scheduling, and the positions are genuinely contradictory.

**The positions:**

- **Post manually, avoid third-party tools** — Tommy Clark (Episode #171) and Dave Gerhardt (Episode #298) both recommend posting directly to LinkedIn rather than using third-party scheduling tools, citing algorithmic throttling as a risk. Gerhardt notes that scheduled posts may receive less engagement than posts published when the account owner is actively present.

## What NOT To Do

**Do not use automation tools to post comments on others' LinkedIn posts.** Automated comments violate LinkedIn's terms of service, risk account suspension, and damage relationships by being obviously inauthentic. Instead, manually comment with genuine, thoughtful responses. (Source: Finn Thormeier, Episode #317)

**Do not post only promotional content.** If every post is promotional, the channel won't work. The sales happen through osmosis — repeated exposure, trust-building, and eventual conversion — not through hard sells. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #171)

**Do not expect immediate sales results from LinkedIn.** Don't abandon the channel if you don't see direct sales after 3 weeks. Commit to 6–12 months of consistent posting before evaluating whether the channel works for your business. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #171; Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Do not spread effort across multiple platforms from the start.** Spreading effort across multiple platforms dilutes your impact and prevents you from reaching the depth needed to see results on any single channel. Master LinkedIn first. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Do not rely solely on like counts to measure content performance.** Assess who is commenting and whether they match your ICP. Generic engagement is not the same as engagement from your target buyers. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #139)

**Do not ghostwrite comments as the executive.** Logging in as the executive to comment on their behalf blurs the line between content creation and impersonation. Instead, highlight high-value commenters to the executive and encourage them to engage directly. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Do not publish AI-generated content wholesale.** Always inject personal details, opinions, and authentic voice that AI cannot replicate. Use AI as a writing assistant, not a content generator. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Do not post vertical video on LinkedIn.** Despite the industry trend toward vertical video on social platforms, LinkedIn's algorithm and user experience actually favor horizontal videos. Vertical videos display poorly in the feed. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #304)

**Do not lead with the link when promoting a resource.** Posts that lead with the call-to-action reduce reach because the content itself is not compelling enough to generate engagement. Write a post that delivers value on its own first. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episodes #262 and #172; Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**Do not blame the algorithm when content underperforms.** When LinkedIn content underperforms, the issue is typically content quality rather than algorithm dysfunction. A strong, newsworthy post can achieve 1,426 likes and 25,000 impressions even on a Friday morning (suboptimal timing). Focus on creating genuinely interesting content rather than blaming platform mechanics. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #316)
