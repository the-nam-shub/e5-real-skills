# Cross-Functional Launch Alignment

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketing teams can align with product, sales, engineering, customer success, and other functions to plan and execute product launches and campaigns effectively. It addresses relationship-building, meeting cadences, stakeholder engagement, and launch structure. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 6 episodes.

---

## Strategic Framing: Launches as Organizational Forcing Functions

**Treat product launches as artificial deadlines that rally the entire company — not just marketing events.**

Schedule tier-one launches a couple of times per year, even when the features feel incremental from a product marketing perspective. The launch date itself is the mechanism: it forces product, engineering, design, sales, CS, and marketing to commit to shipping and stop iterating endlessly. Use launches strategically by bundling announcements — for example, pairing a fundraising announcement with a new feature launch on the same day — to maximize impact and create a shared organizational moment. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

## Building the Right Relationships Before You Need Them

**Invest in the CPO/head of product relationship from day one, not when a launch is already in motion.**

Know how they take their coffee. Invite them to marketing meetings — mark attendance as optional but make clear their presence matters. Include them in keynote development and press release discussions. Map out the launch roadmap together with their input. This relationship is foundational to aligning product and marketing; if it doesn't exist early, it becomes significantly harder to establish later. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)

**Identify key internal influencers across teams before launch planning begins.**

Before a launch kicks off formally, map the relevant stakeholders across sales, product, customer success, and engineering. Rather than sending a broadcast announcement, meet with these individuals individually to get their input and buy-in. Ask each person how they'd like to stay connected to the work — weekly meeting, Slack updates, email — and let them choose their own engagement level. This prevents the "surprise launch" problem where teams feel blindsided and reduces conflicting priorities or lack of readiness at launch time. (Source: Zach Roberts, Episode #163)

## Planning Cadences: Long-Range and Launch-Specific

**Run biannual cross-functional campaign theme planning meetings six months in advance.**

Convene marketing, sales, product, and partner teams twice per year to align on campaign themes for the next six months. Inputs should include the product roadmap, major events, M&A roadmap, and strategic priorities. The output is a short list of themes that will guide marketing campaigns and cross-functional initiatives. The six-month lead time gives teams enough runway to plan major initiatives while preserving quarterly agility for reactive campaigns. Themes may or may not become formal campaigns, but they ensure alignment across functions before execution begins. (Source: Gurdeep Dhillon, Episodes #280 and #203)

**Hold a cross-functional launch kickoff meeting early — not the week before launch.**

Schedule a 30-minute meeting with representatives from marketing, product, sales, engineering, and customer success well in advance of the launch date. In this meeting, present: the launch date, what is being launched, what is currently in progress, and the full list of deliverables marketing will create for each team. Early communication prevents surprises, allows each team to plan their own work around the launch, and increases buy-in because everyone understands the full picture and timeline upfront. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #163)

## Staying Aligned During Launch Execution

**Run daily standups between marketing and product teams for 4–6 weeks leading up to major launches.**

Establish synchronous standups at the beginning and end of each day between marketing leadership, product leadership, design, and engineering during the final 4–6 weeks of launch preparation. Use these standups to shape the product in real time based on marketing feedback, give marketing early visibility into product decisions, and create a shared sense of ownership over the launch. This practice embeds marketing into product development rather than treating it as a downstream function, and ensures both teams are aligned on messaging and positioning before launch day. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)

---

## Pre-Launch Validation With Stakeholders

**Pre-validate campaign concepts with cross-functional stakeholders before launching — especially for unconventional or creative work.**

Before launching an influencer campaign or any campaign that is creative, humorous, or unconventional, bring the concept to your sales team, product team, CS, and other relevant stakeholders. Ask explicitly: Does this align with our customer base? Will our audience expect this? This step catches misalignment early and prevents siloed campaign planning. If a campaign idea carries risk — particularly humor, which is rare in B2B — validate it with your audience before launch rather than hoping for the best. (Source: Brianna Doe, Episode #305)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not treat launches as marketing-only events.** Launches are organizational mechanisms. Keeping them siloed within marketing forfeits their value as cross-functional forcing functions. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)
- **Do not hold the cross-functional kickoff meeting the week before launch.** By then, teams cannot meaningfully adjust their own plans, and the meeting becomes a notification rather than a coordination tool. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #163)
- **Do not send broadcast announcements to stakeholders instead of individual conversations.** Broadcast communication skips the buy-in step and increases the likelihood that key influencers feel blindsided or disengaged. (Source: Zach Roberts, Episode #163)
- **Do not wait to build the CPO relationship until a launch is already underway.** The relationship needs to exist before it is needed; trying to establish it mid-launch is significantly harder. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)
- **Do not launch unconventional or creative campaigns without cross-functional validation.** Skipping stakeholder pre-validation risks misalignment with the customer base and internal conflict after launch. (Source: Brianna Doe, Episode #305)
- **Do not treat marketing as a downstream function that receives product decisions after they are made.** Embedding marketing into product development through regular standups ensures messaging and positioning are shaped alongside the product, not after it. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)
