# Personal Productivity & Habits for B2B Marketers

## Overview
This skill covers personal productivity systems, daily routines, creative ideation practices, and habit-building strategies relevant to B2B marketers. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 6 episodes. No general productivity advice has been added — only what guests specifically described and recommended.

---

## Morning Routines & Physical Foundations

**Structure your morning around exercise before work begins.** Complete a full workout (45–60 minutes of weight lifting) plus a 3-mile hike, run, or walk before 9 AM. This requires waking at 5:30 AM and treating exercise as non-negotiable. The early accomplishment reduces stress and anxiety and makes the rest of the workday feel manageable by comparison. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**Use physical movement as a primary ideation tool.** When stuck on messaging or creative problems, take a run or walk instead of grinding at your desk. Ideas flow more naturally during movement than during forced brainstorming sessions. Capture ideas via voice memo during or immediately after movement. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Take a two-minute cold shower after exercise to reset and stay alert.** Set a timer for exactly two minutes. The cold exposure is uncomfortable but creates a measurable stress-reduction effect and keeps you alert for the rest of the day. A warm shower after exercise can lead to drowsiness and lingering — the cold shower serves as both a time boundary and an energizing tool. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295 and #234)

## Planning & Daily Structure

**Write daily priorities in a physical notebook before bed.** Each night, after kids are in bed or the day winds down, write out 3–4 big priorities for the next day. This gets tasks out of your head, reduces mental load, and allows you to be present and relaxed in the evening. The act of writing clarifies what matters most and creates a clear plan. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Schedule LinkedIn posting as the first thing you do each morning — before email or Slack.** Treat it as a long-term daily practice (like exercise), not a short-term campaign with immediate ROI expectations. The compounding benefits — recruiting, partnerships, customer relationships — emerge over months and years, not from individual posts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Bookmark AI tools prominently in your browser as a daily workflow reminder.** Place tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Perplexity in your top bookmarks bar. This creates a visual habit trigger: ask yourself "Is there an AI tool I could use for this?" before defaulting to manual processes. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

## Ideation & Creative Thinking

**Don't brainstorm on the spot — give yourself days to think before ideating.** When asked for ideas in a meeting, have a conversation about the problem, then give yourself 2–3 days (or longer) to think away from your desk. Ideas come when you're not forcing them. Set expectations with others that good ideas need incubation time. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Use handwriting and physical paper for ideation and thinking before moving to digital tools.** Handwriting engages your brain differently than typing and creates more free-flowing, less structured thinking. Use paper for prep notes, brainstorming, and initial drafts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Take physical notes during conversations even when calls are being recorded.** The physical act of writing helps your brain organize and retain information differently than just listening. You may never re-read the notes, but the act of writing improves recall and creates mental hooks — including for marketing content and newsletter topics. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Print customer research and read it on paper while annotating by hand.** When working with interview transcripts or research data, print them and take handwritten notes. This analog approach engages your brain differently than reading on screen and supports deeper absorption of insights. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episode #234)

## Journaling Practices

**Keep a daily recap journal documenting life and work.** Write a brief recap of each day — ranging from 2–3 lines to 3+ pages depending on time available — covering what happened, interesting moments, funny things said, and how you're feeling. Pair entries with photos from your phone on the same date to create a rich historical record. This also helps you process the day and track personal and professional growth over time. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #295 and #234)

**Use freeform journaling instead of structured gratitude or bullet-journal formats.** Avoid rigid formats if they feel forced. Write freely about what's on your mind, how you're feeling, and what's happening. If you're stressed or angry, write it out on paper to get it out of your body. Freeform journaling is more sustainable than following someone else's system. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

## Reading & Learning Habits

**Establish a rule: immediately buy any book recommended by someone you respect.** The cost ($12–20) is negligible compared to the potential value. This removes friction from the decision to invest in learning and ensures you capture high-signal recommendations before forgetting them. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**Use Kindle Whisper Sync to read the same book across multiple formats and contexts.** Read on Kindle at night; listen to the audiobook during gym sessions or commutes. The app syncs your position across both. This increases reading velocity by letting you consume the same content in different contexts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**Abandon books that aren't working — without guilt.** Reject the sunk-cost fallacy in reading. If a book isn't engaging, quit it. Treat reading as exploration: sample one chapter, extract value, and move on if needed. This removes friction and makes reading more enjoyable and sustainable. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

## Stress Management & Mental Clarity

**Practice meditation as simple sitting — no mantra, no guided audio required.** Sit for 10 minutes. When your brain races to tasks or worries, gently bring your attention back to the present moment. This simpler version is easier to sustain than structured approaches and still delivers stress reduction and mental clarity. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Use cold exposure (cold showers or cold plunges) as a stress regulation tool.** Take a 2-minute cold shower after exercise or as a standalone practice. The discomfort creates a measurable stress-reduction effect afterward and helps regulate your nervous system. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

## What NOT To Do

**Don't expect good ideas to come on the spot in meetings.** Forcing brainstorming in real time rarely produces your best thinking. Protect incubation time and set expectations accordingly. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Don't use rigid journaling formats if they feel forced.** Structured gratitude lists or bullet journals can become obligations that kill the habit. Freeform writing is more sustainable. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #295)

**Don't finish books out of obligation.** The sunk-cost fallacy applies to reading. Finishing a bad book wastes time that could be spent on a better one. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**Don't treat LinkedIn posting as a short-term campaign.** Expecting immediate ROI from individual posts leads to abandoning the practice before compounding benefits emerge. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Don't take a warm shower after midday exercise if you need to stay alert.** A warm shower after exercise encourages lingering and can cause drowsiness. Use a cold shower with a timer instead. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #234)

**Don't default to manual processes without first asking whether an AI tool could help.** Without a visual reminder system (like bookmarked tools), it's easy to miss opportunities to save time on repetitive work. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)
