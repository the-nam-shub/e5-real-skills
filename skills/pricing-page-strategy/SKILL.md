# Pricing Page Strategy

## Overview
This skill covers how to structure, write, and optimize B2B pricing pages for clarity and conversion. It addresses page architecture, copy, CTAs, social proof, objection handling, and the question of pricing transparency. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests; no general marketing knowledge has been added.

---

## Foundational Principles

### The Minimum Viable Pricing Page
Before optimizing details, ensure the page meets three baseline requirements: (1) pricing is visible on the page, (2) the page structure is clear and navigable, and (3) the product is positioned — visitors understand what it does. Meeting these three criteria alone puts you ahead of most competitors. Additional elements like social proof, testimonials, and detailed ROI stories are valuable but secondary to these fundamentals. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

Prioritize correct page structure over perfect pricing details. A well-structured page with clear sections, findable information, and visible pricing will convert better than a poorly structured page with perfect pricing logic. Focus first on having a usable, navigable page with prices visible; then refine the details. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

### The FAST Framework
Use the FAST framework as a systematic approach to audit and build pricing pages across four dimensions:
- **Focus on clarity** — main heading, package names, feature descriptions
- **Amplify confidence** — risk reversal, social proof, support/FAQ
- **Shape packaging** — top 3–5 differentiating features per plan, value metrics, pricing visibility
- **Trigger action** — consistent CTAs, one primary action, multiple placement opportunities

(Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Pricing Transparency

### Show Pricing Publicly
Display pricing clearly on the page. This can be a starting price, a range, or full pricing for all plans. Do not hide pricing behind "Contact Sales" or request forms. Make it easy for visitors to understand how they will scale with your product and what all possible charges are. Transparency builds trust and helps prospects self-qualify. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

Show pricing on your website at least for base tiers; enterprise can be labeled "Custom." Display full pricing for your standard or starter tiers. For enterprise or custom packages, label them as "Custom" or "Contact Sales" rather than hiding all pricing. This gives prospects a ballpark understanding of cost while acknowledging that high-end packages require negotiation. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

### Use Transparent Pricing as a Competitive Weapon
If your competitors hide pricing, showing yours becomes a competitive advantage. Use transparent pricing as a positioning statement: "Everyone else doesn't show you the pricing. We do. And this is why — because we want to be transparent and share with you how you grow." This can help you disrupt your market segment and level the playing field once competitors follow. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Overcoming Internal Objections to Showing Pricing
When stakeholders object to showing pricing (e.g., "Sales needs to explain it," "Competitors will find out," "We need to control who books meetings"), dig deeper to find the real problem. Often these objections mask underlying issues: unclear product positioning, overly complex pricing, or poor product marketing. Address the root cause rather than accepting the objection at face value. Test incrementally — for example, make pricing available via chat — to demonstrate that transparency works. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

To build sales team buy-in, first test showing pricing selectively to inbound prospects and measure the impact on close rates and sales cycle length. Present this data to the sales team. This approach proves that transparency accelerates deals rather than hurting them. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

### ICP Filtering When Full Pricing Cannot Be Shown
If you cannot show full pricing, at minimum define your ideal customer profile (ICP) on the pricing page. State something like "We work best with companies with 100+ employees." This filters out poor-fit prospects upfront and prevents wasted sales conversations. Replace vague "Request Pricing" CTAs with clear "Book a Demo" language. This is more honest and helps both you and prospects understand fit before engaging. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Page Heading and Positioning

### Lead with Value Proposition, Not a Generic Label
Replace generic pricing page headings like "Pricing," "Pricing Plans," or "Pick the Plan for You" with your actual value proposition. The heading is one of the hardest-working elements on your website; use it to communicate what the product does and who it's for, not to announce that pricing information follows. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Place Risk Reversal Messaging Immediately Below the Heading
Immediately below the main pricing page heading, include risk reversal messaging such as "Free trial," "No credit card required," or "Money-back guarantee." This builds trust right away and should not be buried elsewhere on the page. Make it visible and prominent to help visitors feel confident they're making a good choice. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Restate Product Positioning on the Pricing Page
Many visitors arrive at the pricing page directly via search or navigation without seeing your homepage. Restate your core positioning (what it is, who it's for, why it's better) at the top of the pricing page. Additionally, include a positioning statement for each plan — a sentence or two under the plan name explaining what's included, who it's for, and why they might need it. This prevents visitors from having to read fine print to understand the offering. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

### Include Pricing in Top Navigation
Place a link to your pricing page in the top navigation menu of your website. Pricing pages can be among the highest-converting pages on your site, and many visitors search directly for pricing. Making it easy to find in the top nav reduces friction and helps prospects self-qualify quickly. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

## Package Structure and Naming

### Use Descriptive Package Names
Move beyond generic tier names like "Basic," "Pro," and "Enterprise." Create package names that hint at who the package is for or what job it does. Combine this with a subheading underneath each package name to reinforce the target audience and what they'll be able to do. This is described as "probably the ultimate pricing page hack" because it makes downstream clarity and differentiation much easier. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Include Descriptive Subheadings for Each Plan
Below each pricing plan name, include a brief description that summarizes what the plan includes and who it's for. Examples: "Essential social listening and publishing capabilities" or "For small teams getting started." These descriptions help prospects quickly understand the purpose of each plan without having to read all the features. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

### Show Only Top 3–5 Differentiating Features Per Plan
Instead of displaying a full feature comparison grid on the main pricing page, show only the top 3–5 most important and differentiating features for each plan. Move the complete feature grid to a separate, collapsible lightbox with sticky headers that scroll. This prevents analysis paralysis for first-time visitors while still allowing deep exploration for those who want it. When visitors close the lightbox, they return to where they left off on the page. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

Make feature comparison grids collapsible and expandable. This allows first-time visitors to see the key differentiators without being overwhelmed, while still allowing power users to explore the full feature set. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

### Order Feature Lists by Importance and Differentiation
When listing features for each pricing tier, prioritize them by importance and differentiation value. Place the most important features at the top and continue driving differentiation down the list. This helps prospects understand what makes each plan different and guides them toward the right choice. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Display Add-Ons as a Separate Spanning Row
When offering add-ons (e.g., SMS communications, premium support), display them as a separate row that spans across all pricing plan columns. This makes it clear that the add-on is available to all plans and is distinct from the base plan features, preventing confusion about whether add-ons are exclusive to certain tiers. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)

## Feature Copy and Language

### Use Simple, Customer-Centric Language in Feature Descriptions
Avoid jargon, acronyms, and clever internal names in feature descriptions. Use language your customers already understand. If a term is something you created internally and requires explanation, spell it out instead. Keep feature descriptions simple and in the customer's language, even if space is tight. Apply a "jobs to be done" test: if you can look at a package and understand the job it's trying to get done, you've achieved good clarity. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Value Metrics and Pricing Logic

### Establish a Clear Value Metric and ROI Story
Every pricing page must communicate a clear value metric (what drives pricing) and an ROI story (what value the customer gets). Without this, prospects are left to imagine what they're paying for and why. The value metric should be visible upfront and should answer: "What am I buying more of as I scale?" The ROI story should answer: "What value do I get?" Examples include time saved, revenue made, or risk reduced. (Source: Karan Sood, Episode #216)

### Define Value Metrics That Align with Customer Value
A value metric must meet three criteria: (1) be incredibly close to the value your customer gets, (2) feel fair and familiar to them — something they already measure in their business — and (3) be predictable by the customer. Surface value metrics prominently at the top of the pricing page. If you have multiple value metrics, make sure all are visible upfront. When you align your pricing metric with customer success, your job becomes helping them be as successful as possible. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Annual vs. Monthly Pricing Display
If you offer an annual discount, decide whether to lead with annual or monthly pricing. Whichever you lead with, make sure the numbers are clean and easy to say (e.g., $49, $99 — not $41, $83). For discounts less than 25%, express the savings in months free (e.g., "Save 2 months") rather than as a percentage, as it is easier to digest. Let the other pricing option fall where it may mathematically. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Implement Localized Pricing for Non-US Markets
If you have significant traffic or business from countries outside the US, implement localized pricing in local currencies. Studies show that localized pricing converts better in those countries than non-localized pricing. This is a relatively easy win if you have the traffic to justify it. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Social Proof and Confidence Building

### Include Testimonials with Full Attribution for Each Package
For each pricing tier, include at least one testimonial from someone in the target audience. Each testimonial must include: a real photo, a strong quote, the person's name, their job title, and their company. This makes testimonials as real and identifiable as possible, allowing prospects to see themselves reflected in the page. Aim for one testimonial per package targeting that package's audience. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Objection Handling and Support

### Create a Comprehensive FAQ Section
Create a comprehensive FAQ section on the pricing page that addresses every question you've ever received about subscriptions and pricing. Examples include: "What happens if I upgrade during the middle of the month?", "What happens if I go over my usage?", "Do you have a discount for nonprofits?" This proactive objection handling helps head off questions before they become friction points. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Add a Context-Aware Chat Bubble
Include a chat bubble on the pricing page that is context-aware and can surface relevant answers or calls to action. For example, the chat can offer to answer pricing questions or prompt visitors to start a free trial. This makes it easy for visitors to get support before and after the sale without leaving the page. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## Calls to Action

### Designate One Primary CTA with Minimal Secondary Options
Choose one primary call to action (e.g., "Start Free Trial" or "Get a Demo") and use it consistently. If you have a secondary CTA, use it only sparingly and in specific places like the enterprise tier. Do not give visitors multiple equal choices; guide them toward the main action you want them to take. Secondary CTAs should be clearly distinct (e.g., "Contact Sales" or "Get a Demo"). (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Use Consistent CTA Language Across the Entire Page
Ensure all CTAs on the pricing page use the same language. If your primary CTA is "Start Free Trial," do not also use "Get Started" for the same action. Consistency reduces confusion and reinforces the action you want visitors to take. This includes CTAs in the page header and navigation. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

### Place CTAs at Natural Breakpoints Throughout the Page
Do not place CTAs only at the top and within pricing plan boxes. Sprinkle CTAs throughout the page at natural breakpoints — after testimonials, after key value statements, or after sections that seal the deal for a prospect. Allow visitors to convert at the moment they feel ready, without forcing them to scroll to find the button. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not use generic pricing page headings** like "Pricing" or "Pick the Plan for You." Replace them with your actual value proposition. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not hide pricing behind "Contact Sales" or request forms** for standard tiers. At minimum, show a starting price or range. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216; Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)
- **Do not create a "Request Pricing" form that doesn't actually deliver pricing.** If the form only leads to a demo booking, label it "Book a Demo" instead. If you must gate pricing, clearly state your ICP upfront so you filter appropriately without wasting prospects' time. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)
- **Do not use generic tier names** like "Basic," "Pro," and "Enterprise" without descriptive subheadings that explain who each plan is for. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not use jargon, acronyms, or internal product names** in feature descriptions. Use language your customers already understand. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not display a full feature comparison grid on the main page** without a collapsible option. Long grids cause analysis paralysis for first-time visitors. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not place CTAs only at the top of the page and inside plan boxes.** Distribute them at natural breakpoints throughout the page. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not use inconsistent CTA language** (e.g., mixing "Start Free Trial" and "Get Started" for the same action). (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not place links to other pages throughout the pricing page.** The purpose of the page is to convert visitors, not to send them elsewhere. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
- **Do not accept internal objections to pricing transparency at face value.** Investigate whether the real problem is unclear positioning, overly complex pricing, or poor product marketing. (Source: Emily Kramer, Episode #216)
- **Do not omit a value metric and ROI story.** Prospects must be able to understand what they are paying for and what value they will receive. (Source: Karan Sood, Episode #216)
- **Do not bury risk reversal messaging.** Place it prominently in the subheading directly below the main page heading. (Source: Bill Wilson, Episode #216)
