# Remote Work Practices for B2B Marketing Teams

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketing leaders can structure remote and distributed teams for productivity, alignment, and connection — including meeting cadence, async communication tools, trust-building, and organizational design. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 6 episodes. No general best practices have been added.

---

## Meeting Cadence and Structure

When helping a user design their remote team's meeting structure, apply the following framework:

**Establish a tiered one-on-one cadence based on seniority and alignment needs.** Do not default to weekly one-on-ones for everyone. Instead, calibrate frequency to role:
- Weekly: direct reports managing large functions
- Monthly: functional leaders
- Quarterly: country or regional leads
- Twice yearly: junior employees

Supplement all tiers with asynchronous updates (PowerPoint decks, Slack messages) rather than adding more meetings. *(Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #302)*

**Run weekly leadership meetings with broad participation** — include regional and functional leaders, not just direct reports. Hold monthly all-hands that include BDRs and junior staff to maintain company-wide alignment. *(Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #302)*

**Reserve synchronous meetings strictly for two purposes:**
1. **Sparring** — real-time back-and-forth between equals to test ideas, challenge thinking, and improve work through dialogue
2. **Connection** — building trust and rapport through genuine conversation

Do not schedule synchronous meetings for status updates, project progress reports, or one-way information sharing. *(Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264)*

**Define explicit criteria for what qualifies as a real meeting.** A synchronous meeting is warranted when the situation requires real-time debate, resolution of conflicting ideas, or a decision that must be made together. Everything else should default to async. *(Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*

## Asynchronous Communication

When advising on async workflows, apply these practices:

**Replace recurring information-sharing meetings with asynchronous video.** Monthly metrics reviews, status updates, and one-way briefings can be recorded as Loom videos and consumed on-demand — often at 1.5x speed. This frees calendar time and is especially valuable for distributed or global teams across time zones. *(Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*

**Use asynchronous PowerPoint updates to reduce one-on-one meeting frequency.** Have team members submit structured async decks on a regular cadence so the marketing leader stays informed without filling the calendar. Supplement with ad-hoc Slack messages and meetings as needed. This approach is particularly effective for leaders who travel frequently. *(Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #302)*

**Adopt a bursty communication pattern.** Structure team communication in alternating phases: periods of intense, rapid back-and-forth (sparring, real-time collaboration) followed by quiet periods for deep individual work. Teams that talk a lot, get fast responses, then go quiet to execute are the most successful and productive. This prevents constant context-switching while maintaining strong collaboration. *(Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)*

**Do not rely on a single communication channel for all interactions.** Use Slack for updates and administration, but schedule regular calls or in-person conversations for deeper connection and idea-riffing. Recognize that constant messaging without genuine connecting creates loneliness. Vary methods intentionally — some people need text-based communication, others need voice or video. The goal is actual connection, not just administration. *(Source: Kara Hardin, Episode #160)*

## Trust-Building in Distributed Teams

When helping a user build or maintain trust on a remote team, apply the following:

**Invest in in-person time upfront to enable charitable interpretation of async communication.** In async-heavy environments, tone and intent are harder to read. High trust between team members allows them to interpret terse Slack messages as efficiency rather than anger. In-person relationship-building is the foundation that makes async communication work. *(Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*

**Schedule intentional in-person gatherings (ITGs — Intentional Togetherness Gatherings) on a regular cadence** (quarterly or similar). A small amount of in-person time sustains trust and camaraderie for 2–3 months of distributed async work. These gatherings are especially important for new team members and for building the shared context needed to interpret async communication accurately. *(Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*

**Build trust through ownership of visible, recurring deliverables.** In small distributed teams without daily check-ins, establish accountability by owning high-visibility recurring outputs — for example, a monthly webinar or a twice-monthly newsletter. These create natural touchpoints where teammates see your work flowing through the system without requiring status meetings. Pair this with 1–2 strategic planning meetings per year to align on big-picture priorities. *(Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)*

## Organizational Design for Remote Teams

When advising on team structure and org design in a remote context:

**Resist adding unnecessary management layers.** Each layer reduces visibility (two-way understanding of what's happening up and down the org) and camaraderie (the ability for individual contributors to work directly with colleagues). This is especially critical in remote environments where isolation is already a risk. *(Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)*

**Do not create single-report manager roles** when individuals want to grow into management. Use span of control as the trigger for adding a management layer — only add layers when genuinely necessary to manage scale. *(Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)*

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not default to weekly one-on-ones for all team members regardless of seniority.** This creates calendar overload without proportional alignment value. *(Source: Priscilla Barolo, Episode #302)*
- **Do not use synchronous meetings for status updates or one-way information sharing.** These should be async by default. *(Source: Ashley Faus, Episode #264; Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*
- **Do not rely on a single communication channel (e.g., Slack only) for all team interaction.** Constant messaging without genuine connection creates loneliness and erodes team cohesion. *(Source: Kara Hardin, Episode #160)*
- **Do not skip in-person gatherings in the name of full async efficiency.** Without periodic in-person time, trust degrades and async communication becomes harder to interpret charitably. *(Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)*
- **Do not add management layers prematurely or create single-report manager roles.** Unnecessary layers reduce visibility and camaraderie, which are especially fragile in remote environments. *(Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)*
- **Do not allow constant context-switching between sync and async modes.** Structure communication in deliberate bursts — intense collaboration followed by quiet execution time — rather than a continuous stream of interruptions. *(Source: Molly Sands, Episode #264)*
