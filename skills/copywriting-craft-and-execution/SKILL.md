# Copywriting Craft and Execution

## Overview
This skill covers how to write, structure, edit, and refine B2B marketing copy across formats—from landing pages and emails to social posts, video scripts, and sales materials. It also addresses how to build copywriting skills over time and how to use AI tools effectively in the writing process. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast; no outside best practices have been added.

---

## Foundational Principles

**Conviction over creativity.** The most important factor in good marketing is believing in what you're saying. If you have conviction in an idea, you'll make it work. If you don't, no amount of creative technique will save it. Deep category knowledge gives you something to believe in. When you sit down to write, have a clear point of view about your category and be willing to stake your reputation on it. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Storytelling and copywriting are evergreen career skills.** Rather than chasing trendy growth tactics or trying to be well-rounded, focus on becoming excellent at telling stories and writing compelling copy. This skill compounds over time and is valuable across all marketing roles and companies. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #229)

**Writing difficulty is a research signal, not a creative block.** If you're struggling to write or get started on copy, it's not a creative block—it's a research deficit. You don't know enough about the product, customers, or their pain points. Before trying harder to write, go back and review customer interviews, talk to more people, and gather additional data points. Once you have enough research, the writing flows naturally. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

## Story Structure and Narrative Frameworks

**Use the story-problem-solution-CTA framework for marketing copy.** Structure marketing copy using a proven four-part framework: (1) Tell a story to hook the reader emotionally, (2) Present the problem or conflict, (3) Show the solution and back it up with examples, (4) End with a clear call-to-action. This framework works across emails, landing pages, and presentations. Humans operate in stories and need clear examples to understand abstract concepts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Apply the Five Immutable Laws of Storytelling to all marketing copy.** Every story must follow five laws: (1) No conflict, no story; (2) No people, no story—stories are about real people, not abstractions; (3) No 'but' and 'so,' no story—use these words implicitly to drive direction and advance the narrative; (4) No ordinary, no story—start small and make it feel big rather than bragging about big numbers; (5) If you start explaining, no story—show examples instead of explaining concepts. Use these as a checklist when reviewing marketing copy. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Use conflict as the foundation of every story.** Build marketing stories around conflict—the core element that makes narratives compelling. Identify the unresolved tension in your positioning and resolve it through your story. This applies to all marketing copy: homepages, ads, social posts, sales decks. Without conflict, you have no story, regardless of how well-written the copy is. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Use the Rectangle Framework to identify and introduce conflict.** Draw a rectangle and divide it down the middle. On the left, place one idea or position. On the right, place something diametrically opposed to it. This visual exercise helps you find conflict by looking for things that are diametrically opposed. Use this when brainstorming headlines, ad copy, or positioning statements. Examples: 'We're not Steve Jobs' (left) vs. 'We're modest' (right). This framework makes conflict visible and actionable. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Use 'but' and 'so' implicitly to drive story direction.** The words 'but' and 'so' should appear implicitly between sentences to create narrative momentum and advance the story. When you connect ideas, the underlying logic should follow a 'but/so' structure—where 'but' changes direction and 'so' advances the plot. Test your copy by checking if removing 'and' and replacing it with implicit 'but/so' logic makes the narrative stronger. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Tell stories through real people, not abstract concepts or features.** When you need to communicate a capability or feature, don't talk about the feature itself—talk about a real person who embodies or benefits from it. Instead of describing automation or technical capabilities, introduce a character and tell their story. Real people create connection; abstractions create indifference. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Start small and make it feel big rather than bragging about large numbers.** It's easier to make something small feel big than to make something big feel big. Instead of opening with massive metrics (e.g., 'billions of hours saved'), start with a relatable, small detail and build from there. This creates emotional resonance and makes the impact feel real. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Apply the power of three to big-picture storytelling, not sentence-level messaging.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* The power of three works for overarching narrative structure (e.g., a keynote with three acts, a brand story with three chapters), but should not be applied to headlines, value propositions, or individual sentences. When companies force three benefits into a single headline or value prop, they often end up with fluffy, generic messaging where the third item is included just to complete the pattern. Reserve the power of three for macro-level storytelling where it genuinely aids retention and narrative flow. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Structure interactive demos as a story, not a feature dump.** When creating an interactive demo, focus on telling a coherent story that mirrors how a sales rep would demo the product to a prospect. Start with context (homepage screen), walk through a logical sequence, and keep prospects engaged throughout. Avoid simply listing features in isolation. (Source: Madhav Bhandari, Episode #222)

## Writing Process and Craft

**Start with the hook or headline, not the body copy.** When writing any marketing asset (landing page, email, newsletter), begin by identifying and writing the hook or headline first. This becomes your north star. Use it to work backwards and structure the rest of the content around it. Don't write the body first and add the headline last—reverse that process. The hook should answer: what's the one thing we want people to say or remember about this? (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Find and study existing examples before writing from scratch.** Don't start with a blank page. Before writing a landing page, email, or any marketing asset, find 1–3 existing examples of similar work that you think are good. Study their structure, layout, and approach. Use these as inspiration and reference points. Most good ideas have been done before in some form. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Prioritize editing over initial writing; great writing is great editing.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* The first draft is rarely the final version. After writing initial copy, go back and edit aggressively. Generate multiple variations (e.g., 5–10 subject line options), then piece together the best elements from each. Use tools like ChatGPT to generate variations, then cherry-pick words and phrases from different options to build your own final version. Editing is where the real work happens, not the initial writing. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Use themed writing days and shitty first drafts to overcome perfectionism.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* Block dedicated time to produce a rough first draft without aiming for polish. The goal is to get words on the page, not perfection. Label this draft 'drafting table' to signal it's exploratory. Once you have a shitty first draft, you can refine and iterate. This approach prevents the paralysis of trying to write perfect copy on the first pass. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

**Show examples instead of explaining concepts in marketing copy.** When you're tempted to explain a concept or benefit, replace the explanation with a concrete example. Examples are more memorable and emotionally resonant than explanations. If you find yourself writing 'we help you do X because Y,' replace it with a specific story or scenario that demonstrates X and Y in action. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Use analogies and lateral thinking to make complex product concepts accessible.** When explaining complex or technical product concepts, use analogies from familiar domains to make them relatable. Prompt ChatGPT to generate these analogies by asking it to connect your product concept with a personal interest or familiar domain. This makes content more engaging, memorable, and shareable while maintaining accuracy. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #285)

**Use fast feedback loops to iterate on copy quickly.** Create rapid feedback loops by testing copy with your target audience, getting feedback on clarity and resonance, making changes, and testing again. The faster your feedback loops, the faster you reach effective copy. A 24-hour feedback loop is achievable and far superior to monthly testing cycles. This requires putting copy in front of actual target customers, not relying on internal stakeholder opinions. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Create protected deep work blocks for creative messaging and writing.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* Good writing and creative messaging require space and breathing room. Constant context-switching (Slack, email, meetings) prevents the kind of thinking needed for inspired ideas and great headlines. Protect time for focused, uninterrupted work on messaging. (Source: Emma Stratton, Episode #295)

## Tone, Voice, and Language

**Write copy at an 8th grade reading level to maximize clarity and engagement.** Write all landing page copy at an 8th grade reading level or below, regardless of your audience's education level. This removes buzzwords naturally (8th graders don't say 'seamless' or 'supercharged'), increases skimmability, and improves comprehension. This approach makes your content feel accessible rather than trying to sound smart, which actually increases engagement and conversion likelihood. (Source: Tas Bober, Episode #185)

**Use simple, conversational language instead of marketing jargon.** Write website copy in plain language that describes what you actually do, not what you claim to do. Instead of 'Accelerate pipeline 10x,' say 'Get warm intros' or 'Connect prospects with customers.' Avoid industry jargon and overstatement. This makes it immediately clear who the product is for and what value it delivers, reducing cognitive load for buyers. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178)

**Write content in a casual, conversational tone as if messaging a friend, not corporate speak.** To avoid sounding like a corporate marketer, write as if you're texting a colleague or friend. Use casual language, drop corporate jargon, and be conversational. This approach makes content feel genuine and relatable, increasing the likelihood of engagement. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Inject personality into B2B marketing through authentic human details, not corny tactics.** Show personality in marketing by being genuinely human—use real faces (no stock photos), write copy with authentic tone, and sprinkle in real personal details that signal you're a real person. Examples: mention you're writing from 35,000 feet on a flight and miss your kids. This works for enterprise too; personality doesn't mean inappropriate or absurd, it means real and relatable. Avoid inserting name tokens or generic personalization tactics. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #214)

**Inject specific personal details and authentic context into posts.** Add small, specific personal details to your LinkedIn posts to make them feel authentic and human, especially when using AI assistance. Examples: mention you're writing from 37,000 feet on a JetBlue flight, reference what you're wearing, describe a specific moment from your day. These details signal that the post was written by a real person and cannot be replicated by AI. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Use witty, conversational copy in paid creative to stand out and improve performance.** Move away from traditional B2B marketing language and use more casual, sassy, or humorous copy in paid ads. Top-performing creative featured copy like 'You don't have to download an app' and 'Think outside the inbox' instead of generic B2B messaging. This approach resonates better with audiences and improves engagement and conversion rates. (Source: Richard Meyer, Episode #341)

**Avoid templated or overly formal language in sales materials; use conversational copy.** Sales teams often reject marketing-created templates and messaging because they use formal, branded language that doesn't sound natural in a real conversation. Instead of 12th-grade English sentences with big fancy words, write copy that a salesperson would actually feel comfortable saying to a prospect in 30 seconds. Test whether you'd naturally say it aloud. (Source: Jen Allen Knuth, Episode #170; Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #232)

**Refresh dated content by updating tone and language.** When repurposing older high-performing content, audit it for language or framing that no longer aligns with current audience sentiment. Update specific words or phrases to match contemporary values without changing the core idea. Example: replace 'hustle' (which triggers modern audiences) with 'work hard' to preserve the message while removing friction. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #209; Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## Formatting and Structure

**Formatting is an underrated part of writing; structure for readability.** Don't just focus on words—structure and format matter as much as content. Break copy into clear sections with headers. Use white space, bold text, and short paragraphs. When linking, make the entire sentence or phrase a hyperlink and bold it, not just the word 'here.' Format should make the content visually appealing and easy to scan, because people eat with their eyes first. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Use one primary call-to-action per marketing asset.** Each email, landing page, or marketing piece should have one clear, primary call-to-action. Multiple competing CTAs confuse the reader and dilute impact. You can repeat the same CTA link multiple times (top and bottom of email), but it should be the same ask. Be direct about what you want them to do. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Plant a flag in the headline to self-select your target audience.** Write headlines that explicitly signal who the content is for, making it clear to your target persona while naturally filtering out irrelevant readers. This reduces wasted impressions and ensures the people who read your content are the ones it was designed for. Example: '15 Tips Every Crossfitter Should Know' signals the audience immediately. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #155)

**Test copy tone and point of view—generic vs. controversial messaging.** Run A/B tests comparing generic, middle-of-the-road copy against copy with a strong point of view or mild controversy. In one test, generic copy generated 0 comments and 0 shares, while copy that 'chose a side' generated 8 comments and 11 shares with 5x higher engagement—same audience, same design, only the copy changed. Strong POV copy stands out more in feeds and drives higher engagement. (Source: Tagg Bozied, Episode #243)

**Adjust editing rigor based on the stakes of the asset.** Not all marketing assets require the same level of editing and review. Assess the stakes: a high-stakes landing page for a premium event targeting CMOs should get multiple rounds of feedback and careful word-smithing. A daily LinkedIn post has lower stakes and can be shipped faster with less review. Match your editing process to the stakes of what you're shipping. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Integrate ad disclosures authentically into content, not as an afterthought.** FTC requires influencers to disclose paid partnerships. Rather than using a generic '#ad' hashtag at the bottom, encourage creators to integrate disclosure naturally into their content: 'Really grateful to partner with [brand]' or similar language woven into the narrative. This requires giving creators creative freedom and time to develop the content. (Source: Brianna Doe, Episode #305)

## Social and Video Copy

**Apply proven LinkedIn hook templates to increase post performance.** Study and adhere to high-performing hook patterns on LinkedIn rather than writing freely. Effective hooks include definitive statements (e.g., 'The golden age of SaaS is over'), cliffhangers with future implications, and contrarian takes. Templates improve engagement significantly compared to rambling posts. Ensure every post follows proper hook structure, editing, and close format. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Use "How I" framing instead of "How To" in video posts.** Frame video content from personal experience ('How I did X') rather than prescriptive expertise ('How to do X'). Posts using 'How I' framing perform approximately 3x better than 'How to' equivalents because they feel more authentic and less like the creator is grandstanding. This applies to both video and written content on social platforms like LinkedIn. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Use the Reverse Rambo hook format for video openings.** Structure video hooks in three parts: (1) State a common belief in your industry (e.g., 'Everyone says cold emailing is dead'), (2) Say 'but' and present your contrarian point of view, backed by a stat or personal experience, (3) Conclude with 'Here's how you do it.' This format catches attention by challenging assumptions and immediately signals the value the viewer will get. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Spend equal time perfecting the first five seconds as the rest of the video.** Invest as much effort in crafting the hook (first 5 seconds) as you do in the remaining content. A strong hook should: (1) Succinctly tell viewers what the video is about, and (2) Probe on a pain point or problem relevant to the audience. Include on-screen text in the first five seconds to contextualize the content. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Design video hooks for the first 2 seconds using visual action or text.** The first 2 seconds of a video are critical for stopping scrollers. Use either a visual hook (e.g., a striking action or unexpected visual element) or a text-based hook (e.g., a provocative question or statement). Test both approaches to see what stops your audience. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

**Prioritize hook and storytelling over production quality for video engagement.** Video success is determined primarily by the hook, angle, and storytelling—not production quality or editing. A simple, authentic video with a strong hook will outperform a highly polished video with weak messaging. Focus your effort on the creative concept and narrative before worrying about cameras, lighting, or editing. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

**Structure X threads with eye-catching image hooks and data-driven openings.** When writing threads on X, start with a visually compelling image (screenshot of company growth, photo of person being discussed, or data visualization). Follow with a hook that includes statistics or data points to capture attention. Then deliver core valuable content in the thread body. End with a soft call-to-action (subscribe to newsletter, read related thread) rather than a hard sell. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #209; Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Promote webinars as 22 minutes long to increase registration and attendance.** Promote webinars as 22 minutes instead of 30, 45, or 60 minutes. When prospects see '22 minutes,' they block 30 minutes on their calendar, giving them 8 minutes buffer. Non-round numbers feel more credible and specific. The shorter time commitment increases registration rates. If you can't deliver content in 22 minutes, the content is likely unfocused or boring. (Source: Jay Schwedelson, Episode #329)

**Use FOMO messaging to drive executive attendance by highlighting other attendees.** When inviting executives to an event, tell them who else will be attending. Name-drop other companies, competitors, or industry leaders to create a sense of missing out if they don't attend. This is particularly effective for executives who are concerned about staying informed and connected. (Source: Stephanie Christensen, Episode #227)

**Win back churned customers with honesty and humor before pitching product improvements.** When re-engaging churned customers, trust has been broken and must be rebuilt. Lead with honesty and humor: send a message that admits the company fell short (e.g., 'I'm sorry, we sucked') and acknowledge that vendors and humans miss sometimes. Only after establishing this tone should you share product updates or improvements. Consider having the CEO or least-salesy person reach out rather than sales. (Source: Chris Rack, Episode #150)

## Building Copywriting Skills Over Time

**Study one copywriting book deeply rather than reading many books superficially.** Select a single foundational copywriting book and study it thoroughly. Recommended starting point: 'The Copywriter's Handbook' by Robert Bly. Other solid options include 'Ogilvy on Advertising' or 'The Adweek Copywriting Handbook.' The goal is to understand core principles deeply, not to collect surface-level knowledge from many sources. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Build copywriting skills through daily public writing practice on LinkedIn or Twitter.** Establish a daily writing practice by publishing on a public channel. Write about a specific topic or theme related to your expertise. Set a timer for 30 minutes and write daily, treating each piece as an opportunity to apply copywriting principles. This creates consistent feedback loops through engagement metrics and builds a portfolio of work. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Use a weekly newsletter as a copywriting practice vehicle with built-in feedback metrics.** Use a weekly newsletter as your primary copywriting practice ground. Each week provides a natural deadline and feedback loop through open rates, click rates, and direct responses. Over the course of a year, a weekly writing practice generates 52 opportunities to test, learn, and improve copywriting skills in a real-world context. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Maintain a swipe file of high-quality copy examples for reference and study.** Build a collection of well-written copy examples that resonate with you. Screenshot and save examples from LinkedIn ads, emails, landing pages, newsletters, and other sources. Organize these in a tool like Evernote or similar. Review this file regularly to study what makes certain copy effective. The act of collecting and reviewing trains your eye for good copywriting. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Regularly consume competitor and adjacent content for inspiration and ideas.** Build a habit of consuming content from newsletters, brands, and creators you admire. Study what works: email subject lines, landing page layouts, thumbnail designs, messaging approaches. Send yourself screenshots and notes. This constant input feeds your creative output. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Maintain a diverse reading diet (fiction, business, nonfiction) to feed creative writing.** Read a mix of fiction, business books, and nonfiction to improve copywriting quality. Fiction expands vocabulary, teaches narrative structure, and forces creative thinking outside the box. Use tools like Readwise to resurface highlights from past reading daily—this provides a quick creative primer before writing sessions. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

**Learn craft skills through marked-up feedback, not questions.** When teaching someone a craft skill like copywriting, mark up their work with corrections and rewrites rather than asking probing questions. This is how copywriters learn—through repetition and seeing how an expert would rewrite the same piece. Direct feedback with examples teaches faster than Socratic questioning. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #257)

**Dedicate a tone revision step before final approval.** Build a dedicated tone revision step into your production process. After the executive reviews the draft, have them specifically focus on whether the post sounds like them. Use this as a 'heat check' moment where the executive can adjust phrasing, word choice, and voice to match their authentic tone. Start with copy-pasting their raw language from the idea dump, then revise for formatting and clarity, and finally adjust tone to match their voice. (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Apply a five-minute writing threshold to content creation.** *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)* If a piece of content takes longer than five minutes to write, kill it. This filter helps identify what's already fully formed in your head versus what requires excessive deliberation. If it doesn't flow naturally within five minutes, it's either not ready or not the right idea to pursue. This applies especially to LinkedIn posts and short-form content. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #142)

## Using AI in Copywriting

*(Note: There is a genuine disagreement among guests about how far AI should be used in copy generation — see Where Experts Disagree before applying any of the practices in this section.)*

**AI-generated copy requires strong copywriting skills to be effective.** AI is not a substitute for copywriting skill; it's a tool that amplifies existing skill. To get good copy from AI, you must already understand what good copy looks like and be able to prompt specifically for it. Most marketers create average copy at scale because they lack the underlying copywriting ability to teach the AI what 'good' means. Without those skills, AI will just produce mediocre content faster. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episode #318; Kieran Flanagan, Episode #257)

**Build a swipe file of inspiration for prompt writing.** Treat prompting like copywriting: maintain a swipe file of examples, frameworks, and patterns you encounter. When you see something that works well—a design aesthetic, a campaign structure, a communication style—capture it and use it as reference material when writing prompts. The more specific examples you can feed into a prompt (via images, descriptions, or direct references), the better the AI output. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episode #318)

**Use AI as a writing assistant to enhance your own content, not replace it.** Feed AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT) your own past posts and voice memos to build a personalized writing style guide, then use AI to help flesh out ideas, add research, and refine hooks—but always write the core content yourself. Export your top posts into Claude to identify patterns in what works. Never publish AI-generated content wholesale; inject personal details, opinions, and authentic voice that AI cannot replicate. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Use AI to analyze trends, validate ideas, and strengthen your writing with research.** When you have a core idea or analogy that resonates with your audience, use ChatGPT to research and validate it. Ask ChatGPT to find supporting research, frameworks, or studies that back up your idea. Then use those findings to strengthen your writing and add credibility. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #155)

**Use ChatGPT to analyze writing style and provide editing feedback on copy.** Leverage ChatGPT as a copywriting research and editing tool. Feed high-quality copy examples into ChatGPT and ask it to describe the writing style, tone, and techniques used. Then attempt to write in that same style. Additionally, use ChatGPT to review your own writing and ask it to identify weaknesses, poke holes in arguments, or suggest improvements. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**Use AI to identify clichés and abstract language, not to generate ideas.** AI is useful for identifying what's wrong with copy—clichés, abstract verbs, abstract nouns—but it cannot generate ideas with conviction. Feed your homepage copy or draft into an AI tool and ask it to flag clichés, abstract verbs, and abstract nouns. Use the output to identify weak spots. However, don't rely on AI to write the replacement copy; that requires human conviction and deep category knowledge. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Use ChatGPT for brainstorming and ideation, not final marketing copy.** Use AI tools like ChatGPT to accelerate ideation, brainstorm content topics, and generate starting points for marketing work. However, do not rely on AI-generated copy as final marketing output. AI currently lacks the emotional resonance and human understanding needed for effective B2B messaging. Test AI-generated content against human-written content—human-written emails and copy consistently outperform AI-generated versions on engagement metrics. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should AI be used to generate marketing copy, or only to analyze and edit human-written copy?

**Support summary: 5 vs 2**

**Position A — AI can be a genuine creative partner for copy generation (with skill):**
Kieran Flanagan (Episodes #318 and #257) argues that AI amplifies existing skill. Skilled copywriters can use AI as a creative partner to iterate and scale. The problem isn't AI—it's that most marketers lack the underlying skill to prompt well. With strong copywriting and prompting skills, AI can generate strong copy. Dave Gerhardt (Episodes #275, #155, and #129) supports this position in practice: he recommends feeding AI your own past posts to build a style guide, using AI to research and validate ideas, and using ChatGPT to analyze writing style and generate editing feedback—all of which treat AI as an active creative collaborator, not just a spell-checker.

**Position B — AI should be used for brainstorming and ideation only, not final copy:**
Talia Wolf (Episode #132) argues that AI currently lacks the emotional resonance and human understanding needed for effective B2B messaging. In her testing, human-written emails and copy consistently outperform AI-generated versions on engagement metrics. Use AI to move faster on initial drafts, then apply human judgment, tone, and emotional intelligence before publishing. Harry Dry (Episode #303) takes a philosophical position that goes further: AI cannot generate ideas with conviction, and conviction is the most important ingredient in good marketing. AI can flag clichés and weak language, but the replacement copy must come from a human with genuine belief in what they're saying.

**Context dependency:** Kieran Flanagan and Dave Gerhardt's pro-AI-generation stance is explicitly conditioned on the marketer having strong underlying copywriting skills. Talia Wolf's and Harry Dry's skepticism may apply more broadly regardless of skill level. The disagreement partially dissolves if you accept that skilled copywriters can use AI generatively while unskilled ones should not—but Harry Dry's conviction argument is a genuine philosophical disagreement that doesn't dissolve with skill level.

**Trend note:** The more recent episodes (2025–2026) from Kieran Flanagan and Dave Gerhardt lean toward AI as a capable creative partner, while the skeptical positions (Talia Wolf, Harry Dry) come from 2024–2025. This may reflect evolving AI capabilities rather than a stable disagreement.

**What this means for you:** Before using AI to generate copy, honestly assess whether you have strong enough copywriting skills to recognize and prompt for good output. If you do, AI can be a legitimate creative partner. If you don't, treat AI as a brainstorming and ideation tool only, and apply significant human editing before publishing. Regardless of skill level, Harry Dry's conviction argument suggests that the most important creative choices—the bold positioning, the contrarian take, the specific angle—should come from you, not the AI.

### 2. Should good content flow quickly (five-minute rule) or require protected deep work time?

**Support summary: 4 vs 1**

**Position A — Good content flows fast:**
Jason Lemkin (Episode #142) argues that if a piece of content takes longer than five minutes to write, kill it. Content that requires excessive deliberation isn't ready or isn't the right idea. Speed of execution is a quality signal—if it flows naturally, it's fully formed. This applies especially to LinkedIn posts and short-form content.

**Position B — Writing requires protected deep work time:**
Emma Stratton (Episode #295) argues that good writing and creative messaging require space and breathing room. Constant context-switching prevents the kind of thinking needed for inspired ideas and great headlines. Creativity doesn't happen in fragmented time or under pressure. Will Hoekenga (Episode #181) supports this with two related points: first, that blocking dedicated time for a rough first draft (without aiming for polish) is essential to overcoming perfectionism; second, that if you're struggling to write, it's a research deficit—go back and gather more data, and the writing will flow once you have enough. Dave Gerhardt (Episode #314) adds that editing is where the real work happens, not the initial writing—implying that the process requires more than five minutes of effort.

**Context dependency:** Jason Lemkin's five-minute rule may apply specifically to short-form social content (LinkedIn posts) where he has the most experience, while Emma Stratton and Will Hoekenga are speaking more broadly about messaging and landing page copy. The disagreement is real for short-form content but may dissolve for longer, higher-stakes assets.

**What this means for you:** Apply the five-minute rule as a filter for short-form social content—if a LinkedIn post idea isn't fully formed, don't force it. For longer, higher-stakes assets (landing pages, email campaigns, messaging frameworks), protect deep work time and expect the process to take longer. Don't abandon a good idea just because it doesn't flow immediately; it may need more research first.

### 3. Should you use the 'power of three' structure in marketing copy and messaging?

**Support summary: 1 vs 1**

**Position A — The power of three works for storytelling:**
Dave Gerhardt (Episode #314) implicitly endorses structured numerical frameworks in copy through his use of clear sections (Thing One, Thing Two, Thing Three) and his story-problem-solution-CTA framework. This suggests comfort with numbered structures as an organizing principle.

**Position B — The power of three is harmful at the sentence and headline level:**
Diane Wiredu (Episode #226) argues that the power of three should not be applied to headlines, value propositions, or individual sentences. When companies force three benefits into a single headline or value prop, they often end up with fluffy, generic messaging where the third item is included just to complete the pattern. Prioritize ruthlessly at the sentence level: one message per sentence, one key message per section. Reserve the power of three for macro-level storytelling only.

**Context dependency:** This disagreement partially dissolves on closer inspection. Diane Wiredu explicitly allows the power of three at the macro storytelling level and only objects to its use in headlines and value propositions. Dave Gerhardt's use of numbered sections is at the formatting level, not the headline level. However, there is a genuine tension about whether structured numerical frameworks help or hurt clarity in marketing copy.

**What this means for you:** Use the power of three freely for macro-level narrative structure (keynote acts, brand story chapters, email section headers). Do not use it to force three benefits into a single headline or value proposition—if you find yourself adding a third item just to complete the pattern, cut it and lead with your strongest single message.

## What NOT To Do

**Don't use abstract language, clichés, or corporate jargon.** Words like 'seamless,' 'supercharged,' 'accelerate,' and 'leverage' add no meaning and reduce credibility. Replace them with plain language that describes what you actually do. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #178; Tas Bober, Episode #185; Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Don't explain concepts when you can show examples.** If you find yourself writing 'we help you do X because Y,' replace it with a specific story or scenario that demonstrates X and Y in action. Explaining breaks the story; examples sustain it. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Don't open with massive metrics or brag about large numbers.** Starting with 'billions of hours saved' or similar claims creates distance rather than resonance. Start small and make it feel big instead. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Don't use multiple competing CTAs in a single marketing asset.** Multiple CTAs confuse the reader and dilute impact. One primary ask per asset, repeated if necessary, but never competing with itself. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Don't write the body copy first and add the headline last.** Start with the hook or headline and work backwards. Writing the body first and adding the headline last produces copy that lacks a clear north star. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Don't use stock photos or generic personalization tokens to signal personality.** Real personality comes from authentic human details and real faces, not name tokens or stock imagery. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #214)

**Don't use templated, formal language in sales materials.** If a salesperson wouldn't naturally say it aloud in a 30-second conversation, rewrite it. Templates with 'big fancy words' and 12th-grade English sentences don't work in real prospect conversations. (Source: Jen Allen Knuth, Episode #170; Jen Allen-Knuth, Episode #232)

**Don't publish AI-generated content wholesale without injecting human voice.** Even if you use AI to generate drafts, always add personal details, opinions, and authentic voice that AI cannot replicate before publishing. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Don't use AI to generate ideas with conviction.** AI can flag weak language and generate variations, but the bold creative choices—the contrarian take, the specific angle, the positioning stake—must come from a human with genuine belief in what they're saying. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

**Don't force three benefits into a single headline or value proposition.** If the third item exists only to complete the pattern, cut it. Lead with your strongest single message. (Source: Diane Wiredu, Episode #226)

**Don't use a generic '#ad' hashtag at the bottom of influencer content.** Integrate disclosure naturally into the narrative instead. A bolted-on disclosure feels off-putting and signals inauthenticity. (Source: Brianna Doe, Episode #305)

**Don't rely on internal stakeholder opinions to validate copy.** Test copy with actual target customers. Internal opinions don't reflect how buyers will respond. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)
