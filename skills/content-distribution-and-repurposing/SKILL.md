# Content Distribution and Repurposing

## Overview
This skill covers how B2B marketers should distribute content across channels, repurpose existing assets into multiple formats, and build content systems that compound over time. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast and attributed to the specific episode and guest who recommended them. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved.

---

## Core Distribution Philosophy

**Adopt "create once, distribute forever" as your operating mantra.** Take every valuable piece of content and repurpose it continuously across formats and channels. A podcast episode becomes blog posts, LinkedIn posts, Medium articles, YouTube clips, carousels, and social snippets. Reuse the same story across different platforms and audiences. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Lead with distribution when planning any content or campaign.** Before creating anything, identify how it will be distributed and who will amplify it. Ask: Who is the partner on this? Who is a practitioner that will give you a quote? Who will share it when it goes live? This ensures content reaches your audience through multiple channels and gains the authority signals that AI systems now prioritize. (Source: Aditya Vempaty, Episode #304)

**Plan content using a channel-first approach, not a content-first approach.** Start by identifying which channels will distribute the content, not by deciding what to create. Determine where your pipeline will come from and which channels you need to be active in, then design content to fit those channels. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)

**Execute content using the Research-Create-Distribute-Optimize framework.** Structure your content program in four phases: (1) Research your audience's pains, problems, and sales objections by listening to sales calls; (2) Create valuable content in formats and on channels where your audience spends time; (3) Distribute across multiple channels and formats—never publish once and stop; (4) Optimize quarterly by reviewing assets from a conversion and SEO lens. A content calendar in Notion is not a strategy. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Measure content by tracking distribution across multiple channels and engagement with each form.** Evaluate performance by measuring how content performs across different distribution channels and formats. Track engagement in each form (blog, social, email, partner channels) to understand which distribution methods drive the most value. (Source: Lindsay O'Brien, Episode #304)

## Multi-Channel Distribution Strategies

**Create for two core platforms, then distribute everywhere else.** Select two primary platforms to create for (e.g., TikTok/Instagram Reels for short-form video, LinkedIn for thought leadership), then distribute that content across all other platforms. This reduces production complexity while maintaining platform-specific messaging. For ClickUp, this means creating funny business humor for short-form video and thought leadership for LinkedIn, then posting those same videos across Facebook Reels, YouTube Shorts, and other channels. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

**Don't overlook Facebook Reels as a high-performing distribution channel.** Facebook Reels are significantly underutilized by B2B marketers but deliver substantial reach, especially among older decision-makers (38–60 age range). ClickUp generated 100M impressions from Facebook Reels in a single month—roughly half their total monthly impressions. Post the same short-form video content to Facebook Reels alongside Instagram and TikTok. This is a low-effort, high-return distribution channel that most competitors ignore. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

**Develop a "search everywhere" visibility strategy based on buyer research behavior.** Identify where your target buyers are asking questions and seeking information—communities, ChatGPT, LinkedIn, in-person events, analyst reports, tech partner channels. Ensure your brand is visible and adding value in each of those places. Map buyer research behavior and allocate content and presence accordingly. This requires ongoing research into how your buyers' research behavior is evolving. (Source: Lisa Cole, Episode #315)

**Pursue SERP domination by securing placements across multiple sources, not just your own blog.** Actively work to appear in multiple places on the search results page for your target keywords. This includes securing mentions in third-party publications, creating content in relevant Reddit communities, and ensuring your brand appears in comparison tools like G2 and Capterra. Track competitor SERP presence using tools like Stat (owned by Moz) to identify where you're missing placements. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Prioritize third-party brand mentions in AI search strategy.** 85% of brand mentions in AI search come from third-party content, not your own website. Focus on creating quality content that will be organically picked up and cited by other websites and LLMs. Ensure quality content is well-distributed off your website to maximize third-party pickup. (Source: Eoin Clancy, Episode #336)

**Move beyond single-channel content distribution to maximize AI search visibility.** LLMs crawl multiple sources including Reddit, Quora, Wikipedia, G2/Capterra reviews, influencer content, PR mentions, and event coverage. Repurpose core content into different formats (YouTube tutorials, podcasts, blog posts, social media) and distribute across channels where your audience and LLMs can find it. (Source: Andrei Țiț, Episode #269)

**Drive audience from rented channels into owned channels.** Use rented platforms (LinkedIn, YouTube, TikTok, Google Search) as acquisition channels to build awareness and drive traffic, but funnel subscribers into owned channels (email list, direct community) where you control the relationship and can monetize without algorithmic or platform dependency. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145)

**Treat search (SEO) as an owned distribution channel alongside social media.** Search is an organic distribution channel that doesn't require paid spend or algorithm luck. Hundreds of thousands of people search for keywords relevant to your industry and problems you solve. By creating content that ranks for those keywords, you capture a percentage of that search volume. While search requires more upfront work than paid, it pays significant dividends over time because the traffic is intent-driven and recurring. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #209)

## Content Repurposing Systems

### The Content Flywheel

**Use podcast interviews as the hub for a multi-format content flywheel.** Start with a weekly podcast interview format as your core content production engine. From each one-hour episode, extract multiple content formats: short video clips, transcripts, newsletter content, social media posts, and website articles. This single source of content fuels multiple distribution channels and reduces the production burden. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #225)

**Build a content flywheel: podcast episode → newsletter → LinkedIn posts → webinar → content series.** Build a repeatable content system where a single source feeds multiple downstream formats. Each step in the flywheel provides feedback on what resonates, informing the next step. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

**Build a demand flywheel where every content asset feeds into and reinforces others.** Design your content and demand generation strategy as an interconnected flywheel: thought leadership content drives web traffic → website conversion points capture leads → demos are personalized to inbound source → sales calls are recorded and transcribed → transcripts become new content → content performance data informs event topics → events create transcripts that become content again. This creates compounding returns and reduces the need for paid demand generation. (Source: Kyle Coleman, Episode #206)

**Build a three-tier flywheel: social media → newsletter → product feedback loop.** Social media drives traffic to your newsletter, your newsletter reveals what resonates with your audience, and that feedback informs both your social content and product development. This creates a continuous loop of learning and refinement. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episodes #244 and #158)

### Repurposing Specific Asset Types

**Repurpose podcast episodes into blog posts, video clips, carousels, and social media content.** Take podcast episodes and systematically convert them into multiple formats: embed the audio in blog posts on related topics, create short video clips for social media, turn key quotes into carousel posts, publish full transcripts on Medium with canonical links back to your site, and share individual insights as social media posts. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Create a content waterfall from podcast episodes into articles, social clips, and newsletters.** Use podcast transcripts as a nucleus piece of content. Extract key learnings, stories, and tactical plays from each episode and repurpose them into written articles, social media threads, short-form video clips, and newsletter content. This creates a feedback loop where you learn what resonates and generate multiple pieces of distribution-ready content from a single source. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episode #262)

**Start with live video content (webinars) as the primary source, then repurpose across channels.** Repurpose a single live webinar into: short clips and playlists on YouTube, blog posts, downloadable assets, and community distribution. This approach maximizes efficiency by creating one high-quality source and distributing it broadly rather than creating separate content for each channel. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)

**Repurpose webinar Q&A into short-form social content.** Extract the Q&A section (not the main content) from webinars and repurpose the top audience questions into short-form videos for social and email. Display the question as on-screen text in the first few seconds, then show the answer. This approach outperforms generic webinar recaps because it targets what the audience actually cares about. Use the question as the video title to drive clicks from people who have that same question. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Repurpose executive content (podcasts, presentations, blog posts) into new campaign formats.** Feed existing executive content—podcast transcripts, presentation decks, blog posts, event talks—into a ChatGPT Project and prompt it to generate new campaign assets in different formats. For example, upload a podcast transcript and ask the AI to create a white paper outline, LinkedIn carousel ideas, email series, or ad hooks based on the content. Repeat this process whenever new content is created to maintain a steady stream of repurposed campaign ideas. (Source: Pranav Piyush, Episode #285)

**Turn top blog posts into YouTube videos using SEO ranking tools.** Identify your best-performing blog posts, use a YouTube SEO tool like TubeBuddy to test different video title variations that rank well, then create a video backed by that validated title. Embed the video back into the original blog post with an updated "last written" date to refresh its Google ranking and expand visibility across search and video tabs. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

**Republish blog posts on LinkedIn and Medium using canonical links to avoid duplicate content penalties.** Take your original blog post and republish it on LinkedIn or Medium to reach different audiences. Use canonical links pointing back to your original post to signal to search engines which version is authoritative. You can use AI to rewrite the intro and title so it doesn't appear as exact duplicate content while maintaining the core message. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Extract quotes and statistics from white papers and republish as social media content.** Don't let valuable data and insights live only within white papers. Pull out key statistics, quotes, and findings, turn them into screenshots or graphics, and reshare them across social media channels. This extends the lifespan of research and makes insights accessible to audiences who won't read the full white paper. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Use AI to extract quotes from content and automate social media graphic creation.** Upload your content (blog posts, transcripts, etc.) to Claude or ChatGPT and ask it to generate a spreadsheet of quotes. Take that spreadsheet and upload it to Canva with automation enabled so that Canva automatically populates each quote into a template. This creates dozens of social media graphics from a single piece of content in minutes. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Use AI-powered video content labs to automatically extract and repurpose webinar clips.** Implement tools like Goldcast's Content Lab to automatically extract relevant clips, timestamps, and snippets from recorded webinars and events using AI. Use these extracted moments as part of your content strategy, reducing the need for manual video editing and enabling faster content distribution across channels. (Source: Kris Rudeegraap, Episode #159)

**Use AI to create derivatives from original content, not to generate all content from scratch.** Rather than using AI to create all original content, invest in high-quality original work (e.g., a 30-page ebook written by a human writer) and then use AI to generate 45+ derivative pieces from it across channels (social, email, webinars, etc.) without sacrificing quality or authenticity. The AI accelerates distribution and repurposing, not creation. (Source: Kelly Hopping, Episode #255)

**Repurpose battle cards and assets into alternative formats using AI.** Instead of discarding existing competitive or enablement content, use AI tools like NotebookLM to convert battle cards or detailed assets into alternative formats (e.g., a 20-minute podcast, audio summary, or interactive guide). This allows sales teams to consume the same information in a more engaging format, increasing adoption without starting from scratch. (Source: Talya Heller, Episode #246)

**Convert fireside chats and customer conversations into blog content using AI transcription.** Record fireside chats, customer interviews, or partner conversations and use AI transcription tools to automatically generate transcripts. Convert these transcripts into blog posts or unique content pieces. This allows your content team to produce more content in less time while maintaining authenticity. (Source: Michael Cole, Episode #212)

**Use the 'Content Turkey' framework to carve a single piece of substantive content into multiple formats.** Take a single piece of substantive content (e.g., a weekly topical session) and carve it into multiple formats and snippets for distribution across different channels where your audience searches. This maximizes reach and visibility without requiring proportional content creation effort. (Source: Morgan Cole, Episode #315)

**Repurpose interview content and provide share kits to extend reach and amplification.** When you conduct interviews with customers, practitioners, or experts, repurpose that content across multiple formats and provide share kits to the interviewees. Share kits make it easy for them to share the content on their own channels, multiplying distribution without additional effort. (Source: Lindsay O'Brien, Episode #304)

**Conduct an interview-based content flywheel for brand building.** Conduct podcast-style interviews with internal subject matter experts and external customers/non-customers aligned to a specific strategic message. Repurpose each interview into multiple formats: written content, video clips, and webinar assets. This creates a scalable content machine that injects human personality and authentic voices into brand messaging while serving both awareness and lead generation functions. (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

## Reposting and Content Longevity

**When content achieves market fit, continue to milk and reuse that story repeatedly.** When you create a piece of content that resonates with your audience (evidenced by engagement, shares, or conversions), don't move on to the next topic. Recognize that you've found content market fit and continue to tell that story over and over again in different formats and channels. Reuse the same core narrative, update it with new data or visuals, and redistribute it. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Reuse proven stories across new audiences and time periods without fear of fatigue.** Don't worry about content fatigue or repeating yourself. Most people didn't see your original content, and new people enter your market constantly. The same stories that worked four years ago will work today with new audiences. Add new data, new research, or new visuals to the same core story and redistribute it. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Overcome "same song syndrome"—repeat successful content with new angles instead of always creating new topics.** Identify content that performs well and reshare it with different hooks or framings. Since only a small percentage of your audience sees any given piece of content at any given time (e.g., less than 5% of LinkedIn followers), repeating the same topic with a fresh angle is not boring to your audience—it's necessary reach. (Source: Danielle Messler, Episode #169)

**Repost high-performing content on a recurring basis.** Identify your top-performing posts using LinkedIn Analytics or Shield, then repost them on a recurring schedule (e.g., every 3–6 months). Most of your followers will not have seen the original post due to feed ephemerality, and the content will perform well again. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #275, #262, #172)

**Repurpose high-performing content every 3 weeks.** Use tools like Shield to identify posts from 2–3 years ago that performed well. Repost that same content periodically because it will generate similar engagement. If a post performed well before, it will perform well again. This signals that the topic is evergreen and valuable, and you can then expand it into talks, videos, or other formats. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #139)

**Replay successful content multiple times across different audiences and channels.** Once you identify content that works, don't publish it once and move on. Replay that content multiple times to different segments of your audience and across different channels. Most of your audience won't see a single post, so repeating successful content ensures it reaches more people. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #200)

**Refresh dated content by updating tone and language.** When repurposing older high-performing content, review it for language or framing that may feel dated or misaligned with current audience sentiment. Update specific words or phrases to match current cultural context without changing the core idea. For example, replace language like "hustle" with "work hard" if the original resonates but the terminology now triggers negative reactions. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episodes #209 and #121)

**Identify and recycle top-performing posts by changing headlines and hooks to give them new life.** Use LinkedIn's analytics to find your highest-performing posts from the past 365–730 days. Repurpose these posts by updating the headline, hook, or framing while keeping the core story intact. Schedule these recycled posts alongside new content to maintain a consistent posting cadence. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #156)

## LinkedIn Distribution

**Post LinkedIn content in the 6–9 AM Eastern window for maximum reach.** LinkedIn posts perform best when published between 6–9 AM Eastern time. Avoid third-party scheduling tools due to reports of account penalties; scheduled posts may receive less engagement than posts published when the account owner is actively present on the platform. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #298 and #225)

**Include links in LinkedIn posts by leading with standalone value, not the link itself.** When promoting a webinar, article, or resource on LinkedIn, write a post that delivers value on its own and can stand alone without the link. The post should address a related topic or tell a story that makes the reader want to learn more, then include the link as a natural next step. (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episode #262)

**Leverage first-hour comment velocity on LinkedIn for algorithmic boost.** Post content on LinkedIn and actively encourage your team to comment within the first hour of publication. LinkedIn's algorithm prioritizes posts with high comment velocity in the first hour, so orchestrating team engagement during this window significantly increases reach. (Source: David Kline, Episode #138)

**Amplify high-performing organic LinkedIn posts with paid thought leader ads.** After identifying organic posts that perform well, amplify them using LinkedIn's thought leader ads feature. This approach is more efficient than spending paid budget upfront to test messaging—you first validate what resonates organically, then amplify winners. (Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Empower team members beyond the founder to post on LinkedIn to amplify brand presence.** Once the founder is posting consistently, expand LinkedIn presence by getting other team members (CMO, head of brand, sales leaders, customer success managers) to post regularly. This creates multiple touchpoints in the feed, makes the company appear "everywhere," and brings diverse perspectives to your audience. (Source: Tommy Clark, Episode #171)

**Use the company LinkedIn page as a news feed for all content and product updates, with zero-click value.** Post all company content—webinars, blog posts, product launches—to the company LinkedIn page with a focus on zero-click content: a standalone insight or takeaway that readers get without clicking a link. Employees then amplify by reposting from their personal pages with their own perspective. (Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**Mine internal communications and conversations for LinkedIn content.** Systematically convert internal work communications into LinkedIn posts. When you write Slack messages, internal memos, quarterly updates, or answers to team questions, identify sections that could be repurposed as LinkedIn content. If you answer 20 questions from a marketing team in a Google Doc, chunk those answers into individual LinkedIn posts over the following weeks. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #275)

**Build employee-amplified LinkedIn distribution by connecting with target customers first.** Systematically connect with 500–1,000 target customers on LinkedIn before launching content campaigns. This creates an opted-in audience that sees your content in their feed when employees and executives post. The connection acceptance rate from real decision-makers is typically 70–80%, creating multiple distribution points for company content without paid media. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

**Implement a repeatable LinkedIn growth system: posting frequency, engagement, and comment management.** Post twice daily, engage with ten specific users daily, and spend at least 30 minutes responding to comments on your own posts immediately after publishing. This creates a consistent, measurable approach to growing followers and engagement. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episode #244)

**Use LinkedIn Creator Mode and Featured Section to gate and promote content.** Enable LinkedIn Creator Mode on the executive's profile to unlock the ability to pin gated content in the featured section. Additionally, upgrade to LinkedIn Premium to add a website link in the profile. Use this link to drive traffic to a gated resource (e.g., newsletter signup, whitepaper). (Note: gating is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Devin Reed, Episode #196)

**Prioritize LinkedIn and Twitter for text-based content distribution.** LinkedIn and Twitter (X) are the best channels for B2B content distribution because they have the lowest barrier to success: written text. You don't need to produce highly polished videos. You can learn and iterate quickly with text-based posts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #139)

**Separate content concept from delivery channel; test multiple formats.** Develop your core content idea (the hook, the concept, the value) independently from the delivery mechanism (webinar, podcast, article, event, etc.). Once you have a strong concept, test it across multiple formats to see which resonates most with your audience. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #155)

**Extract reusable "bits" from conversations and meetings for content.** During conversations, webinars, or meetings, listen for moments where an analogy, joke, or framing lands well with the audience. Write these down as reusable "bits" that can be repurposed across different channels (LinkedIn, short-form content, presentations). The bits become building blocks for your content strategy. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #257)

## Video Distribution

**Repurpose long-form content into YouTube Shorts to drive awareness and build audience.** Take podcast episodes or long-form video content and clip them into 60-second YouTube Shorts. This format drives significantly more views and engagement than the original long-form content alone. People can become fans of your brand by watching short clips without consuming the full episode. Track which clips drive the most engagement to inform future content topics. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

**Edit content individually for each platform to improve algorithmic distribution.** When posting the same root video across multiple platforms, apply platform-specific edits (different cover photos, filters, music, text boxes, captions) rather than uploading the same file unchanged. Instagram's algorithm rewards content it recognizes as unique to the platform and reduces distribution for content that appears to be cross-posted without modification. (Source: Jenn Herman, Episode #168)

**Adapt content format and tone to channel norms; don't force B2B messaging onto consumer-native platforms.** When distributing to consumer-native platforms (TikTok, YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels), adapt content to match platform norms and audience expectations rather than forcing traditional B2B messaging. High production value can read as advertisement; short-form, unpolished, personality-driven content performs better. Commit fully to the channel's format or don't use it; half-measures fail. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #145)

**Be aware of platform-specific video length requirements when repurposing content.** Different platforms have different video length requirements that may prevent easy repurposing. For example, LinkedIn videos can be 1.5+ minutes, but YouTube Shorts requires 1-minute videos. Accept that you may need to create platform-specific content if you want to expand beyond your primary platform. (Source: Tim Davidson, Episode #127)

## Podcast Strategy and Distribution

**Use a podcast as an "awareness magnet" to build top-of-funnel brand awareness rather than a direct lead generation tool.** Create a podcast designed to continuously attract people to your brand by providing value independent of your product. The podcast should not be about selling your product. Measure success not just by direct pipeline attribution but by secondary signals like growth in your personal social following, registrations for related events, and general buzz. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #321)

**View podcasting as a multi-purpose business engine, not just a direct sales channel.** Measure podcast success beyond direct pipeline attribution. A podcast can serve multiple functions: building brand trust, generating content insights that feed marketing and product strategy, interviewing potential customers, and creating touchpoints that contribute to long-term relationship building. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #320)

**Launch a podcast that serves your audience's needs, not your product's narrative.** Create a podcast that is valuable to your target audience independent of your product. Instead of talking about your solution, focus on problems your audience faces. If you find yourself constantly steering episodes back to your product, scrap the concept and pivot. The podcast should power multiple content formats (newsletter, social clips, written summaries) to maximize ROI. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

**Position podcasts as both brand-building and lead nurturing, not direct response.** Podcasts serve dual purposes: building brand awareness, affinity, and consideration over time, while also nurturing leads asynchronously. Recognize that podcasts are not direct-response channels and won't generate immediate sales. Someone may listen for 6 months before becoming a customer when a catalyst triggers a purchase decision. Measure success through brand metrics and long-term engagement. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #137)

**Market webinars as optional live attendance with guaranteed recording access.** Stop treating live webinar attendance as a required metric or lead quality indicator. Instead, market webinars by explicitly stating that live attendance is optional and that recordings will be sent to all registrants. Write this into the marketing copy: "We'd love to have you live for the chat and discussion, but if you can't make it, no worries—we'll send you the recording." (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #137)

**Appear on multiple B2B podcasts to build awareness and create content repurposing opportunities.** Commit to appearing on 8–10+ B2B podcasts per month, even if individual podcast audiences are small (50–200 listeners). Each appearance builds credibility with a fanatical, devoted audience. Record these appearances and repurpose them: slice short-form clips by idea, extract key questions and insights, and turn them into LinkedIn posts and content. The cumulative effect of multiple small audiences is more valuable than chasing one large platform. (Source: Adam Robinson, Episode #157)

**Use podcast recording and re-listening as a creative outlet and mechanism for refining strategic thinking.** Record long-form podcast content (20–30 minutes uninterrupted) as a way to externalize and test ideas in real time. Then re-listen to your own recordings to identify strong points, refine language, spot product positioning insights, and cement ideas into repeatable frameworks. (Note: episode length is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #281)

**Position podcast as anchor piece of integrated content strategy, not standalone channel.** When pitching a podcast to sales or leadership, frame it as part of an integrated content strategy that serves multiple purposes: CEO visibility, sales enablement, content generation (clips, posts, articles), and brand building. Show how each podcast episode generates multiple content assets. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

## Email and Newsletter Distribution

**Build email lists by creating valuable content first, then promoting it.** Start by identifying what content your target audience values and needs. Create that content (e.g., reports, guides, research). Then promote it via paid ads, social media, or events to drive signups. Do not start by trying to build a list; start by creating content worth subscribing for. (Source: Sara McNamara, Episode #256)

**Build welcome sequences based on available content, not arbitrary email count.** Do not create a welcome sequence with a predetermined number of emails. Instead, determine how much valuable content you can create, then build the sequence around that. Start with one email and expand as you create more content. Sending low-value emails to meet a quota will increase unsubscribes and harm deliverability. (Source: Sara McNamara, Episode #256)

**Build nurture sequences around whitepaper content, not additional downloads.** When someone downloads a whitepaper, don't ask them to download another asset in the follow-up nurture sequence. Instead, break down the whitepaper's key insights across multiple emails and deliver them in digestible pieces. Reference the whitepaper they already downloaded, remind them why they downloaded it, and then expand on specific sections or insights from that document. (Source: Samar Owais, Episode #247)

**Build newsletters with 50% evergreen content and 50% ephemeral content to reduce production burden and increase reusability.** Structure newsletters so that half the content is evergreen (timeless, high-quality pieces that can be reused indefinitely) and half is ephemeral (current events, timely value). Send evergreen content on a fixed schedule (e.g., Wednesdays) and ephemeral content on another (e.g., Sundays). Evergreen emails can be resent to new subscribers without filters or concerns about repetition. (Note: newsletter cadence is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Brendan Hufford, Episode #242)

**Add a personality section to newsletters to increase time-on-page and reader retention.** Include a recurring section in your newsletter (e.g., "Since You Didn't Ask") where you share personal, non-marketing content—random observations, pop culture takes, office anecdotes, or anything unrelated to your core topic. This gives readers a reason to stay until the end of the newsletter beyond just marketing offers. It humanizes the sender and makes the newsletter feel like it's from a real person, not a brand. (Source: Jay Schwedelson, Episode #329)

**Only send emails when you have a specific, defensible reason—never to meet a sending quota.** Establish a principle that every email must serve one of two purposes: (1) share an idea or insight you believe is important and worth your audience's time, or (2) promote a product feature, event, or initiative that drives business results. Never send an email simply because it's been a while or to hit a sending target. (Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**Build newsletter content by curating and storytelling around existing podcast episodes.** Instead of creating original newsletter content from scratch, listen to high-performing podcast episodes and extract key frameworks or insights. Wrap these in a personal story or hook, then present the guest's framework or takeaway. The overlap in audience is minimal—less than 5% of LinkedIn followers see any given post, so repurposing is not redundant. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #169)

**Test multiple content formats in recurring channels to find what resonates.** In a recurring channel like a newsletter, intentionally vary the format and content type across issues to learn what your audience responds to best. Try personal notes, listicles, thought pieces, webinar recaps, how-to guides, etc. Track which formats generate the most engagement and responses. Then deliberately increase the frequency of high-performing formats in your editorial calendar. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #314)

**Measure newsletter success beyond open rate.** In addition to unique open rate, track total open rate (which includes people reopening the email) and monitor how often readers return to old newsletters. Also measure downstream engagement by repurposing newsletter content into video, LinkedIn posts, or blog content and tracking shares, comments, and reach. (Source: Arielle Gordis, Episode #166)

**Layer multiple touchpoints into nurture strategy beyond email alone.** Nurture sequences should not rely solely on email. Incorporate multiple channels and signals: website retargeting, paid ads, podcast listening, video consumption, and other owned properties. Track when prospects return to your website or engage with other content properties as signals of engagement, not just email opens and clicks. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

## Gated vs. Ungated Content

*(Note: This is a contested topic — see Where Experts Disagree for the full breakdown of positions.)*

**Publish ungated content with optional soft email capture to maximize reach and conversion.** Make primary content freely accessible without a gate, but include a non-coercive email capture option (e.g., a popup offering to send the content via email). Erin May's team achieved a 13% email conversion rate on ungated content by offering a compelling reason to subscribe without forcing users to provide their email to access the content. (Source: Erin May, Episode #337)

**Build an ungated content hub and use your own product to track engagement and attribute it to pipeline.** Create a centralized, ungated landing page that aggregates your best content—videos, shows, podcasts, blog posts—organized by category. Do not gate content behind email forms. Instead, use your own analytics/attribution product to track engagement and match that engagement to known prospects via email or CRM data. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Gate content based on ad budget size and asset value.** For small ad budgets, gate valuable content (proprietary data, annual reports) behind a form to capture leads you can follow up with, since limited impressions mean low conversion probability without follow-up. For larger budgets, ungate content to increase sharing and organic reach. Only gate content that represents genuine value exchange—not generic blog posts. (Source: Anthony Blatner, Episodes #228 and #152)

## Organic vs. Paid Content Strategy

*(Note: This is a contested topic — see Where Experts Disagree for the full breakdown of positions.)*

**Test content organically first, then amplify with paid.** Create 10 pieces of content and distribute them organically to identify which ones resonate with your audience before spending paid media budget. Once you have clear data showing which content performs well organically, use paid distribution (thought leader ads, etc.) to amplify only the validated winners. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #139)

**Use content performance data to inform and improve paid media campaigns.** Leverage insights from your content strategy (newsletter, podcast, social, blog) to identify which topics, themes, and messaging resonate most with your audience. Use this data to inform paid media creative and targeting. A strong content machine provides tested ideas, audience feedback, and high-performing topics that can be quickly turned into ad campaigns. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #141)

**Use organic content performance data to inform paid media strategy.** Track which organic content pieces get the most engagement and comments. Use these insights to identify which messages and topics resonate most with your audience. Feed these validated messages into paid media campaigns rather than guessing what will work. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

## Influencer and Partner Distribution

**Invest in influencer marketing for B2B by collaborating on content and leveraging their channels.** Influencer marketing is viable for B2B, not just B2C. Identify influencers in your industry and collaborate with them in two ways: (1) work with them to create content that helps you understand what messaging resonates with your audience, and (2) use their channels to promote your content. This works across different business models and buyer types, including enterprise and infrastructure. (Source: Ido Mart, Episode #229)

**Co-create content with influencers rather than forcing brand messages.** When working with influencers, don't force boilerplate brand messaging into their content. Instead, show them your product, understand their existing perspective and angle, and workshop content together that feels authentic to their voice and audience. If the resulting post won't be authentic to the influencer, it won't get distribution and people will see through it. (Source: Kevin White, Episode #179)

**Extract and distribute partner-created content across multiple owned channels.** When partners create content for your audience, don't limit it to one channel. Extract that content and place it across podcast, newsletter, social media (YouTube, LinkedIn, Twitter), and events. This multiplies the reach and impact of the content while building trust through repeated exposure to partner voices. (Source: Jared Fuller, Episode #174)

## Sales Team as Distribution Channel

**Train sales team to build personal brands and share content on social media.** Empower your sales team to build their own social media presence and share content about your industry, products, and customer problems. Sales reps with strong personal brands also close deals faster and build stronger customer relationships. Provide them with training on how to show up on social, help them answer FAQs from calls as content, and use tools like ChatGPT to convert their voice notes into polished posts. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episodes #209 and #121)

**Convert sales FAQs into social content via voice notes and ChatGPT.** Ask sales reps to record voice notes answering their most frequently asked customer questions. Feed those voice notes into ChatGPT with the prompt "Write me a LinkedIn post based on what I said." Have reps then adjust the output to match their tone and post. This converts real customer pain points into authentic, valuable social content while reducing the friction of content creation for sales teams. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Partner demand gen with brand/community team to leverage organic insights.** The brand and community team should be the best partner to demand gen. They have thousands of data points from organic engagement (comments, shares, questions) that reveal what messages resonate. Demand gen should use these insights to inform paid campaigns rather than guessing. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

## Content Hubs and Mid-Funnel Distribution

**Use a content hub to build trust with prospects in the consideration phase by showcasing familiar voices and partners.** When prospects are evaluating your solution, they want to know who trusts you and who you work with. A content hub that features recognizable industry voices, customers, and partners serves as a trust signal. Prospects see familiar faces they follow on LinkedIn, watch content from people they respect, and infer that your company is credible. The hub becomes a "rabbit hole" where prospects spend hours consuming content, which strengthens their confidence in your solution. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Drive webinar attendance and distribution by creating shareable, high-value content that audiences promote organically.** Rather than relying solely on paid promotion or gating to drive attendance, create webinars with such high perceived value that attendees become advocates. Pick hot, timely topics; deliver genuine insights and expert perspectives; make content easily shareable. Attendees will screen-record segments, share clips on LinkedIn and TikTok, and create their own summaries. (Source: Eoin Clancy, Episode #326)

**Expect and embrace disagreement on research reports; controversy drives engagement and reach without requiring additional paid promotion.** When publishing original research with strong findings or contrarian takes, anticipate that some audience members will disagree publicly. Rather than avoiding controversy, recognize that disagreement and debate increase organic reach and engagement. Do not polarize on purpose, but do not shy away from publishing data-driven findings that may challenge conventional wisdom. (Source: Emir Atli, Episode #165)

**Tease event details publicly for 2–3 months before ticket sales to build anticipation.** Begin mentioning the event on social media 2–3 months before ticket sales go live. Don't wait for the official announcement to start building awareness. Post periodically about the event concept, venue, or speakers to keep it top-of-mind and build pent-up demand. (Source: Dan Murphy, Episode #147)

## Original Content and AI

**Focus content strategy on creating net-new, original research, real experience, and expert insight.** Use AI tools to distribute this original content faster, not to generate the foundational content itself. 75% of internet content is already AI-generated, so differentiation comes from human-created original data and expertise, which AI then amplifies through distribution channels. (Source: Jess Lytle, Episode #319)

**Use podcast recording and re-listening as a mechanism for refining strategic thinking.** Record long-form podcast content (20–30 minutes uninterrupted) as a way to externalize and test ideas in real time. Then re-listen to your own recordings to identify strong points, refine language, spot product positioning insights, and cement ideas into repeatable frameworks. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #281)

**Coordinate company-wide video takeovers with employee participation.** For major product launches or announcements, ask employees across all departments to record short (under 1 minute) videos explaining why the launch matters. Provide a script and talking points but allow flexibility. Share videos on the same day to create a coordinated wave of content across employee networks. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #134)

**Integrate reviews into all marketing channels rather than running isolated review campaigns.** Instead of running one-off review campaigns, weave customer reviews and testimonials into your ongoing marketing efforts across all channels—website pages, email campaigns, ads, content, etc. This approach treats reviews as a core marketing asset rather than a separate initiative. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #143)

**Reposition customer success stories as news announcements.** Take long-form customer success stories and repurpose them as short news-style announcements (4 paragraphs) with a customer quote. Frame them as PR announcements rather than sales enablement content. This makes them more consumable for social media and positions them as news rather than promotional content. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #139)

**Balance consistent content cadence with opportunistic, attention-grabbing moments.** Combine predictable content rhythm (regular publishing schedule, consistent distribution across channels, clear calls-to-action to drive subscribers) with spontaneous, high-impact moments designed to break through attention clutter. The rhythm ensures relevance and builds habit; the jazz creates viral moments. Neither alone drives significant subscriber growth; both are required. (Source: Anthony Kennada, Episode #145)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should B2B marketers gate their content behind forms?
**Support summary: 6 (anti-gating) vs. 5 (pro-gating, strategic)**

This is one of the most actively debated topics across Exit Five guests, and the disagreement is genuine for most B2B SaaS marketers.

**Position A: Don't gate content — distribute freely**

Six guests argue that gating content degrades trust, reduces reach, and provides weak intent signals in an era where prospect data is available via tools like Apollo and ZoomInfo:

- **Tom Wentworth (Episode #304)** argued that gating prevents content from being discovered, indexed, and shared by AI systems and humans alike. In an AI-driven buying world, free distribution increases the likelihood your content surfaces in AI research and gets shared across networks.
- **Chelsea Castle (Episodes #262 and #172)** argued that gating written content typically degrades trust and brand perception because it creates a bait-and-switch experience. For B2B SaaS, email addresses from gating aren't strong intent signals since you can get them via Apollo or ZoomInfo anyway. Recommended building audience and trust by giving valuable content away freely.
- **Andrew Davies (Episode #195)** recommended replacing gated ebook content with free product access when the business model allows self-serve signup, arguing this reduces friction and increases product trial.
- **Emir Atli (Episode #165)** built an ungated content hub ("Flow") and used their own analytics product to track engagement and attribute it to pipeline, explicitly not gating content behind email forms.
- **Erin May (Episode #337)** published primary content freely without a gate but included a non-coercive optional email capture, achieving a 13% email conversion rate without forcing access restrictions.

**Position B: Gate strategically on high-value assets**

Five guests argue that gating is still valid when applied to genuinely high-value assets and when ad budgets are small enough that follow-up is necessary:

- **Ross Simmonds (Episode #224)** recommended gating high-value, in-depth resources (checklists, templates, detailed guides) behind a form to capture lead information, arguing gated assets still drive conversion and can be effective for lead generation.
- **Anthony Blatner (Episodes #228 and #152)** offered the most nuanced framework: gate for small ad budgets where limited impressions mean low conversion probability without follow-up; ungate for larger budgets to increase sharing and organic reach. Only gate content representing genuine value exchange (proprietary data, research)—not generic blog posts.
- **Dave Gerhardt (Episode #256)** recommended building opt-in lists using a high-value gated offer (report, guide, benchmark data) based on customer research, promoted via paid ads to drive signups with a welcome sequence.
- **Devin Reed (Episode #196)** recommended using LinkedIn Creator Mode to pin gated content in the featured section, describing this as a top growth lever for driving MQLs and website traffic.

**Context dependency:** Anthony Blatner's budget-dependent framework partially dissolves the conflict for large-budget scenarios—both camps agree that ungating makes sense when you have enough impressions to generate conversions without capturing contact info. However, the core disagreement persists for mid-market B2B SaaS companies with moderate budgets: Tom Wentworth, Chelsea Castle, and Andrew Davies argue against gating categorically for B2B SaaS, while Ross Simmonds and Devin Reed argue for gating high-value assets regardless of budget.

**Trend note:** The anti-gating voices are more concentrated in recent episodes (2025–2026: Tom Wentworth ep. 304, Chelsea Castle ep. 262, Erin May ep. 337, Andrew Davies ep. 195), while pro-gating voices span a wider date range. This suggests a field shift toward ungating, though pro-gating voices (Ross Simmonds ep. 224, Anthony Blatner ep. 228) also appear in 2025.

**Why it matters:** Gating decisions directly affect content reach, AI indexability, and lead quality—getting this wrong either starves your pipeline of contact data or throttles the organic distribution that builds brand awareness and AI search visibility.

### 2. Should organic content be created independently of paid media, or should all content be designed to fuel paid campaigns?
**Support summary: 3 (organic-first) vs. 1 (paid-first)**

**Position A: Create organically first, then amplify winners with paid**

Three guests argue for validating content organically before spending paid budget:

- **Chris Walker (Episode #139)** recommended creating 10 pieces of content organically to identify which resonate before spending paid media budget. Use paid distribution only to amplify validated organic winners. This reverses the old approach of running all content in ads and hoping something works.
- **Tommy Clark (Episode #171)** recommended identifying organic posts that perform well first, then amplifying them using LinkedIn's thought leader ads. Organic content provides real-time feedback on what your audience cares about before paid amplification.
- **Dave Gerhardt (Episode #134)** recommended using organic content performance data to inform paid media strategy, creating a feedback loop where organic performance informs paid campaigns.

**Position B: Design all content to fuel paid campaigns from the start**

- **Ryan Narod (Episode #330)** stated explicitly: "Do not create content purely for organic distribution." All creative assets should be developed with the intention of amplifying through paid channels. Even scrappy, low-cost content should be backed by paid media to maximize reach and ensure accountability for creative production. Measure performance through paid media metrics and incrementality testing.

**Context dependency:** Ryan Narod's paid-first approach may reflect a specific company context (larger budget, performance-marketing-oriented culture) where organic reach is insufficient to validate messaging at scale. The organic-first advocates may be speaking to companies with smaller paid budgets or stronger organic audiences. However, the tactical advice directly conflicts: Narod says don't create content purely for organic; Walker says create organically first to validate before spending paid budget.

**Why it matters:** The sequencing of organic vs. paid content strategy determines how marketing budget is allocated and how quickly messaging is validated—getting this wrong either wastes paid budget on unproven content or leaves organic insights unused.

### 3. Should B2B newsletters publish at high frequency or reduce cadence to avoid fatigue?
**Support summary: 4 (maintain high frequency) vs. 1 (reduce cadence)**

**Position A: Reduce cadence to avoid audience fatigue**

- **Sara Lattanzio (Episode #268)** recommended shifting to monthly or less frequent cadence to avoid exhausting audiences. Argued that lower cadence makes subscribers anticipate the newsletter like a seasonal release, improving engagement and retention. Content saturation is hitting a cap, and less frequent, higher-quality content outperforms constant output.

**Position B: Maintain high-frequency, quality-filtered sending**

Four guests argue for maintaining consistent, high-frequency output with quality as the filter—not frequency reduction:

- **Amanda Goetz (Episode #244)** recommended posting twice daily on LinkedIn and building a content flywheel where social drives newsletter traffic, implying high-frequency content output is the growth mechanism.
- **Amanda Natividad (Episode #205)** recommended only sending emails when you have a specific, defensible reason—but framed this as a quality filter, not a frequency reduction. The implication is that high-quality sends at consistent cadence are the goal.
- **Jay Schwedelson (Episode #329)** recommended adding personality sections to newsletters to increase time-on-page and reader retention—a tactic oriented toward sustaining engagement at existing cadence rather than reducing frequency.
- **Brendan Hufford (Episode #242)** recommended a framework with two newsletter sends per week (Wednesdays for evergreen, Sundays for ephemeral), explicitly maintaining high cadence by using 50% evergreen content to reduce production burden rather than reducing frequency.

**Context dependency:** Sara Lattanzio's advice may apply more to content-heavy newsletters competing in saturated niches, while the high-frequency advocates may be speaking to newsletters with strong differentiation or personal brand backing. However, both camps are addressing the same general B2B newsletter context, making this a genuine disagreement.

**Why it matters:** Newsletter cadence directly affects subscriber retention, deliverability, and brand recall—over-sending risks list churn while under-sending risks losing top-of-mind awareness with prospects in long buying cycles.

### 4. Should B2B podcast episodes be short (under 25 minutes) or longer?
**Support summary: 4 (long-form) vs. 1 (short episodes)**

**Position A: Keep episodes short (18–22 minutes) for algorithmic distribution**

- **Jay Schwedelson (Episode #329)** cited data showing shorter episodes get more downloads than longer ones regardless of content quality. Listeners decide based on title and length before committing. Recommended recording longer interviews but breaking them into multiple 20-minute segments to increase total downloads and improve algorithmic distribution on Apple Podcasts and Spotify.

**Position B: Long-form episodes serve brand depth and nurture quality**

Four guests argue for longer formats optimized for trust-building and content depth:

- **Chris Walker (Episode #281)** recommended recording 20–30 minutes of uninterrupted long-form podcast content as a mechanism for externalizing and testing ideas, then re-listening to refine strategic thinking. No mention of shortening for algorithmic optimization.
- **Dave Gerhardt (Episode #137)** positioned podcasts as brand-building and lead nurturing tools measured by long-term engagement, not immediate conversion or download counts. The Exit Five podcast itself runs longer episodes, implying length is not the primary optimization lever.
- **Dave Gerhardt (Episode #225)** recommended using weekly podcast interviews (one-hour format) as the core content production engine, extracting multiple formats from each full episode rather than splitting episodes into shorter segments.
- **Matt Carnevale (Episode #320)** framed podcasting as a multi-purpose business engine measured by brand trust, content insights, and relationship building—not by download counts or algorithmic distribution signals.

**Context dependency:** Jay Schwedelson's advice is explicitly about maximizing downloads and algorithmic distribution, while Chris Walker and Dave Gerhardt are optimizing for brand depth and nurture quality. A podcast optimizing for reach/discovery might follow Schwedelson's advice; one optimizing for depth and trust-building might follow the longer format. However, both camps are addressing the same B2B podcast context, making this a genuine strategic disagreement about what to optimize for.

**Why it matters:** Episode length affects both algorithmic distribution and the depth of value delivered to listeners—choosing the wrong optimization target could mean either poor discoverability or shallow content that fails to build the trust needed to convert listeners into customers.

## What NOT To Do

**Don't create content for every platform independently.** Creating unique content for every platform is unsustainable. Select two primary platforms to create for and distribute that content across all others. (Source: Chris Cunningham, Episode #347)

**Don't create content purely for organic distribution without a paid amplification plan** — at least according to one guest. (Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

**Don't publish once and move on.** A common mistake is creating content and then abandoning it after a single publish. Replay successful content multiple times to different segments of your audience and across different channels. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #200)

**Don't gate content unless it is genuinely high-value and exclusive.** Gating mediocre content creates a bait-and-switch experience that damages the brand. For B2B SaaS, email addresses obtained through gating are often not strong intent signals anyway. (Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Chelsea Castle, Episodes #262 and #172)

**Don't force B2B messaging onto consumer-native platforms without adapting to platform norms.** High production value can read as advertisement on TikTok, YouTube Shorts, and Instagram Reels. Commit fully to the channel's format or don't use it; half-measures fail. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #145)

**Don't send emails to fill a quota.** Never send an email simply because it's been a while or to hit a sending target. Every email must serve a specific, defensible purpose. (Source: Amanda Natividad, Episode #205)

**Don't create a welcome sequence with a predetermined number of emails.** Build the sequence around how much valuable content you can create, not an arbitrary email count. Sending low-value emails to meet a quota will increase unsubscribes and harm deliverability. (Source: Sara McNamara, Episode #256)

**Don't ask nurture email recipients to download another asset immediately after they've downloaded one.** Break down the whitepaper's key insights across multiple emails instead of asking for another commitment. (Source: Samar Owais, Episode #247)

**Don't create a podcast that's really just a product pitch.** If you find yourself constantly steering episodes back to your product, scrap the concept and pivot. The podcast should be valuable to your target audience independent of your product. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #266)

**Don't upload the same video file unchanged to every platform.** Apply platform-specific edits (different cover photos, filters, music, text boxes, captions) when cross-posting video. Instagram's algorithm rewards content it recognizes as unique to the platform. (Source: Jenn Herman, Episode #168)

**Don't spend too much effort creating campaigns and not enough on distributing and iterating them.** Campaigns need time to breathe, evolve, and run through multiple cycles to have impact. Create fewer campaigns and invest heavily in getting them in front of the right audience multiple times. (Source: Andrew Davies, Episode #195)

**Don't use AI to generate all original content from scratch.** Invest in high-quality original work and use AI to generate derivative pieces from it. The AI accelerates distribution and repurposing, not creation. (Source: Kelly Hopping, Episode #255)

**Don't let valuable data and insights live only within white papers.** Pull out key statistics, quotes, and findings and reshare them across social media channels. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200)

**Don't co-create influencer content by forcing boilerplate brand messaging.** Show influencers your product, understand their existing perspective, and workshop content together that feels authentic to their voice and audience. (Source: Kevin White, Episode #179)
