# Marketing Career Development

## Overview
This skill covers career development practices for B2B marketers, from individual contributors building foundational skills to senior leaders navigating CMO-level decisions. It addresses skill-building, role transitions, job evaluation, networking, and leadership development. All practices are sourced exclusively from guests on the Exit Five podcast and attributed to the specific episode in which they appeared.

---

## Skill Development and Specialization

### Identify Your Core Strength and Build Mastery
Double down on your single strongest skill rather than attempting to become well-rounded across all marketing disciplines. The impact of becoming A-grade in one skill (e.g., writing, copywriting, content strategy) far exceeds the marginal gains from lifting a weak skill from D to B. Leaders still need foundational knowledge across business areas but do not need hard skills in every discipline — they can hire and delegate to specialists. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

Use generalist years at early-stage companies to explore and identify what you want to specialize in later. Specialization should be viewed as a point-in-time decision, not a permanent identity. It is easier to move a B-level skill to A-level than to move an F-level skill to C-level. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

**(Note: the question of whether to specialize deeply or maintain a generalist frame is actively contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)**

### Copywriting and Storytelling as Foundational Skills
Treat storytelling and copywriting as evergreen skills that will have the biggest return on your marketing career. Rather than chasing trendy growth tactics or trying to be well-rounded, focus on becoming excellent at telling stories and writing compelling copy. This skill compounds over time and is valuable across all marketing roles and companies. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #229)

When studying copywriting, select a single foundational book and study it thoroughly rather than reading multiple books superficially. A recommended starting point is *The Copywriter's Handbook* by Robert Bly; other solid options include *Ogilvy on Advertising* or *The Adweek Copywriting Handbook*. Most copywriting books share similar core lessons, so depth in one book is more valuable than breadth across many. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

**(Note: the question of deep vs. selective reading is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)**

Build a swipe file of high-quality copy examples — screenshots from LinkedIn ads, emails, landing pages, and newsletters — organized in a tool like Evernote. Review this file regularly to study what makes certain copy effective. The act of collecting and reviewing trains your eye for good copywriting. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

Establish a daily writing practice by publishing on a public channel like LinkedIn or Twitter. Write about a specific topic related to your expertise, set a timer for 30 minutes, and treat each piece as an opportunity to apply copywriting principles. If public writing isn't feasible, maintain a private daily writing practice in a Google Doc with the same discipline. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

If you manage or write a weekly newsletter, use it as your primary copywriting practice ground. Each week provides a natural deadline and feedback loop through open rates, click rates, and direct responses — 52 opportunities per year to test, learn, and improve. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #129)

### Building Technical and Creative Muscles
When moving from a content or creative role to VP of Marketing, identify two specific muscles to build: (1) metrics and reporting — learn to track what's working, build dashboards, and distinguish between noise and signal in data; (2) team leadership — managing and developing a marketing team requires different skills than managing creative work. Be explicit about these gaps and create a plan to address them. Partner with specialists (e.g., a RevOps lead) to build reporting infrastructure. (Source: Jess Cook, Episode #321)

Develop the ability to speak on camera as a fundamental marketing skill. If you tense up thinking about being on camera, practice by recording videos you never show anyone — the goal is to build the muscle and get comfortable. You cannot effectively brief a video hire or agency if you haven't experimented with being on camera yourself. (Source: Connor Lewis, Episode #240)

Maintain a diverse reading diet of fiction, business books, and nonfiction to improve copywriting quality. Fiction expands vocabulary, teaches narrative structure, and forces creative thinking. Use tools like Readwise to resurface highlights from past reading daily as a creative primer before writing sessions. (Source: Will Hoekenga, Episode #181)

### Learning Habits and Continuous Development
Block one hour daily — ideally in the morning before operational demands take over — for learning, research, and strategic thinking. Use this time to listen to podcasts, read books, review industry research, or listen to customer calls. This prevents atrophy of marketing skills and keeps you sharp on industry trends. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

Establish a rule: if someone you find exceptionally smart and look up to recommends a book, buy it immediately without deliberation. The cost ($12–20) is negligible compared to the potential value. This removes friction from the decision to invest in learning and ensures you capture high-signal recommendations before forgetting them. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #298)

**(Note: how to read books once you have them is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)**

Use podcasting — even if it never grows an audience — as a practice medium for developing clear, concise points of view and thinking on your feet. The act of articulating ideas in conversation trains you to think more clearly and develop stronger opinions. The learning happens through the doing, not through audience size. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episode #257)

For marketers in smaller markets or regions that lag behind the US in marketing adoption, actively follow US-based marketers, SaaS companies, and communities to learn about emerging channels, content formats, and tactics before they reach your local market. Attend US conferences and events, join US-based communities, and engage with US marketing leaders to stay ahead of the curve. (Source: Max Van Den Ingh, Episode #161)

### AI and Emerging Tools
Push yourself to be an early adopter of AI tools and workflows. In every company, there are typically 1–2 people willing to experiment with new tools and break things. These early adopters gain outsized advantages because they learn the tools faster and can implement them before competitors catch up. Treat this like venture investing — make multiple bets, expect most to fail, but the winners will return significant value. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #279)

Teach junior marketers from day one that AI is part of their operating system, not an optional tool. Emphasize that success requires being more curious and strategic than AI itself — reading widely, building reference points, questioning outputs, and knowing which tool to use for which task. The goal is to shift from "finding information" to "discerning and questioning." (Source: Jennifer Cannizzaro, Episode #267)

## Career Planning and Trajectory

### The Two-Year Mission Framework
Structure your career in two-year increments with a clearly defined core mission for each period. This timeframe is long enough to earn your stripes and see meaningful impact, but short enough to maintain motivation. Use the last six months of each two-year cycle to identify the next mission. The framework applies whether you're early-career (e.g., aiming for a specific title at a US company) or senior (e.g., leading a major transformation). The key is having a foreseeable end point that lets you measure impact. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episodes #318 and #257)

### Specialization Strategy
As AI commoditizes middle-ground marketing skills, position yourself at one of two extremes: either become highly technical (understanding how to deploy AI across marketing workflows, thinking like an engineer about systems and automation) or become exceptionally creative (mastering ideation, storytelling, copywriting, and taste). The "messy middle" — being good enough at both but expert at neither — is where AI will have the most impact. Choose your lane and go deep. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episodes #318 and #257)

Rather than remaining a generalist marketer, identify an emerging or underserved category with minimal competition and high-quality content gaps. Become the category expert by systematically replicating what category leaders do (conferences, books, frameworks) but with better execution. This approach allows you to dominate mindshare and become the default expert in that space within five years. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**(Note: the specialization vs. generalist question is actively contested — see Where Experts Disagree.)**

### Expanding Responsibility Within a Role
Start with a core function (e.g., community manager) and identify adjacent functions that are underperforming or misaligned. Take ownership of those functions one at a time, expanding your responsibility in concentric circles. This allows you to grow your role and impact without waiting for a new position to open, and it naturally consolidates related functions under one leader. (Source: Dan Cmejla, Episode #134)

Attach yourself to the biggest unsolved business problem in the company and find a way to help solve it — whether that's sales qualification, positioning, activating a new distribution channel, or anything else critical to company growth. By becoming the person who helps solve that problem, you naturally earn a seat at the table and influence without needing to ask for it. First excel at your core job, then use that credibility to take on bigger problems. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

### Self-Awareness and Role Fit
Practice job crafting: even within a larger organization, identify big and small choices you can make to align your work with your values and preferences. Are you having lunch meetings every day? Can you find time for coffee with colleagues? Can you negotiate meeting times or project types? This requires self-awareness about your preferences and the courage to advocate for them. (Source: Kara Hardin, Episode #160)

Use a series of simple preference questions (hot or cold, movie or magazine, spring or summer) to build self-awareness about your dispositions and needs. Start with inconsequential preferences to develop the skill of identifying what you actually want, then expand to more consequential work preferences like management style, meeting frequency, and project types. (Source: Kara Hardin, Episode #160)

When deciding whether to leave a company, consider whether you're in over your head, whether a better opportunity exists, and whether your values align with the company's values. Performance issues and skill gaps can be worked through, but misaligned values are a red flag. (Source: Tara Robertson, Episode #188)

## Transitioning to Marketing Leadership

### The IC-to-VP Mindset Shift
When transitioning from individual contributor to leadership, recognize that the VP Marketing role is not an execution role but a strategic one focused on managing opinions, personalities, and cross-functional alignment. Expect your task list to shrink significantly as you delegate execution work. Use the freed-up mental space to think strategically: take walks, listen to podcasts, and consume high-quality information to generate bigger-picture ideas. Avoid immediately pushing new tactical ideas to your team; instead, present them casually as suggestions and let the team decide whether to pursue them. (Source: Jessica Andrews, Episode #217)

As you move from IC to VP level, your job shifts from executing the work to inspiring and leading people. Additionally, you must start thinking like the CMO or CEO — making decisions in the best interest of the entire company, even when suboptimal for your specific product line or business unit. This broader perspective is essential at VP+ levels and requires letting go of the need to control execution. (Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)

Tell your direct manager early and clearly that you aspire to a marketing leadership role. Managers who know about your leadership ambitions will flag you as "leadership potential" and actively create opportunities for you to develop the skills needed for that role. The earlier you communicate this, the more time your manager has to intentionally develop you through stretch assignments and cross-functional exposure. (Source: Jessica Andrews, Episode #217)

### Reaching CMO Level
Early in your marketing career, you own tactical execution and know every detail of your team's work. To reach CMO level, you must deliberately let go of day-to-day details and shift into a cross-functional leadership role. Your job becomes helping drive company strategy (where do we play, how do we win), serving as the glue across product, sales, and customer success, and ensuring marketing strategy ladders up to corporate strategy. This shift from execution to strategy is what earns you a seat at the executive table. (Source: Kady Srinivasan, Episode #276)

Understand that as a CMO, you cannot spend every day executing in your area of passion. CMO roles require constant context-switching between strategic planning, team management, and occasional tactical execution. If your passion comes primarily from day-to-day execution in one area, a CMO role may make you miserable. Assess whether you're willing to trade hands-on work for strategic leadership before pursuing the role. (Source: Mychelle Mollot, Episode #182)

Invest in executive coaching as a leadership development tool. A coach provides outside perspective, helps you see blind spots, and guides you through situations you haven't encountered before. This is especially valuable as you scale into VP and C-level roles where the stakes and complexity increase. Treat coaching as a non-negotiable investment in your growth. (Source: Matt Devincentis, Episode #260)

### Diagnosing CMO Failure Archetypes
Identify which of eight CMO failure archetypes you may embody and apply targeted remediation:
- **He Liquor** (chokes in board meetings): find a coach who understands both boards and marketing
- **Vendor Whisperer** (outsources all decisions to vendors): build internal decision-making capability
- **Finger Pointer** (defensive, blames others): listen more and talk less
- **Tasmania Devil** (chaotic, unfocused energy): slow down and communicate clearly
- **Sneaker** (gets undermined by other executives): build direct relationships with the CEO to prevent being undercut
- **Two Thirds** (stays in a narrow functional lane): expand cross-functional engagement
- **Second Guesser** (presents data that burns into decision-makers' minds without context): add context and framing to data presentations
- **Self Promoter** (overexposed externally): redirect energy internally

(Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

Establish yourself as a credible marketing professional by demonstrating expertise, studying the discipline, and learning from other examples. Do not let yourself be treated as an amateur or dabbler. Remind stakeholders that you study marketing, learn about it, and look at other examples. This is especially important if you transitioned from a technical or non-marketing background. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

## Evaluating and Entering New Roles

### Evaluating a Company Before Joining
Before accepting a marketing role, assess whether the product has achieved early product-market fit by looking for: (1) raving fans among early customers who actively advocate for the product, (2) clear articulation of what value the product provides to which user, and (3) evidence that customers want the product enough to pay for it without heavy persuasion. If a company lacks these signals, marketing efforts will fail not due to marketer incompetence but due to lack of product-market fit. (Source: Udi Ledergor, Episode #237)

Assess whether the CEO has marketing experience or a clear, articulated vision for how the company should be perceived in the market. CEOs without marketing background who hire early-stage marketers without authority or experience create a "deadly combination" that results in mediocre, consensus-driven marketing. A CEO with marketing credibility or vision provides permission and direction for courageous marketing decisions. (Source: Udi Ledergor, Episode #237)

Understand the company's financial situation — runway, funding stage, and cash position — as this dictates what marketing plays and bets you can make. A well-funded company with long runway allows for different strategic choices than a bootstrap company needing revenue in 60–90 days. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)

### CMO-Specific Interview Questions
When interviewing for a CMO role, ask:
- How does the company think about marketing budgeting and planning? (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)
- What is the founder's appetite to invest in long-term marketing and brand building versus short-term pipeline generation? This reveals whether you'll have freedom to make long-term bets or be under constant pressure to hit quarterly revenue targets. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)
- What are the top three things you want marketing to do? (Force-rank this question.) This uncovers whether the role aligns with your strengths and interests. If their priorities don't match your expertise or passion, it's a signal to walk away. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #142)

When evaluating a CMO opportunity, assess whether your marketing style and approach align with the CEO/founder's vision and leadership style. Use a sports analogy: a Chelsea-style direct football player will fail at Barcelona's possession-based style, even though both are successful. This fit is more important than company prestige or compensation. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

When considering a CMO role, establish 3–4 non-negotiable criteria upfront (e.g., geography, company scale, product portfolio, strategic fit) and use these to filter opportunities before beginning your search. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #128)

### Honesty About Role Fit
Be self-aware about whether you're willing to do hands-on execution work before accepting an early-stage marketing role. Many marketers claim they want early-stage roles but are unwilling to do the hands-on work (webinars, email campaigns, list building, HubSpot execution, etc.). If you're not willing to do this work yourself, don't take the job. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #142)

If you find yourself in a CMO or marketing role that isn't a perfect fit, don't immediately jump ship. Instead, identify specific skills or knowledge you can acquire during your tenure (e.g., sales-led go-to-market motion, automation, enterprise sales dynamics) and treat departing leaders or peers as mentors. Extract value from the experience and build capabilities that will serve you in future roles. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

### Compensation
Don't assume a raise will be offered; proactively negotiate by asking for a specific percentage increase (e.g., 20%) when changing jobs or during planned career conversations. Establish clear expectations with your manager about job performance, goals, and compensation review timing (quarterly or annual). Recognize that compensation is a legitimate career concern — it's acceptable to prioritize earning alongside learning. If a company lacks formal processes for raises and promotions, that's a signal about company maturity. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

## Building Credibility in a New Role

### Quick Wins and Early Impact
When stepping into a new marketing leadership position, prioritize shipping a small, visible win in week one or two that directly supports the quarterly goal. This builds credibility with leadership and the broader team, generates momentum, and buys you time and political capital to work on longer-term initiatives. Share the results publicly (e.g., in team meetings) to reinforce progress. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #133)

When joining a new company as a marketing leader, identify low-hanging fundamental gaps (e.g., missing Salesforce fields, event infrastructure) and fix them quickly. Frame these as favors for stakeholders whose trust you need. This builds credibility faster than large initiatives, helps you learn the business, and creates goodwill that makes it easier to propose and execute on larger strategic initiatives later. (Source: Taylor Udell, Episode #190)

When joining an early-stage company as a solo marketer, prioritize executing visible, measurable wins in the first six months (e.g., launching a blog, driving website traffic, building an email list) before attempting to influence strategic decisions like positioning. This execution track record earns you the right to be brought into higher-level conversations. Avoid spending weeks on research or frameworks before showing results. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #189)

**(Note: the question of quick wins vs. proactive idea generation without waiting for a brief is discussed — see Where Experts Disagree.)**

Don't wait for leadership to ask you to do the work. If you see an opportunity to tell a better story or solve a problem, do the work yourself and present it to leadership. If they like it, great. If not, move on. This approach gives you agency and demonstrates value. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

## Networking and Visibility

### Building Your Network Strategically
Revenue professionals tend to network only with peers in their function. Instead, intentionally build relationships with founders and CEOs. Attend events like SaaStr where you can meet founders in high density. These relationships are more valuable long-term than peer networks because founders are the decision-makers and can open doors that peers cannot. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episodes #207 and #142)

Develop a network of mentors and peers who don't work at your company and don't know your business. These relationships are valuable because they can give you unbiased, actionable advice without being influenced by internal politics or company dynamics. Reaching out to strangers in your industry for mentorship or advice is a high-ROI activity that many people are willing to do. (Source: Tara Robertson, Episode #188)

Rather than only posting your own content, actively comment on posts from people you want to be associated with or learn from. This curates your own feed by increasing visibility of their content and creates a halo effect where you become associated with those voices. Some practitioners have grown 10,000+ followers primarily through high-quality commenting. Top comments can also surface ideas for your own posts. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #138)

### Visibility Through Public Work
Speaking on webinars and sharing tactical work publicly serves as a talent identification mechanism. When you demonstrate expertise and execution publicly — showing real AI use cases, sharing prompts, engaging authentically with an audience — hiring managers can evaluate your actual work and thinking before any formal interview process. This is more efficient than traditional recruiting because it shows work in context rather than relying on resumes and references. (Source: Jess Lytle, Episode #319)

### Building an Agency or Consultancy
Build your methodology and authority while employed elsewhere before launching an agency or consultancy. Write about your approach, develop a point of view, and create content that demonstrates your expertise. This can start as a course or digital product before evolving into an agency. This path reduces risk and gives you proof of concept and an audience before you fully commit. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

Develop a proprietary, named methodology or framework to differentiate your agency or consultancy. Identify a specific problem you solve better than others, develop a repeatable process to solve it, and name it. Your methodology should emerge from your own experience solving a problem that existing frameworks didn't address. This gives you a clear positioning story, helps you attract ideal clients, and makes it easier to scale. (Source: Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

When starting an agency and lacking a public portfolio, approach potential clients and offer to do work for free in exchange for the ability to use that work as portfolio pieces. Complete 2–3 free projects over three months, learn from mistakes, and then launch with a strong portfolio and case studies to show future paid clients. (Source: Matt Carnevale, Episode #137)

When transitioning to fractional CMO work, apply the same brand positioning framework to yourself. Define your specific specialty (e.g., "pre-Series A brand readiness" rather than "growth marketing") and ideal client profile. The fractional model works best with companies that have existing operational rhythms, a CEO aligned on marketing's importance, and feedback loops with the team. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episode #158)

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should marketers specialize deeply or develop broad generalist skills?
**Support summary: 3 vs 3**

This is a genuine, evenly matched disagreement about how to position yourself for maximum career value.

**Position A — Specialize deeply at the outer edges (technical or creative):**
Kieran Flanagan (Episodes #318 and #257) argues that AI is commoditizing middle-ground marketing skills, so marketers must position at one of two extremes: highly technical (deploying AI across marketing workflows, thinking like an engineer) or exceptionally creative (ideation, storytelling, copywriting, taste). The "messy middle" — being competent but not exceptional at either — is where AI poses the biggest threat. Peep Laja (Episode #119) adds that identifying an emerging or underserved category and becoming the category expert through systematic execution allows you to dominate mindshare and become the default expert within five years.

**Position B — Double down on your core strength within a generalist frame:**
Dave Gerhardt (Episodes #298, #131, and #229) argues that the impact of becoming A-grade in one skill far exceeds marginal gains from lifting a weak skill, but this doesn't require abandoning a generalist role. He cites Peter Drucker's framework: it's easier to move a B-level skill to A-level than an F-level skill to C-level. He recommends using generalist years at early-stage companies to explore and identify what to specialize in later, and treating specialization as a point-in-time decision rather than a permanent identity. He specifically calls out storytelling and copywriting as the evergreen skill to double down on.

**Context dependency:** Kieran Flanagan and Peep Laja frame specialization as a response to AI commoditization and competitive market dynamics — a forward-looking argument. Dave Gerhardt's generalist-within-strength framing is more applicable to early-career marketers at early-stage companies who are still exploring. However, both camps are making claims about the same career question — how to position yourself for maximum value — so the disagreement is genuine even if the AI-commoditization angle is a newer framing.

**Trend note:** Kieran Flanagan's episodes (June 2025 and January 2026) both emphasize the AI-driven urgency to specialize at the extremes, suggesting a more recent shift toward the "pick a lane" view. Dave Gerhardt's generalist-friendly advice spans 2024–2025 without incorporating the AI commoditization argument.

**Why it matters:** A marketer choosing between deepening one skill versus maintaining breadth will make fundamentally different career investments in training, job selection, and personal branding. Getting this wrong could mean either becoming commoditized in the middle or over-specializing into a narrow lane that limits future optionality.

### 2. Should a new marketing leader prioritize quick wins immediately or invest time in research and strategy first?
**Support summary: 3 vs 1**

**Position A — Quick wins first for credibility (majority view):**
Dave Gerhardt (Episodes #133 and #189) and Taylor Udell (Episode #190) all advocate for shipping a small, visible win in week one or two. Dave Gerhardt explicitly states that early-stage marketers should avoid spending weeks on research or frameworks before showing results — execution track record earns you the right to be brought into higher-level conversations. Taylor Udell recommends identifying low-hanging fundamental gaps and fixing them quickly, framing these as favors for stakeholders whose trust you need.

**Position B — Proactive idea generation without waiting for permission:**
Harry Dry (Episode #303) argues that you shouldn't wait for leadership to ask you to do the work or give you a brief. If you see an opportunity, do the work yourself and present it. He describes working with Ramp without waiting for a brief — he met the founder, wrote the story, texted the founder, and it went live in hours.

**Context dependency:** These positions are more complementary than conflicting on close inspection — both advocate for action over deliberation. The quick-wins camp emphasizes internal credibility-building within a new role, while Harry Dry's advice is about external proactivity with clients and partners. This disagreement may dissolve when accounting for context: internal new-hire dynamics vs. external freelance or agency dynamics.

**Why it matters:** A new marketing leader who spends their first months on research and strategy frameworks without visible output risks losing stakeholder confidence before they can execute their vision; but moving too fast without understanding the business can produce misdirected quick wins.

### 3. Should marketers read books cover-to-cover or selectively skim for relevant chapters?
**Support summary: 2 vs 1**

**Position A — Read deeply and completely (majority view):**
Dave Gerhardt (Episodes #298 and #129) recommends instantly buying any book recommended by someone you respect and removing friction from the decision to invest in learning. For copywriting specifically, he recommends selecting a single foundational book and studying it thoroughly rather than reading multiple books superficially, arguing that most copywriting books share similar core lessons so depth in one is more valuable than breadth across many.

**Position B — Read selectively, skim for relevant chapters:**
Shane Murphy (Episode #173) argues that many business books contain one or two core ideas padded with filler content. He recommends scanning books for the most relevant chapters or sections that apply to your current challenges and treating them like blog posts, skipping the rest. This allows you to extract value from more books without the time commitment of reading cover-to-cover.

**Context dependency:** Dave Gerhardt's "study one book deeply" advice is specifically about copywriting books, while Shane Murphy's selective reading advice is about business books generally. However, both are making claims about how to extract learning value from books, and the underlying approaches genuinely conflict — one says go deep on one source, the other says skim many sources for relevant parts.

**Why it matters:** A marketer's reading strategy directly affects how they allocate limited learning time; committing to deep reading of fewer books versus skimming many books produces different knowledge profiles and different returns on time invested.

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not let yourself be treated as an amateur or dabbler.** Establish yourself as a credible marketing professional who studies the discipline. Do not allow sales or other executives to treat marketing as a tactical execution service without strategic input. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

- **Do not wait for a brief or formal approval before doing work you believe in.** If you see an opportunity, do the work and present it. Waiting for permission slows you down and reduces your agency. (Source: Harry Dry, Episode #303)

- **Do not accept a CMO role without assessing CEO/founder fit.** Misalignment between your marketing style and the CEO's vision will lead to frustration and failure regardless of other factors. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

- **Do not accept an early-stage marketing role if you're unwilling to do hands-on execution work.** Many marketers claim they want early-stage roles but are unwilling to do the actual work. This creates failure for both the marketer and the company. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #142)

- **Do not join a company without assessing product-market fit.** Marketing efforts will fail not due to marketer incompetence but due to lack of PMF. Most marketers can scale products with early PMF but cannot create demand for vague ideas. (Source: Udi Ledergor, Episode #237)

- **Do not network only with peers in your function.** Revenue professionals who only network with other marketers miss the higher-leverage relationships with founders and CEOs who are the actual decision-makers. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episodes #207 and #142)

- **Do not assume a raise will be offered.** Proactively negotiate compensation with specific numbers and clear timelines. If a company lacks formal processes for raises and promotions, treat that as a signal about company maturity. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #131)

- **Do not spend your first months in a new role on research and strategy frameworks without visible output.** This risks losing stakeholder confidence before you can execute your vision. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episodes #133 and #189)

- **Do not pursue a CMO role expecting to continue doing the hands-on work you're passionate about.** CMO roles require constant context-switching between strategic planning, team management, and occasional tactical execution. If your passion comes primarily from day-to-day execution, a CMO role may make you miserable. (Source: Mychelle Mollot, Episode #182)

- **Do not try to be a generalist agency or consultancy.** When launching an agency or consultancy, define a specific specialty and ideal client profile. Vague positioning leads to lower rates and being perceived as a generalist. (Source: Amanda Goetz, Episode #158; Talia Wolf, Episode #132)

- **Do not ignore the CMO failure archetypes.** Failing to diagnose and remediate your own failure patterns (e.g., blaming others, chaotic energy, staying in a narrow functional lane) will derail your career regardless of your technical marketing skills. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
