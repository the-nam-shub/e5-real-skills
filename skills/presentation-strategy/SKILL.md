# Presentation Strategy

## Overview
This skill covers how to build and deliver presentations that resonate — whether you're speaking at a conference, pitching to a board, or briefing executives. It addresses audience analysis, structural frameworks, content development, slide philosophy, and delivery mindset. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests; nothing has been added from general knowledge.

---

## Start With Your Audience, Not Your Slides

**Define your audience before touching any deck.**
When building a presentation, start with a blank outline and ask: Who is in the audience? What do they want to hear? Do not open an existing deck and start copying slides — this is "the road to hell." Reusing slides from a quarterly business review for a board meeting, or analyst briefing slides for a customer pitch, will produce the wrong presentation every time because each audience has different needs and concerns. Create audience-specific decks even if it takes more time. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

**Identify one specific real person you want to help before writing a single word.**
Before outlining a talk, identify one person you know personally who could benefit from your message. Use this person as your mental model throughout development — imagine explaining your ideas to them over coffee. This prevents generic, industry-standard advice and pulls authentic, high-value insights from your experience. When you write something that would genuinely help one real person, the broader audience will respond to it. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Use the single-person framework to ruthlessly narrow scope.**
When you have too many ideas (e.g., 17 points for a 35-minute slot), ask: which of these ideas would actually help that one person succeed? Focus on high-value, unique ideas that only you can deliver based on your experience, rather than trying to cover everything. This prevents talks from becoming generic surveys of a topic and ensures you have time to develop each point properly. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

## Structuring Your Talk: The Four-Part Framework

Use the following four-part structure for conference and thought leadership presentations. This ensures talks are persuasive, memorable, and emotionally resonant rather than generic. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

### 1. Tension
Begin by introducing a real problem or pain point your audience experiences — do not jump to your main points or credentials. Use storytelling, analogies, and specific details to make the tension tangible and emotionally resonant. Take time to help the audience feel the weight of the problem; do not rush through it. This creates engagement and makes the audience want to hear your solution. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

### 2. Trust
After establishing tension, share a brief, genuine story about a time you faced a similar challenge or made a mistake. The story should be somewhat embarrassing or show you in a vulnerable light — this is what builds trust and connection. Do not use this section to establish credentials or prove expertise; instead, show that you've walked in the audience's shoes. This makes the audience feel you understand them and are not lecturing from on high. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

### 3. Teaching
Deliver your main points in this section, which should comprise approximately 70% of your total talk length. Develop each main point using the Three Cs framework (see below). (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

### 4. Takeaway
Close with a story about a person other than yourself who followed the playbook you just taught and achieved success. This positions the audience member as the hero rather than you, and demonstrates that your ideas work for others — not just for you. It leaves the audience with a relatable example they can envision themselves in. Alternative closing options include a clear call to action or vision casting about the future, but the third-party success story is particularly effective for making ideas feel transferable. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

## Developing Each Main Point: The Three Cs Framework

For every main point in the Teaching section, structure it with three elements: (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

1. **Catchy one-liner** — A memorable, punchy statement that captures the core idea. This is the intellectual moment.
2. **Creative analogy** — An analogy pulled from everyday life that makes the concept click emotionally before you explain it tactically. This is the emotional moment.
3. **Concrete example** — A case study or real-world example that shows the idea in practice. This is the practical moment.

Spend balanced time on all three elements. Do not rush through the one-liner and analogy to get to the example — the emotional resonance of the analogy is what makes the example land.

## Executive and Board Presentations

**Answer the question directly before providing context.**
When a board member or senior executive asks you a question, answer it directly and concisely first — then offer caveats or nuance. Do not tell a story or provide a lengthy explanation before answering. For example, if asked "What is our churn rate?", answer "Twelve percent, measured on an ARR basis last quarter" before offering context like "There are other ways to measure it" or "It varies by cohort." Board members and executives have a series of questions lined up; storytelling burns their time. Answer the question, then leave a thread for follow-up if they want to dig deeper. Consider creating a friendly internal code word (e.g., "bus cost") to remind your team to answer questions without over-explaining. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

**Never show audience-inappropriate content.**
For example, never show a competitive positioning chart to a customer — that type of content is appropriate for analysts only. Audience-specific deck creation is especially critical for board presentations where time is limited and conciseness is required. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)

## Slide Philosophy and Delivery

**Practice your talk before building your deck.**
Start practicing using only your outline (the four-part structure with Three Cs for each point) before creating or heavily designing slides. Develop natural delivery and conversational pacing without being constrained by slide content. Once you're comfortable with the flow, build the deck as a visual accent — not a script. This prevents decks from becoming crutches that make you sound scripted or overly formal. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Use minimal slides to maximize audience connection.**
Reduce the number of slides and bullet points to keep audience attention on you rather than the screen. Every time a slide appears, the audience looks away from you. Use slides sparingly — only when a visual is essential to understanding a concept. If you have a clear outline and have practiced, you can deliver most of your talk without slides, speaking conversationally. This approach, drawn from pulpit speaking, creates stronger emotional connection and makes you appear more authoritative and confident. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Begin practicing early.**
Start practicing well in advance of the event, not just days before. Early practice helps you identify gaps in your outline, refine pacing, and build confidence. The more you practice, the more natural and conversational you'll sound, and the less you'll rely on slides or notes. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Get feedback on your main ideas as early as possible.**
Before finalizing your talk structure, share your core ideas and outline with trusted colleagues or mentors. Early feedback on the big ideas — your main points and how they connect — helps you course-correct before investing time in full development and practice. This also builds confidence that your talk addresses a real need. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

## Delivery Mindset

**Focus on helping the audience, not on performing.**
When you're on stage, redirect your focus from nervousness or performance anxiety to the question: How can I help this person succeed? What difference will this make in somebody's life? This mindset shift helps you stay grounded, speak authentically, and connect with the audience rather than getting caught in your head about delivery mechanics. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Prioritize authenticity over sounding expert.**
Draw from your real experience and what you've actually done, rather than trying to sound smart or include industry best practices you haven't personally tested. Audiences respond to authentic stories and genuine insights far more than to polished expertise. If you try to sound like an expert without grounding ideas in your own experience, you'll struggle to develop the Three Cs framework and will likely resort to generic content. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

**Use humor cautiously.**
If humor is not a natural part of your communication style, do not force it into your talk. Attempting humor that doesn't land creates awkwardness and can derail your delivery as you spiral thinking about the failed joke. It's better to be less funny than to try to be funny and miss. If humor comes naturally to you, use it; otherwise, focus on authenticity, vulnerability, and clear storytelling. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not open an existing deck and start copying slides** when building a new presentation. Starting from existing slides produces the wrong presentation for the wrong audience every time. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
- **Do not show audience-inappropriate content** — e.g., competitive positioning charts in customer-facing decks. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
- **Do not answer executive questions with a story or lengthy preamble.** Answer the question first, then provide context. (Source: Dave Kellogg, Episode #342)
- **Do not open a talk by stating your main points or credentials.** Open with tension — a real problem the audience experiences. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not use the Trust section to establish expertise.** Use it to show vulnerability and shared experience. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not close by making yourself the hero.** Close with a story of someone else who applied your ideas successfully. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not build your slide deck before practicing your talk.** Slides built before practice become scripts and crutches. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not try to cover too many points.** Use the single-person framework to narrow scope to only the ideas that genuinely help your target audience member. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not force humor** if it doesn't come naturally to you. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
- **Do not try to sound like an expert** by including best practices you haven't personally tested. Draw only from authentic experience. (Source: Vincent Pierri, Episode #272)
