# Marketing Team Structure

## Overview
This skill covers how to design, build, and evolve B2B marketing organizations — including org design principles, role definitions, reporting relationships, team size decisions, and hiring philosophy. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 55 episodes. Where guests disagree, those disagreements are surfaced explicitly rather than resolved artificially.

---

## Org Design Principles

### Start with strategy, not structure
Before building or rebuilding a marketing organization, define your business strategy, goals, growth rate, and target customer. The organization structure must follow from strategy, not precede it. If your strategy is thought leadership, staff deep content experts; if digital demand generation, build in-house capability; if PLG, organize differently; if in-person events, build strong field capability. There is no universal template. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

When stepping into a new marketing leadership role, resist the urge to immediately restructure. Spend 2–3 months consuming information about the organization, strategy, people, and processes before making structural decisions. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

### Keep structure simple and small
Design your marketing organization so the top-level structure is simple enough to fit on a napkin. A 200-person marketing organization can have just four direct reports to the CMO (product marketing head, growth head, corporate marketing head, ops head) plus a chief of staff and EA. Simplicity enables cross-functional coordination. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

Build the smallest organization necessary. Resist growth for growth's sake. Large organizations are slow and create restrictions. Use part-time specialists and outsourcing to fill gaps instead of adding headcount. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202) *(Note: team size is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

Resist adding organizational layers. Avoid creating single-report manager roles to accommodate career growth. Use span-of-control as the trigger for adding layers only when necessary. Each layer reduces visibility and camaraderie, especially in remote environments. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

### Design within financial guardrails
Understand your target customer acquisition cost (CAC) and payback period, then build your organization to fit within those financial constraints. Calculate your target CAC ratio, determine your go-to-market split (field-led vs. marketing-led), and ensure your organization model fits within these financial guardrails. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

### Continuously adapt
Regularly assess whether strategy has changed (M&A, pivots, go-to-market shifts), market conditions have shifted, or new marketing models have emerged (e.g., AI-based tools, new channels). When these changes occur, explicitly evaluate the implications for your organization and make adjustments. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

### Do not design around individuals
Resist the temptation to create special roles, titles, or reporting structures to accommodate a high-performing individual. This breaks the organization's design and creates problems when that person leaves. Maintain organizational integrity even if it means losing the individual. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

### Leverage the new CMO window
When starting as a new CMO, you have a limited window (typically the first 3–6 months) where stakeholders expect and accept dramatic change. Use this window strategically to make structural changes, reorganizations, and difficult decisions that would be harder to implement later. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #128)

## Team Size and Headcount Strategy

*(Note: this is a contested area — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### The lean-team argument
Maintain smaller internal W2 marketing teams (4–5 people rather than 50–100) to reduce fixed costs and increase the percentage of budget available for programs, external experts, and creative initiatives. Smaller teams move faster, avoid approval bureaucracy, and allow senior leaders to stay close to execution and customer feedback. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #281; Episode #211)

Most mature companies ($50M+ ARR) are over-staffed with internal marketing headcount. Reinvest headcount savings into external expert consultants for specific areas like positioning, messaging, and strategy — typically a 3–6 month engagement for $50–200K that delivers more value than a full-time hire. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #211)

Smaller teams with lower fixed headcount costs allow you to flex marketing spend and strategy up and down based on seasonal demand and market conditions. Large teams with high fixed costs force you to spend consistently regardless of demand. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #211)

### The budget-threshold argument
If your marketing budget is under $10M annually, expect your VP of Marketing or CMO to do hands-on execution work (webinars, campaigns, list building, etc.) rather than pure strategy. At $10M+ in budget, you can afford specialized roles (brand, field, demand gen, growth) and a senior leader to manage them. Below $10M, hire people willing to execute, not just strategize. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207; Episode #142)

### Explicit strategy for full-time vs. part-time vs. outsourced
For each capability, decide whether it should be full-time, part-time, or outsourced. Full-time: core to strategy, long-term need, affordable. Part-time: specialized skills you can't afford full-time or need short-term. Outsource: non-critical to core, difficult to source, or temporary. Maintain 10–50 interim roles at any time; use outsourcing for non-core work to avoid over-building. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

## Org Structure Models

*(Note: functional vs. pod-based structure is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### Functional specialization model
Organize marketing around functional jobs-to-be-done, not industry templates. One example structure that emerged from actual strategic needs at ContentStack: (1) Product, Solution & Brand Marketing (product marketing, competitive intelligence, analyst relations, pricing, creative); (2) Integrated Marketing (content, campaigns, customer marketing); (3) Earned Media (social, PR, influencer marketing); (4) Digital Operations (web, MOPS, analytics, prospecting operations); (5) Global Marketing (regional field alignment, partner marketing, events). (Source: Gurdeep Dhillon, Episode #280; Episode #203)

Clearly define which teams are service centers (enabling other teams) and which are outcome centers (directly responsible for revenue/pipeline outcomes). Service centers should be measured on turnaround time and efficiency; outcome centers on business results. Make this distinction explicit. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

Establish clear accountability and KPIs for each organizational unit. Cascade corporate goals down through the organization and assign each unit specific, measurable accountability. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

Organize marketing around three targeted focus teams (demand gen, marketing growth, product marketing) aligned to specific funnel stages or customer segments, plus two centers of excellence (brand studio, marketing ops) that flex across all teams. This prevents competing interests and ensures shared resources are allocated based on company-level priorities. (Source: Jason Lyman, Episode #263)

### Outcome-based pod model
Reorganize marketing teams around outcomes and customer problems rather than traditional job titles and functions. Create cross-functional pods focused on specific outcomes (e.g., "brand relationship building," "customer engagement and referrals," "product delight"). Each pod owns a measurable outcome and includes a mix of people and AI agent capabilities. Define clear KPIs for each pod's outcome. (Source: Sydney Sloan, Episode #289)

In AI-first companies, replace traditional functional silos with outcome-focused work streams (inbound, outbound, communications). Each work stream owns the full set of tactics needed to achieve its outcome, rather than each function owning a tactic across all outcomes. This enables faster shipping, clearer accountability, and better integration of AI tools. (Source: Kady Srinvivasan, Episode #276)

Organize marketing into cross-functional squads focused on specific motions (e.g., paid squad, product launch squad, SEO squad). Each squad includes all necessary functions (design, dev, content, etc.) and meets weekly. Use a shared project management board for each squad to track quarterly projects. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episode #283; Episode #199)

Organize marketing teams as small, autonomous pods that can move quickly and test ideas without excessive approval layers. When a small team discovers something that works, create a mechanism to share learnings across the organization and scale successful experiments. (Source: Emma Robinson, Episode #277)

### Strategy vs. pipeline split
Split marketing into two distinct functions with different skill sets, timelines, and measurement approaches. Strategy includes analyst relations, PR, comms, product positioning, competitive intelligence, and customer research — measured on long-term impact across the entire customer lifecycle (win rates, NRR, expansion, retention). Pipeline creation includes demand creation, lead qualification, and prospecting — measured on short-term ROI and conversion efficiency. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #281; Episode #211)

Separate marketing into two distinct teams: strategy team (product marketing, content, creative, thought leadership, social, PR) and pipeline team (demand gen, ABM, ops, paid digital, SDR, BDR). These teams need to work together but have fundamentally different objectives and skill sets. Combining them often creates friction. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #214)

### Matrix structure for B2B/B2C companies
Structure marketing teams around business units (B2B, B2C, International) with core channel centers of excellence that ladder into business outcomes. Treat B2B and B2C as fluid rather than siloed, with CMO coordinating across all units to ensure brand consistency while allowing each unit to optimize for its specific audience. (Source: Kristine Segrist, Episode #277)

## Reporting Relationships and Org Hierarchy

### Marketing should not report to a CRO
Marketing should not report to a Chief Revenue Officer. CROs are incentivized to hit short-term sales numbers and will prioritize rapid capital deployment over strategic brand building and long-term pipeline development. This removes the checks and balances that marketing provides. Instead, maintain separate reporting lines for Sales, Marketing, and Customer Success, all reporting to the CEO. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207; Episode #142)

### CMO span of control
Structure the marketing organization so the CMO has only 3 direct reports (e.g., VP of Integrated Marketing, Head of Demand Gen, and an individual contributor experimenter). This allows the CMO to spend approximately 50% of time on outward-facing activities — customer meetings, prospect meetings, speaking at conferences — while strong internal leaders manage day-to-day operations. (Source: Andrew Davies, Episode #195)

### CMO role evolution
The CMO role is shifting toward being a hands-on craftsperson and operator with a smaller team, rather than a pure people manager overseeing large departments. This is enabled by AI reducing the need for large execution teams. CMOs will be cross-functional practitioners who can actually do the work. (Source: Kieran Flanagan, Episode #318; Episode #257) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### Define the CMO role based on your strengths
Rather than attempting to be a well-rounded generalist CMO, identify your core strengths (e.g., strategy, product marketing, communications) and build your marketing organization and priorities around those strengths. Communicate clearly to the organization what the role of marketing will be at your company, since unlike CFO or other C-suite roles, the CMO mandate is not standardized. (Source: Kimberly Storin, Episode #229)

### Visionary-integrator pairing
Structure business leadership around the visionary-integrator pairing from the Traction/EOS framework. The visionary (founder) is creative, strategic, and full of ideas; the integrator brings structure, process, and operational clarity. Formalizing this separation through titles provides structure for future growth. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)

## BDR/SDR Reporting Structure

*(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### The case for marketing ownership of BDRs/SDRs
Move the BDR team from sales to marketing when: (1) pipeline is a key north star metric for marketing; (2) BDR activities are closely aligned with marketing campaigns (following up on webinar leads, nurturing marketing-generated prospects); (3) there's a gap in sales leadership structure. (Source: Kelly Cheng, Episode #297)

Consolidate all prospecting activities under a single marketing-led pipeline creation function. This eliminates the blame game between departments, provides clear visibility into blended prospecting efficiency, and allows the CMO to own and optimize the entire path from lead generation through qualified meeting. (Source: Chris Walker, Episode #281; Episode #211)

Place the SDR/ADR team under marketing's organizational structure to eliminate misalignment between marketing's account selection and sales' prospecting activities. Negotiate this structure early, ideally before hiring the first sales rep, to avoid legacy incentive conflicts. (Source: Trinity Nguyen, Episode #219)

Have outbound sales development (cold email, cold DM, outreach campaigns) report to the marketing leader because outbound is fundamentally another distribution channel for messaging, and the marketing leader controls messaging strategy, landing pages, ads, and content. (Source: Sara Lattanzio, Episode #268)

## Specific Role Definitions and Ownership

### Product marketing
Adapt product marketing responsibilities based on whether the company is sales-led, PLG, or hybrid, and based on company maturity. In sales-led companies, product marketing focuses heavily on sales enablement and training. In pure PLG companies, it focuses more on research, data analysis, and experimentation with product managers. Don't apply the same product marketing playbook from your previous company to your new situation. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

When a company has both a PLG motion and an enterprise sales motion, split the product marketing team into specialized roles: one group focused on working with product managers on research and experimentation for free/freemium users; a separate solutions marketing team focused on working with sales, customer success, and demand gen to support enterprise features and sales enablement. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

Establish a solutions marketing function with team members who specialize in specific ideal customer profiles (e.g., tech industry, financial services). Their responsibilities include creating sales assets (PDFs, pricing pages, battle cards), writing email copy for sales outreach cadences, and helping demand gen teams understand messaging for specific ICPs. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

Separate product marketing from campaign execution. Create a clear handoff: product marketing develops strategy; revenue marketing executes campaigns and drives pipeline. When product marketers are split between strategy and campaign execution, they lose focus on their core strength. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #128)

Assign demo building to product marketing as primary owner. Product marketers know the product in depth and understand how to craft compelling product stories. While demand gen distributes demos and sales uses them in the sales cycle, product marketing should own creation and strategy. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #176; Episode #122)

Combine brand and product marketing under a single senior director to ensure that messaging scales consistently from company mission and category positioning down to individual product features and launches. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

### Creative and design
*(Note: in-house vs. external creative is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

Organize creative (video, motion, design, copy, web) as a centralized in-house resource reporting to a single creative director and supporting all marketing functions, rather than embedding creative talent within individual teams. This ensures consistency and allows creative to be allocated flexibly across priorities. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

Keep creative design in-house rather than outsourcing to agencies to maintain brand consistency and reduce creative direction overhead. An in-house team understands the brand essence, company rhythms, and strategic direction deeply enough to execute with minimal direction. (Source: Mychelle Mollot, Episode #182)

Establish a workflow where brand/PMM defines the audience, key stories, and campaign strategy, then briefs the creative team on the desired outcome. The creative team proposes how to bring that strategy to life. This separates strategic thinking from execution. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

Use design-as-a-service agencies to maintain creative velocity and avoid bottlenecks. In-house resources often get bogged down in operational work. Maintain a list of external creative partners you can tap on-demand when you need creative assets quickly. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #153)

### Demand gen and growth
Merge self-serve and enterprise growth teams under a single growth leader to eliminate misaligned incentives and improve efficiency. This ensures high-quality leads entering through one funnel are properly nurtured through the other. (Source: Shane Murphy, Episode #173)

Structure demand gen and creative teams to collaborate on messaging iteration. Position demand gen as the team that operates distribution levers (ad spend, targeting, conversion rate optimization) but not as the sole owner of creative success. Create a feedback loop where demand gen identifies creative wear-out and escalates to product marketing and creative teams for new messaging. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

### Content
Build content team roles around channel mastery, not generalist execution. Identify one channel where someone can excel and let them master it. Once they've cracked the code on one channel, give them 10–20% of their time to experiment with new channels. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #200) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

Organize content teams using a "guild" structure where content managers are embedded within and report to functional teams (e.g., lifecycle marketing, SEO, enterprise sales) for day-to-day work and KPIs, but receive professional development and strategic alignment from central content leadership. (Source: Eliana Atia, Episode #166)

Position content leadership as a curator and managing editor who sources insights from existing assets (podcasts, community, guest experts) rather than as an original content creator. This scales better than relying on one person's ideas. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #169)

### Social media
Hire a social media person whose primary job is not to post what people ask them to post, but to be a gatekeeper and taste enforcer for all social content. This person partners with teams across the business to elevate content before it ships and maintains a high bar for quality and consistency. (Source: Ryan Narod, Episode #330)

### Customer reference and advocacy
Establish a customer reference marketing function (sometimes called customer marketing) focused on getting customer references, case studies, testimonials, and customer advocacy content. This role is distinct from lifecycle marketing and can be staffed by someone transitioning from customer success. This prevents customer reference work from being scattered across product marketing, demand gen, or CS without clear ownership. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

### Partner marketing
At mid-market stage, assign partner marketing responsibility to product marketing until the company is large enough for a dedicated function. Assign one person within product marketing to own partner marketing activities like co-marketing with integration partners, webinars, and lead sharing arrangements. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

Rather than creating a standalone partnerships department, embed partnership strategy into each marketing function's quarterly and annual planning. For each campaign, identify trusted voices and companies your customers already know, and attach those partners across social media, content marketing, demand gen, and events. (Source: Jared Fuller, Episode #174)

Partnerships should report into the CMO or, ideally, have a Chief Ecosystem Officer role at the C-suite level. Avoid creating partnerships as an independent business unit that interfaces separately with marketing, sales, and success. (Source: Jared Fuller, Episode #174)

### Events
Consolidate all event management — regardless of size, format, or source — under a single team within Global Marketing. This includes corporate events, partner events, and sponsored events. Treat events as a system designed to accomplish specific outcomes rather than as isolated tactical executions. (Source: Gurdeep Dhillon, Episode #280)

### ABM and field marketing
Set a maximum number of accounts each ABM marketer can own based on account size and type. For major/enterprise accounts, cap at 20 accounts per person; for mid-market, 40–50 accounts per rep supported; for prospect accounts, limit to 2–3 ABM accounts per sales rep (roughly 120–150 total per ABM marketer). (Source: Casey Patterson, Episode #331)

Organize field marketers into pods, each responsible for a consistent group of accounts. This creates accountability for immediate follow-up after in-person events. Field marketers should be tightly integrated with sales reps and expected to ensure follow-up happens the next day, not weeks later. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #331)

### Pricing
As soon as your company reaches a critical mass of revenue, establish a dedicated pricing function or hire a pricing person. Without this, pricing becomes reactive and inconsistent — a "mishmash" of different people's opinions. (Source: Karan Sood, Episode #216)

### PR and earned media
Consolidate PR, social media, influencer marketing, and employee advocacy under one PR leadership. This reflects the reality that PR and word-of-mouth are now primary channels for brand credibility and reach. (Source: Dave Steer, Episode #324)

### Data and analytics
Frame the relationship between data/analytics and marketing leadership as a pilot-and-instruments model. The marketing leader is the pilot making strategic decisions; the data team provides instruments (dashboards, tests, insights) to inform those decisions. The data team's job is not to make decisions but to give leaders the information they need to decide where to allocate budget. (Source: Drew Pinta, Episode #346)

Rather than centralizing all data science, allocate data scientists to specific business units (B2B, B2C, etc.) while maintaining a shared measurement and brand tracking team. This allows each unit to have dedicated analytical support while ensuring consistency in how metrics are defined and tracked. (Source: Kristine Segrist, Episode #277)

### Ops
Structure ops team work into three categories: Planning (budgeting, headcount planning, quota setting, comp design, strategic decisions before execution begins), Execution (day-to-day customer journey, pipeline management, prospecting), and Insights (bridging the two by analyzing what's working). Assign dedicated people to own planning work so it happens continuously, not just annually. (Source: Sean Lane, Episode #274; Episode #187)

Distinguish GTM Engineering from RevOps by focus: RevOps focuses on operating within existing systems; GTM Engineering focuses on building new systems and competitive advantages. Use this distinction when hiring — a RevOps posting attracts people focused on reporting and process management, while a GTM Engineering posting attracts builders. (Source: Cammy Keiler, Episode #271)

Establish a Growth Ops function (distinct from Revenue Ops) focused on orchestrating outbound campaigns, managing APIs and automations, centralizing AI tool management, and stitching together multiple data sources. This team owns the execution of programmatic outbound strategies rather than delegating complex tools to individual contributors. (Source: Kris Rudeegraap, Episode #159)

### Embedded subject matter experts
Hire a former member of your target buyer persona (e.g., a former CISO) as a direct report to the CMO and member of marketing leadership. Position them to participate authentically in target buyer communities as a peer, not as a vendor. Their role is to agitate for the problems the category solves and provoke conversation about solutions — without pitching the product. (Source: Melton Littlepage, Episode #223)

## AI's Impact on Team Structure

### AI-enabled role expansion
AI tools enable marketers to blur traditional role boundaries by handling design work (image generation, slide creation), engineering work (building interactive tools), and product work (creating calculators, dashboards) without waiting for specialized teams. This creates "hybrid operators" who can execute across multiple disciplines within a single day. (Source: Jess Lytle, Episode #319)

Reduce handoffs and waiting time by enabling individual team members to be fully self-sufficient in their roles. Use AI tools and pre-built templates to allow team members to handle tasks they would normally hand off. This shifts collaboration from dependency-based handoffs to optional partnership on brainstorming and ideation. (Source: Sylvia Lepoidevin, Episode #283; Episode #199)

### AI agent management
When deploying AI agents for sales and marketing functions, hire dedicated people to manage and coach those agents daily. These roles (e.g., "AI marketing operations" or "go-to-market engineer") are responsible for ensuring agents are trained, receiving feedback, targeting the right leads, and maintaining a high bar for output quality. Agents should not be set-and-forget. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)

### AI adoption infrastructure
Create a formal AI committee and designate AI champions in each department (marketing, sales, product, etc.). These champions lead discussions about how AI could improve workflows, test new tools, and share learnings across the organization. This distributes the responsibility for AI adoption beyond the CMO. (Source: Jennifer Delevante-Moulen, Episode #288)

Create a dedicated, full-time AI champion or program director role rather than assigning AI adoption as an additional responsibility on top of existing jobs. This person should have authority to set guidelines, train teams, evangelize use cases, and measure impact. This role sits at the intersection of marketing operations, content strategy, and organizational change management. (Source: Jessica Hreha, Episode #136)

## Hiring Philosophy

*(Note: specialist vs. generalist hiring is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

### Match the hire to the actual job
Don't hire a VP of Marketing based on title or past success alone. Identify the top 2–3 things your company needs marketing to accomplish right now, then hire someone whose passion and expertise align with those specific needs. A brand-focused marketer will fail at a company that needs demand generation, and vice versa. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207)

### Do the role before hiring for it
Before hiring someone for a new function, spend time doing that role yourself first — either solo or with a contractor on a part-time basis. This gives you clarity on what success looks like, prevents expensive hiring mistakes, and ensures the new hire inherits a role with clear processes and expectations. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #169)

### Hire senior peers for small teams
If you've been operating solo or in a very small team, prioritize hiring someone at or near your level of seniority who can operate independently and doesn't require extensive training or management. This preserves your ability to move fast without the overhead of coaching and one-on-ones. Look for people you already know and trust. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #205)

### Structure roles around business functions and goals
When building a marketing team, start by identifying the key functions and goals your business needs to accomplish, then hire people who can own those functions. Avoid defaulting to traditional org charts or industry benchmarks. Allow roles to evolve as the business grows rather than locking people into rigid titles. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #143)

### Multi-threaded marketers
Rather than hiring specialists in demand gen, content, or product marketing, hire and develop marketers who can think strategically about how multiple tactics work together and execute across these areas. A multi-threaded marketer understands how content, paid media, lifecycle marketing, and events interconnect. This approach is especially valuable in AI-first companies where you need fewer people doing more. (Source: Kady Srinvivasan, Episode #276) *(Note: this is contested — see Where Experts Disagree)*

## Where Experts Disagree

### 1. Should marketing teams be kept intentionally small and lean, or scaled with specialized roles?
**Support summary: 5 (lean) vs. 4 (scale with specialization)**

**Position A — Keep teams small and lean:**
Chris Walker (Episodes #281, #211) explicitly recommended maintaining smaller internal W2 marketing teams (4–5 people rather than 50–100) to reduce fixed costs and increase budget available for programs and external experts. He argued most mature companies ($50M+ ARR) are over-staffed and that smaller teams produce better work faster by avoiding meeting bloat and approval hierarchies. Peter Mahoney (Episode #202) cited OpenAI (~1,500 employees, ~$100B enterprise value), Berkshire Hathaway corporate (25–30 people), and Plana (max 2 marketing employees outpacing a 200-person team) as evidence that smaller organizations move faster, recommending part-time specialists and outsourcing over headcount. Kieran Flanagan (Episodes #318, #257) predicted the future CMO role will shift toward smaller teams and more hands-on craft work, enabled by AI reducing the need for large execution teams.

**Position B — Scale with specialized roles at the right budget:**
Jason Lemkin (Episodes #207, #142) argued that at $10M+ in marketing budget, you can and should afford specialized roles (brand, field, demand gen, growth) and a senior leader to manage them. Below $10M, the VP/CMO should do hands-on execution. This is a budget-based scaling model, not a preference for permanent smallness. Gurdeep Dhillon (Episodes #280, #203) described a large, multi-layered marketing org with five distinct functional groups and multiple sub-functions within each — implying a much larger team than Walker's 4–5 person model.

**Context dependency:** Jason Lemkin explicitly ties team size to budget ($10M threshold), which could partially reconcile with Walker's lean-team view if Walker is primarily addressing sub-$10M companies. However, Walker explicitly targets $50M+ ARR companies as over-staffed, which directly conflicts with Lemkin's and Dhillon's models at that scale. The disagreement is genuine for mid-to-large B2B companies.

**Trend note:** The lean-team / AI-enabled-small-team position is more prominent in 2025–2026 episodes (Walker ep. #281 Sep 2025, Flanagan ep. #318 Jan 2026, Flanagan ep. #257 Jun 2025), while the specialized-scaling model appears across both 2024 and 2025. This may reflect an emerging AI-driven shift toward smaller teams, but the sample is too small to be definitive.

### 2. Should BDR/SDR teams report to marketing rather than sales?
**Support summary: 5 (marketing ownership) vs. 2 (sales/separate ownership)**

**Position A — BDRs/SDRs should report to marketing:**
Chris Walker (Episodes #281, #211) argued that consolidating all prospecting under marketing eliminates split accountability and allows the CMO to own and optimize the entire path from lead generation through qualified meeting. Kelly Cheng (Episode #297) recommended moving BDRs to marketing when pipeline is a key north star metric and BDR activities closely mirror marketing campaigns. Trinity Nguyen (Episode #219) advocated placing the SDR/ADR team under marketing to eliminate misalignment between marketing's account selection and sales' prospecting activities. Sara Lattanzio (Episode #268) argued outbound is fundamentally another distribution channel for messaging, and the marketing leader controls messaging strategy.

**Position B — Maintain separate reporting lines; marketing should not report to a CRO:**
Jason Lemkin (Episodes #207, #142) explicitly argued marketing should not report to a CRO because CROs prioritize short-term sales numbers over strategic brand building and long-term pipeline development. He recommended separate reporting lines for Sales, Marketing, and Customer Success to the CEO. Note: Lemkin's objection is specifically about marketing reporting to a CRO, which is a slightly different structural question than marketing owning BDRs while reporting to the CEO — but the core tension about where prospecting belongs organizationally is a genuine disagreement.

**Context dependency:** Walker and Cheng specify conditions (pipeline as north star, campaigns tightly aligned with BDR activity) under which the move makes sense. The disagreement persists even accounting for context.

**Trend note:** The pro-marketing-ownership voices are clustered in 2025 (Walker ep. #211 Jan 2025, Lattanzio Jul 2025, Cheng Oct 2025, Walker ep. #281 Sep 2025, Nguyen Feb 2025), while Lemkin's objections span both 2024 and 2025. No clear directional shift.

**Position A — Keep creative in-house:**
Mychelle Mollot (Episode #182) argued for keeping creative design in-house to maintain brand consistency and reduce creative direction overhead. An in-house team understands the brand deeply enough to execute with minimal direction. Gerhardt's external-agency preference may reflect smaller team contexts where in-house creative gets overwhelmed. However, both positions are presented as general principles, not stage-specific advice, making this a genuine disagreement.

### 4. Should marketing teams be organized around functional specialties or outcome-based pods?
**Support summary: 6 (functional) vs. 4 (outcome/pods)**

**Position A — Organize by functional specialties:**
Gurdeep Dhillon (Episodes #280, #203) described organizing marketing around five functional groups with specialized sub-functions, explicitly framed as organizing around "functional jobs-to-be-done." Peter Mahoney (Episodes #202, #128) advocated for keeping top-level org structure simple with functional leaders (product marketing head, growth head, corporate marketing head, ops head) reporting to CMO, with clear service center vs. outcome center distinctions. Jason Lyman (Episode #263) described organizing around three targeted focus teams plus two centers of excellence — a functional specialization model. Dave Gerhardt (Episode #214) recommended splitting marketing into two functional teams (strategy and pipeline) with different focuses and working styles.

**Position B — Organize by outcomes or pods:**
Kady Srinvivasan (Episode #276) argued for replacing traditional functional silos with outcome-focused work streams (inbound, outbound, communications), where each stream owns the full set of tactics needed to achieve its outcome. Sydney Sloan (Episode #289) recommended reorganizing around outcomes and customer problems rather than traditional job titles, creating cross-functional pods with clear KPIs. Sylvia Lepoidevin (Episodes #283, #199) described organizing into cross-functional squads focused on specific motions, each including all necessary functions. Emma Robinson (Episode #277) advocated for small, autonomous pods that can move quickly and test ideas without excessive approval layers.

**Context dependency:** The outcome/pod model advocates tend to be speaking about AI-first or rapidly scaling companies, while the functional model advocates tend to be speaking about larger, more mature organizations. Both groups present their models as broadly applicable, and the disagreement is genuine for mid-market B2B companies deciding how to structure a 10–50 person marketing team.

**Trend note:** The outcome/pod model advocates are clustered in mid-to-late 2025 (Srinvivasan Aug 2025, Robinson Aug 2025, Lepoidevin Sep 2025, Sloan Oct 2025), while functional model advocates span 2024–2025. This may reflect an emerging shift toward pod-based structures, potentially driven by AI enabling smaller cross-functional teams.

### 5. Should you hire specialist marketers or multi-threaded generalists?
**Support summary: 3 (generalists) vs. 1 (specialists)**

**Position A — Hire channel specialists:**
Ross Simmonds (Episode #200) explicitly argued for building content team roles around channel mastery rather than generalist execution. Rather than having one person be mediocre across all channels, identify one channel where someone can excel and let them master it. Once they've cracked the code on one channel, give them 10–20% of their time to experiment with new channels.

**Position B — Hire multi-threaded generalists:**
Kady Srinvivasan (Episode #276) argued for hiring multi-threaded marketers who can execute across integrated tactics rather than siloed specialties, especially in AI-first companies where the ability to integrate tactics creates competitive advantage. Jess Lytle (Episode #319) described AI tools enabling marketers to blur traditional role boundaries by handling design, engineering, and product work — creating "hybrid operators" who execute across multiple disciplines. Sylvia Lepoidevin (Episodes #283, #199) recommended building self-sufficient team members who can handle tasks they would normally hand off, using AI tools and templates to enable end-to-end ownership of complete work streams.

**Context dependency:** Ross Simmonds is specifically discussing content team roles, while Srinvivasan and Lytle are discussing broader marketing team composition in AI-first contexts. However, the underlying tension — depth vs. breadth in individual marketer skill sets — is a genuine disagreement about hiring philosophy.

**Trend note:** The multi-threaded generalist position appears in more recent episodes (Srinvivasan Aug 2025, Lytle Jan 2026), while Simmonds' specialist argument is from Dec 2024. Too few data points to confirm a trend.

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not report marketing to a CRO.** CROs are incentivized to hit short-term sales numbers and will prioritize rapid capital deployment over strategic brand building and long-term pipeline development. This removes the checks and balances that marketing provides. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207; Episode #142)

- **Do not design organization structure around individual high performers.** Creating special roles or reporting structures to accommodate a high-performing individual breaks the organization's design and creates problems when that person leaves. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

- **Do not copy competitor org charts.** Avoid copying standard templates from other companies. Build structure around your actual strategic needs. (Source: Gurdeep Dhillon, Episode #280)

- **Do not apply the same product marketing playbook from your previous company.** Analyze what the company actually needs (what does product management need? what does sales need? what does marketing need?) and build your approach accordingly. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

- **Do not immediately restructure when taking over a new organization.** Spend 2–3 months listening and learning before making structural changes. Reactive changes made without context cause more harm than good. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

- **Do not create unnecessary management layers.** Avoid single-report manager roles created to accommodate career growth. Each layer reduces visibility and camaraderie. (Source: Peter Mahoney, Episode #202)

- **Do not hire for a role before doing it yourself.** Before hiring someone for a new function, spend time doing that role yourself first — either solo or with a contractor. This prevents expensive hiring mistakes. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #169)

- **Do not hire marketing leaders based on title or past success alone.** Match the person to the actual job, not the job title. A brand-focused marketer will fail at a company that needs demand generation. (Source: Jason Lemkin, Episode #207)

- **Do not let AI agents run without active management.** Agents should not be set-and-forget. They require dedicated people to manage and coach them daily to prevent quality degradation. (Source: Maura Rivera, Episode #301)

- **Do not scatter customer reference work across multiple teams without clear ownership.** Without a dedicated customer reference marketing function, case studies and testimonials fall through the cracks between product marketing, demand gen, and CS. (Source: Jeff Hardison, Episode #335)

- **Do not let demand gen bear all the stress of hitting targets while lacking the tools to solve the core problem.** Create a feedback loop where demand gen identifies creative wear-out and escalates to product marketing and creative teams for new messaging — rather than expecting demand gen to optimize their way out of a messaging problem. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

- **Do not assign AI adoption as an additional responsibility on top of existing jobs past the pilot phase.** This is not sustainable. Create a dedicated full-time role focused on AI strategy, adoption, and training. (Source: Jessica Hreha, Episode #136)

- **Do not create partnerships as an independent business unit.** This silos partnerships from marketing, sales, and success. Partnerships should report into the CMO or have C-suite visibility as a Chief Ecosystem Officer. (Source: Jared Fuller, Episode #174)
