# Team Offsite & Culture Building

## Overview
This skill covers how to structure in-person team offsites for remote marketing teams, with a focus on what makes them valuable and how to approach planning, budget, and time allocation. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests and reflect their direct experience.

## Offsite Structure & Time Allocation

When planning a team offsite, resist the instinct to fill the schedule with formal agenda items. The primary value of an in-person gathering is the relationship-building that happens in unstructured time — not the planning sessions.

**Prioritize unstructured time over structured programming.** Allocate only 1–2 hours per day to formal planning or business discussions. Dedicate the remaining time to meals, activities, and casual hangouts. This is where chemistry, rapport, and the nuanced understanding of colleagues that cannot be replicated over video calls actually develops. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)

**Invest in quality accommodations and experiences.** The environment you choose signals your company's culture and values to the team. Nice lodging and thoughtful activities communicate that the team is worth investing in. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)

**Budget realistically for a small team.** For a team of approximately 7 people, expect to spend $10,000–$15,000 covering travel, lodging, meals, and activities. Use this as a planning benchmark when scoping costs. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)

## What NOT To Do

- **Do not over-schedule the offsite with back-to-back agenda items.** An agenda-heavy offsite undermines the core purpose of the gathering. Formal planning time should be the minority of the schedule, not the majority. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)
- **Do not treat the offsite primarily as a planning retreat.** The relationship-building value — chemistry, nuance, rapport — is what justifies the investment. Planning can happen remotely; genuine human connection cannot. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #311)
