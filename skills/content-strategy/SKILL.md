# B2B Content Strategy

## Overview
This skill covers content strategy best practices for B2B marketers, including how to structure interactive demos, test and scale content ideas, use AI as an execution tool, distribute content across channels, and build brand authority over time. All practices are sourced exclusively from Exit Five podcast guests across 5 episodes. No general marketing knowledge has been added.

---

## Positioning Your Content Strategy

**Become the go-to resource in your niche, not a product promoter.**
Structure your entire content strategy around answering your target audience's most pressing questions — not around promoting your product. If you sell to HR professionals, your content should make you the authoritative resource for HR questions (payroll, W2 vs. W9, compliance, etc.). This builds brand affinity and expert positioning before a prospect ever needs to buy. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

**Specialize in an underserved category to build authority.**
Rather than remaining a generalist, identify an emerging or underserved topic area where existing content is mediocre and competition is limited. Systematically dominate that space by creating superior content, hosting events, writing books, and developing proprietary frameworks. This approach can move you from unknown to category leader within five years. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Build an audience before you launch.**
Establish credibility, an email list, and a social following in your target market before launching a new company or product. This audience becomes a distribution channel for your new venture, providing initial signups and validation without requiring paid acquisition. Note: the audience doesn't automatically convert to customers in a new category, but it provides a significant head start and reduces acquisition costs. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

## Finding and Validating Content-Market Fit

**Test small before scaling.**
Share smaller content ideas, concepts, and tidbits to test what resonates with your audience. When a piece generates strong engagement — reshares, reposts, likes, bookmarks — you've found content-market fit. Once identified, expand that piece into multiple formats: podcast episode, social clips, blog post, etc. Track which content drives leads and customers, and double down on topics that "ring the cash register." This prevents wasting resources on content that doesn't resonate. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Use Twitter/X for rapid message testing, then bring validated ideas to LinkedIn.**
Post frequently on Twitter (10+ times per day) to test which messaging ideas resonate. Each tweet lives independently and gets its own reach. When a tweet shows strong engagement, expand that idea into a longer-form LinkedIn post or thread. (Note: how aggressively to then use LinkedIn is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Study top-performing content on your target platform before creating.**
Before publishing on a new platform, research what's already working. Search for keywords relevant to your industry, sort by top posts or highest engagement, and analyze the format, length, structure, and messaging of high-performing content. Use this as a template for your own content rather than importing formats from other platforms. On TikTok, for example, search relevant hashtags, find videos with the most likes and comments, and recreate similar formats with your own angle. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

## Content Distribution and Repurposing

**Treat search as a primary distribution channel.**
Create content — blog posts, videos, Twitter threads, YouTube videos — optimized to appear on search engine results pages for keywords relevant to your industry and customer problems. When someone searches a query relevant to your space, your content should occupy multiple positions on the SERP. This builds both SERP domination (owning the phrase) and mindshare domination (becoming the go-to resource). (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Repurpose top-performing social posts on a monthly cadence.**
Identify your highest-performing social media posts each month using analytics tools (e.g., Shield app for LinkedIn, Taplio), then repost the exact same content or slightly refreshed versions back into your content queue. This leverages proven engagement without requiring new creative work. Example: a LinkedIn post about a lead magnet drove 700 subscribers on first posting; reposting it six months later drove 200 more subscribers in a single day. (Note: how frequently to repost on LinkedIn is contested — see Where Experts Disagree.) (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

**Refresh tone and language when reposting evergreen content.**
When republishing high-performing older content, audit the language and tone for shifts in audience sentiment or cultural context. Update terminology that may now be perceived negatively while preserving the core idea. Example: replace "hustle" with "work hard" if audience sentiment has shifted against hustle culture, keeping the message intact while avoiding potential backlash. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Recognize that nurturing happens across multiple channels, not just email.**
Nurturing is not limited to email sequences. A prospect who listens to your podcast for six months and then converts after seeing a LinkedIn post is being nurtured across multiple touchpoints. 95% of your audience is not ready to buy right now — your job is to stay top-of-mind across whatever channels they consume (podcast, LinkedIn, content, events) so that when they are ready, you're the first company they think of. Reframe nurturing from "how many emails should we send" to "how do we maintain presence across channels." (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #120)

## Community and Identity Building

**Build a shared identity across open channels rather than investing primarily in a walled platform.**
When building community around your brand, prioritize creating a shared identity and mindset that exists across multiple open channels — LinkedIn, podcasts, email, events, content — rather than investing primarily in a walled community platform (Slack group, Circle, Facebook group). A strong identity can be moved into a walled space later if needed, but a walled community without underlying identity is hard to scale and often dies. Examples: Drift built community through social, podcasts, and events; Intercom built community through content that became a resource for product managers and marketers. This approach requires sustained investment but creates a moat that is harder for competitors to replicate. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)

## Interactive Product Demos

**Keep interactive demos to 8–15 steps and approximately 30 seconds.**
Structure interactive product demos to be short — 8 to 15 steps taking approximately 30 seconds to complete. This length is sufficient to show major "aha moments" and key value without revealing your entire product or giving competitors a complete blueprint. Think of it as an appetizer, not the full meal. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

**Edit product data within demos to improve the product story.**
Use the demo editor to modify or clean up product data shown in the demo without needing a perfect demo environment. If your product's UI is messy or contains negative data, edit the demo to show only the beautiful end-state dashboards or change negative numbers to positive ones. This lets you tell a better product story even if the underlying product isn't visually perfect. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)

## Using AI in Content Strategy

**Use AI as an execution accelerator, not a content creator.**
Treat AI tools (ChatGPT, voice-to-text tools like Eleven Labs, etc.) as a force multiplier for execution speed, not as a replacement for human creativity or judgment. AI doesn't close the gap between great and mediocre marketers — it just makes both faster. Use AI for: translating content into other languages, converting voice notes to written posts, generating multiple prompt variations, editing and refining, or handling repetitive tasks. The human brings strategy, taste, and judgment; AI handles the mechanical work. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Use AI as a copilot for ideation and prompt generation.**
Feed AI basic context about your role, company, and upcoming work, then ask it to generate ideas or questions. Example: "I'm a former CMO and host of the Exit Five podcast. We share tactical advice for B2B marketers. I'm about to interview [guest name]. What questions would you ask them?" Use the output as a starting point for your own thinking — you likely won't use most of it, but it accelerates your ideation process and surfaces angles you might have missed. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121)

## Where Experts Disagree

### Should you post frequently on LinkedIn, or treat it as a low-frequency, high-polish channel?

**The disagreement (2 vs. 1):**

**Position A — LinkedIn penalizes frequent posting; treat it as a constrained, polished channel.**
Peep Laja explicitly states that posting more than once per day on LinkedIn kills reach. His recommended strategy: use Twitter/X for high-frequency testing (10+ posts per day), and only bring validated, polished ideas to LinkedIn at a controlled cadence. LinkedIn is a destination for proven content, not a testing ground. (Source: Peep Laja, Episode #119)

**Position B — LinkedIn rewards volume and repetition of proven content; repost aggressively.**
Dave Gerhardt and Ross Simmonds both advocate for treating LinkedIn as a channel where reposting top-performing content — including the exact same post months later — is a core repeatable tactic. Dave's example of driving 200 new subscribers from a repost frames LinkedIn as a channel where volume and repetition are rewarded, with no mention of frequency penalties. Ross similarly advocates for scaling and repurposing evergreen content across formats without flagging any LinkedIn frequency constraint. (Sources: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #121; Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)

**Context dependency:**
There is a partial distinction worth noting: Peep's advice is specifically about posting frequency for *new* content (10+ times/day on Twitter vs. once/day on LinkedIn), while Dave's advice is about reposting evergreen content *months apart*. These are not identical scenarios. However, their implied strategies conflict at the level of how aggressively to use LinkedIn as a publishing channel — Peep treats it as constrained and low-frequency, while Dave and Ross treat it as one where volume and repetition are rewarded.

**Trend note:** None provided.

**Why it matters:** If LinkedIn's algorithm genuinely penalizes frequent posting, marketers who repost content too often or publish multiple times per day will see diminishing returns. Getting this wrong means wasted content effort or suppressed organic reach on one of B2B's most important channels. When advising on LinkedIn posting cadence, present both positions and ask the user about their current posting frequency and goals before recommending a specific approach.

## What NOT To Do

- **Don't use interactive demos as a full product tour.** Showing too much reveals your entire product to competitors and overwhelms prospects. Keep demos to 8–15 steps as an appetizer. (Source: Natalie Marcotullio, Episode #122)
- **Don't use AI to replace human strategy, taste, or judgment.** AI accelerates execution but doesn't close the quality gap between great and mediocre marketers. Using AI as a substitute for strategic thinking produces mediocre output faster. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Don't scale content before validating it.** Investing in full content production (podcast episodes, long-form posts, video) before testing whether an idea resonates wastes resources. Test small first. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Don't apply formats from one platform to another without research.** What works on LinkedIn doesn't automatically work on TikTok or Twitter. Study top-performing content on each platform before creating. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
- **Don't build a walled community platform before establishing a shared identity.** A Slack group or Circle community without underlying brand identity is hard to scale and often dies. Build the identity first across open channels. (Source: Brian Kotlyar, Episode #118)
- **Don't treat nurturing as purely an email function.** Limiting nurture to email sequences ignores the majority of touchpoints where prospects are actually consuming your content. (Source: Dave Gerhardt, Episode #120)
- **Don't repost evergreen content without auditing the language.** Tone and terminology shift over time. Republishing content with outdated or culturally misaligned language can generate backlash even if the core idea is sound. (Source: Ross Simmonds, Episode #121)
