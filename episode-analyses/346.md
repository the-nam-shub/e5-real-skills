# Episode #346: Drew Pinta on Measurement, Creative Bets, and AI Agents at Ramp

Drew Pinta, Director of Growth Data at Ramp, covered three areas: measuring brand and creative campaigns, structuring channel investment and testing, and operationalizing AI agents across the marketing function. The AI material breaks meaningful new ground, with implementation specifics that go well beyond the general "use AI to work faster" framing common in earlier episodes.

## New practices extracted

- **AI agent for vertical go-to-market at scale.** Ramp built an end-to-end agent that replicates the workflow of a human specialist who built out a single vertical strategy. The agent conducts market research, scans sales call transcripts, generates SEO keyword lists, spins up landing pages, and produces sales enablement materials from a single trigger. The enabling move was decomposing the specialist's workflow into atomic Claude skills, each with defined inputs, outputs, and test cases (evals), then chaining them together. This is the most operationally specific AI-in-marketing practice the podcast has documented.

- **Guardrails framework for AI agents by risk level.** Pinta distinguishes low-risk agent actions (internal creative generation, research) from high-risk actions (sending emails, interacting with ad platforms). Low-risk actions run without review. High-risk actions route through an internal middleware layer that validates behavior before execution, illustrated by a custom layer on top of HubSpot that prevents lifecycle agents from sending duplicate or erroneous emails.

- **CEO mandate to deprioritize current work in favor of AI learning.** Ramp's CEO explicitly told the team it was acceptable for short-term metrics to slip while people invested time learning and building with AI tools. Pinta frames this as a prerequisite for genuine adoption: without it, team members face a rational incentive to protect their performance reviews rather than experiment.

- **Internal AI champion identification and activation.** Ramp built a simple dashboard tracking AI tool usage by team member, identified the heaviest users, and turned them into evangelists who recorded Loom walkthroughs, ran demos, and shared workflows with peers. Pinta argues this bottom-up approach outperforms top-down training because people respond more to seeing a peer succeed than to a leadership mandate.

- **Event study methodology for measuring brand stunts.** Rather than attributing a brand activation through MTA or last-touch models, Ramp forecasts baseline marketing performance, runs the stunt, then compares actuals to the forecast. The delta is treated as the causal impact. Pinta applied this to Ramp's glass box installation in Madison Square Park.

- **Measurement should meet the marketing, not vice versa.** Forcing creative campaigns into direct-response measurement frameworks is, in Pinta's framing, the fastest way to kill creative work. The measurement approach should be selected based on channel type: event studies for brand stunts, surveys for podcast sponsorships, Gong call analysis for awareness channels, geo lift tests for out-of-home.

- **AI-powered Gong call scanning for attribution validation.** Ramp pipes sales call transcripts through an LLM to identify how customers say they heard about the company, then compares those mentions against their MTA model. The exercise revealed LinkedIn was mentioned three times more than Meta in customer conversations, directly contradicting their MTA model, which showed the inverse. This prompted incremental testing to resolve the discrepancy.

- **Channel maturity framework with progression gates.** Channels move through defined stages: early stage requires solid optimization events and baseline reporting; mid-stage runs in-platform lift tests (cheaper, less rigorous); mature stage requires external incremental tests comparable across channels. Channels must meet specific criteria to advance, preventing premature scaling.

- **70/30 budget split between proven and experimental channels.** Seventy percent of budget goes to mature channels; thirty percent funds experimental bets, tracked separately from core channel LTC and not subject to the same performance scrutiny. Pinta notes that finance alignment on this separation is a prerequisite, otherwise experimental underperformance drags down overall reported efficiency.

- **MTA models scoped to within-channel decisions only.** Pinta uses MTA for goal-setting and campaign-level analysis within a channel but explicitly does not use it for cross-channel budget allocation. Cross-channel decisions rely on incremental testing and customer feedback signals. Privacy regulations are cited as an additional reason MTA models are unreliable for allocation.

## Where this creates tension

Pinta's bear case on AI automation reinforces an existing disagreement with Liz (episode #345, April 9, 2026). The count is now 1 vs. 1.

Liz argued that AI creative tools should be framed as enablers that free up creative professionals for higher-value work, and did not anticipate significant job displacement. Pinta holds that approximately 80% of current marketing work will be automated away and that marketers must proactively reinvent their roles or face disruption.

The positions are partially context-dependent: Liz was speaking specifically about creative roles, while Pinta's claim applies broadly across marketing functions including operational and campaign work. But both are addressing the same underlying question of whether AI displaces or augments marketers, and their expected degree of displacement genuinely conflicts. The disagreement is worth tracking as more guests address it directly.
