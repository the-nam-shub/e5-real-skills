# Episode #336: Multiple Guests on Creating High-Quality Content with AI

This episode brought together three practitioners (Connor Beaulieu of LegalZoom, Adina Timar of Weflow, and Eoin Clancy of AirOps) to address a specific operational problem: how to use AI in content production without producing generic output. The episode contributed meaningfully to the body of knowledge on AI-era SEO and content operations, with several practices specific enough to be immediately actionable.

## New practices extracted

- **Automate the operations around expert quotes, not the quotes themselves.** LegalZoom's "Project Penny" workflow takes article URLs, uses an LLM to identify five quote opportunities per article, frames strategic questions with context, matches questions to relevant internal experts via a knowledge base, and routes them to those experts through Slack using AirOps. The expert answers are then reinserted into the content. The key distinction is that the workflow handles logistics at scale (potentially across a thousand articles simultaneously) while the actual insight still comes from a human expert.

- **Treat content creation as assembly, not generation.** Timar's framing is that AI should be used to assemble granular pieces of intelligence from multiple sources (sales call transcripts, competitor analysis, product documentation, G2 reviews) rather than to generate full pages from a prompt. The workflow involves extracting each piece of intelligence separately, mapping it to specific content sections via structured JSON, and writing one section at a time with focused context. This prevents the generic output that results from asking AI to produce a complete article in one pass.

- **Rebuild competitor comparison pages with depth and acknowledged trade-offs.** Rather than surface-level feature comparisons that favor your own product, Timar's approach involves rebuilding these pages from scratch using multiple intelligence sources and explicitly including cases where competitors win. The rationale is that LLMs evaluate trustworthiness similarly to humans: pages that read as marketing spin are less likely to be cited in AI search results than pages that demonstrate genuine analytical depth.

- **Build brand kits from structured intelligence sources, not from scratch.** Timar's process pulls from sales transcripts, product documentation, and internal knowledge bases, formats everything in markdown for LLM readability, and validates accuracy against source material. The output is a granular brand kit that AI systems can apply consistently, rather than a high-level brand document that produces vague output.

- **Prioritize frontier knowledge as the primary lever for AI search citation.** Clancy's argument is that content LLMs cannot already answer is the most valuable category to produce. Examples include proprietary transaction data (Ramp's credit card data surfaced on their pages) or internal expert insights that have not been published elsewhere. This replaces the older playbook of scraping and repackaging competitor content.

- **Meet a higher structural bar for AI search citations.** Clancy cited research showing that the gap between what it takes to rank in traditional Google search and what it takes to be cited in AI search is growing. The specific requirements: more data points, more expert quotes, more internal links, and richer schema markup. Content refreshed quarterly receives approximately three times the citations of static content.

- **Use Search Console and prompt analysis to identify AI search gaps.** Timar's team filters Search Console data for long-tail queries that resemble AI prompts rather than traditional search queries, then builds a library of potential prompts people might ask LLMs about their topic. They analyze which prompts do not mention their brand and which mention it in the wrong context, using this to prioritize which pages to refresh or create.

- **Validate AI output against subject matter expert knowledge before publishing.** Beaulieu's example: when LegalZoom first ran the Project Penny workflow, the LLM suggested that business owners should choose flashy, memorable names. A real business attorney corrected this, explaining that the base business name should be deliberately generic for filing purposes, with a DBA used for the public-facing brand. This validation step is positioned as non-negotiable in trust-sensitive verticals.

- **Treat third-party brand mentions as a primary AI search visibility lever.** Clancy cited data that 85% of brand mentions in AI search come from third-party content. This means investing in content quality that earns organic pickup from other sites, combined with outreach and backlink tactics, is more important for AI search visibility than on-site optimization alone.

- **Redefine "incremental" as new value, not small improvements.** Beaulieu's framing is that as AI systems fulfill more informational demand directly, the question for content teams becomes what they can offer that goes beyond what the engines already provide. This requires internal conversations about proprietary assets: subject matter experts, unique data, original research, or first-hand product experience.
